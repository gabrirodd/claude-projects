## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper is explicit about its prolegomenatory character — it does not execute the framework it proposes but establishes the conditions for it to be built. The opening diagnoses a gap: translation studies has extensive literature on technological and business dimensions of machine translation but almost none on individual, sociocultural, and ecological consequences. A genealogical reconstruction of three philosophical traditions (critical theory, posthumanism, Anthropocene) follows, justifying their relevance to translation. The framework proposal — a broadly conceived consequentialist ethics for translation studies — is the paper's main contribution, but it is stated programmatically, not executed. This gives the paper a Type 1 structure (research-agenda closure, collective orientation, incompleteness constitutive) with a Type 2b kernel (canonical thinkers reconstructed for normative application).

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

Translation studies has generated extensive literature on the technological and business dimensions of machine translation and AI-assisted communication, but comparatively little on the *individual, sociocultural, and ecological consequences* of these technologies. What conceptual and ethical resources does the field need to address these consequences — and how should they be integrated into a field historically shaped by neoliberal logics of efficiency, scalability, and commodification?

Urgency is indexed to ChatGPT's launch (November 2022) as a crystallising moment: a "revolutionary leap" that has steadily reshaped human perception, communication dynamics, and the social fabric, yet has arrived without adequate conceptual frameworks in translation studies to interpret it.

---

## 4. Core argument

Translation studies needs a *broadly conceived consequentialist ethics* for investigating the sociotechnical implications of its technologies. "Consequentialist" here does not mean narrowly utilitarian calculation but a broad ethical orientation that takes seriously the downstream individual, sociocultural, and ecological consequences of technological choices — consequences currently invisible within the field's dominant political economy, governed by neoliberal dogma and unequal power dynamics.

Three philosophical-conceptual resources are proposed as building blocks: (1) **critical theory** (Frankfurt School — ideology critique, negative dialectics, attention to how technological rationality suppresses human capacities); (2) **posthumanism** (dissolution of the human/machine boundary in translation practice, and the need for new accounts of agency, authorship, and responsibility); (3) **the Anthropocene** (translation technologies as embedded in broader ecological systems — carbon costs of AI infrastructure, linguistic biodiversity as an environmental concept).

The key claim is that these resources must be *connected* to the specific political economy of translation, not imported wholesale from other disciplines.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Programmatic-normative: the paper surveys existing philosophical paradigms and connects them to the specificities of translation studies as both discipline and professional field. The argumentative move is agenda-setting: establishing *why* translation studies needs a critical, consequentialist ethics; *what* resources are available; and *how* they should be connected through the political economy of translation. The "Prolegomena" framing is deliberate and epistemically honest — the paper prepares ground for future work rather than executing it.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Maschinisierung** (machination/mechanisation): the increasing subordination of human translation practice to machine logics (pace, format, post-editing, quality assessment)
- **Sociotechnical translation studies** (emerging field the paper names and positions itself within)
- **Consequentialist ethics** (applied to technology): ethical evaluation of technologies by their downstream consequences across individual, sociocultural, and ecological dimensions
- **Critical theory** (Frankfurt School): particularly the critique of instrumental rationality and the suppression of critical reflection by technological systems
- **Posthumanism**: the displacement of the human as centred, sovereign agent of translation — who or what translates, and what accountability remains
- **Anthropocene**: the geological epoch of human-induced planetary transformation; applied to AI infrastructure's material and ecological footprint
- **Political economy of translation**: the structural conditions (labour markets, corporate ownership, regulatory regimes) within which translation technologies are developed and deployed

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Explicitly and self-consciously open — "prolegomena" by design. The normative claim (translation studies needs a critical, consequentialist ethics) is advanced with confidence. The three-part toolkit (critical theory + posthumanism + Anthropocene) is well-motivated but not synthesised: the paper establishes relevance without showing how the three interact or resolve their tensions (e.g., critical theory's humanist residues vs. posthumanism's anti-humanism). The conclusion is a research agenda, not a resolved position.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

The paper is explicit about its preliminary character. No empirical data. Engagement with posthumanism and the Anthropocene is broad rather than fine-grained. The paper does not address existing translation ethics literature (Chesterman's norm theory, Pym's ethics of translation), creating a gap between the "prolegomena" and the existing debates it seeks to transform. The consequentialist ethics proposed is not specified enough to adjudicate between competing technological choices.

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Connects to **Amaral et al. (2025)** and **Jaros (2019)** in its diagnosis of neoliberal political economy as the structural condition to be challenged, and in its programmatic-normative register.
- The posthumanism discussion links to **Danter et al. (2016)** and **Niu (2008)** — all three contest the posthuman as a site of critical revision.
- The Frankfurt School / critical theory lineage is largely absent elsewhere in the corpus, which is predominantly Foucauldian; Baumgarten introduces negative dialectics and ideology critique as a distinct theoretical lineage.
- The Anthropocene dimension is unique in the corpus: no other paper addresses the ecological/material footprint of AI infrastructure — a significant gap given the environmental costs of these systems.
