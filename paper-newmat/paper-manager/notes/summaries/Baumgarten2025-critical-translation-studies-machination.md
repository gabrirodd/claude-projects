## 1. Paper type detected: Theoretical / normative

This is a programmatic intervention in translation studies — a "prolegomena" (explicit in the title: preliminary groundwork) rather than a completed analysis. Its primary contribution is the proposal of a new ethical-critical framework for translation studies that can address the sociotechnical implications of machine translation and AI communication technologies. No original empirical data; the method is philosophical-synthetic.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

Translation studies has generated extensive literature on the technological and business dimensions of machine translation and AI-assisted communication, but comparatively little on the *individual, sociocultural, and ecological consequences* of these technologies. What conceptual and ethical resources does the field need to address these consequences — and how should they be integrated into a field that has historically been shaped by neoliberal logics of efficiency, scalability, and commodification?

The urgency of the question is indexed to ChatGPT's launch in November 2022 as a crystallising moment: the paper reads this as a "revolutionary leap" that has steadily reshaped human perception, communication dynamics, and the social fabric, yet has arrived without adequate conceptual frameworks in translation studies to interpret it.

## 3. Core argument

Translation studies needs a *broadly conceived consequentialist ethics* for investigating the sociotechnical implications of its technologies. "Consequentialist" here does not mean a narrowly utilitarian calculation of outcomes, but a broad ethical orientation that takes seriously the downstream individual, sociocultural, and ecological consequences of technological choices — consequences currently invisible within the field's dominant political economy, which remains governed by neoliberal dogma and highly unequal power dynamics.

Three philosophical-conceptual resources are proposed as building blocks: (1) **critical theory** (Frankfurt School lineage — ideology critique, negative dialectics, attention to how technological rationality can suppress human capacities); (2) **posthumanism** (the dissolution of the human/machine boundary in translation practice, and the need for new accounts of agency, authorship, and responsibility); (3) **the Anthropocene** (translation technologies as embedded in broader ecological systems and resource flows — carbon costs of AI infrastructure, linguistic biodiversity as an environmental concept).

The paper's key claim is that these resources must be *connected* to the specific political economy of translation — not imported wholesale from other disciplines — in order to generate a genuinely critical translation studies adequate to the current conjuncture.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Programmatic-normative: the paper surveys existing philosophical paradigms and connects them to the specificities of translation studies as a discipline and as a professional field. The argumentative move is agenda-setting: establishing *why* translation studies needs a critical, consequentialist ethics (the gap between technological change and conceptual apparatus), *what* resources are available (critical theory, posthumanism, Anthropocene), and *how* they should be connected (through the political economy of translation). The paper does not execute the framework it proposes — it prepares the ground for future work. The "Prolegomena" framing is deliberate and epistemically honest.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Maschinisierung** (machination/mechanisation): the increasing subordination of human translation practice to machine logics — pace, format, post-editing, quality assessment
- **Sociotechnical translation studies** (emerging field the paper names and positions itself within)
- **Consequentialist ethics** (as applied to technology): ethical evaluation of technologies by their downstream consequences across individual, sociocultural, and ecological dimensions
- **Critical theory** (Frankfurt School): particularly the critique of instrumental rationality and the way technological systems suppress critical reflection
- **Posthumanism**: the displacement of the human as centred, sovereign agent of translation — the question of who (or what) translates, and what accountability remains
- **Anthropocene**: the geological epoch of human-induced planetary transformation; here applied to AI infrastructure's material footprint
- **Political economy of translation**: the structural conditions (labour markets, corporate ownership, regulatory regimes) within which translation technologies are developed and deployed

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Explicitly and self-consciously open — the paper frames itself as preliminary by design. The normative claim (translation studies needs a critical, consequentialist ethics) is advanced with confidence and the three-part conceptual toolkit (critical theory + posthumanism + Anthropocene) is well-motivated. However, no synthesis between the three resources is offered: the paper establishes their relevance but does not show how they interact or resolve their tensions (e.g., critical theory's humanist residues vs. posthumanism's anti-humanism). The conclusion is a research agenda, not a resolved position.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

The paper is itself explicit about its preliminary character. No empirical data are presented. The engagement with posthumanism and the Anthropocene is broad rather than fine-grained — neither is fully theorised in relation to translation specifically. The paper does not address existing work in translation ethics (e.g., Chesterman's norm theory, Pym's ethics of translation), which creates a gap between the "prolegomena" and the existing ethical debates in the field it seeks to transform. The consequentialist ethics proposed is not specified enough to adjudicate between competing technological choices.

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Connects most directly to **Amaral et al. (2025)** and **Jaros (2019)** in its diagnosis of neoliberal political economy as the structural condition that must be challenged, and in its programmatic-normative register.
- The posthumanism discussion links to **Danter et al. (2016)** and **Niu (2008)** — all three engage the posthuman as a site of critical contestation, though Baumgarten does not engage the literary/cultural dimension.
- The critical theory framework (Frankfurt School) is largely absent from the rest of the corpus, which is predominantly Foucauldian. Baumgarten's inclusion of negative dialectics and ideology critique represents a distinct theoretical lineage that could productively challenge the corpus's dominant framework.
- The Anthropocene dimension is unique in the corpus: no other paper addresses the ecological/material footprint of digital infrastructure, which represents a significant gap given the environmental costs of AI systems.
