# Pavlov et al. (n.d.) — From Bitcoin to Ethereum: Ethics and Antifragility of Decentralization

**Zotero key:** HJLRA2L5

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (conceptual paper with qualitative illustrative cases)

Self-identified as a "conceptual paper." The primary contribution is a new theoretical framework ("adaptive decentralized power") synthesising Foucault and Taleb for blockchain systems. Three historical blockchain incidents are used as qualitative case evidence to demonstrate the framework's explanatory value, not as primary research objects.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

How does the transition from Bitcoin to Ethereum transform concepts of power, resilience, and ethics in decentralised digital systems? Bitcoin established the paradigm of decentralised trustlessness; Ethereum extends it with programmability (smart contracts, DAOs). What new power configurations does this extension produce, and how can existing theory (Foucault, Taleb) illuminate them?

## 3. Core argument

The Bitcoin-to-Ethereum transition instantiates a new governance paradigm the authors call *adaptive decentralized power*, characterised by three interlocking properties:

1. **Codified biopower**: Algorithmic consensus mechanisms replace institutional authority as the site of governance. Foucault's biopower — the management of populations through normalising knowledge — is here enacted not by institutions but by protocol code. The network disciplines participants through transparent, automatic enforcement of rules embedded in smart contracts.

2. **Economically incentivised antifragility**: Ethereum's Proof of Stake mechanism operationalises Taleb's "Skin in the Game" as a protocol-level design principle. Validators stake ETH as collateral; poor or malicious behaviour results in "slashing" (loss of stake). This means the system structurally rewards agents who are invested in the network's health and punishes those who undermine it — aligning individual incentives with systemic resilience. Crises (like the DAO hack) are absorbed and the system emerges stronger, which is the definition of antifragility.

3. **Panoptic consensus**: Blockchain's immutable public ledger functions as a form of distributed Foucauldian surveillance. Every transaction is permanently visible to all participants. Unlike the Panopticon, however, surveillance here is horizontal (peer-to-peer) rather than vertical (authority watching subjects), and it disciplines through transparency rather than visibility asymmetry. The paper argues this constitutes a novel form of surveillance that simultaneously enables and constrains autonomy.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Three-stage conceptual methodology: (1) theoretical synthesis — adapting Foucault's biopower/panopticism and Taleb's antifragility/Skin in the Game to the blockchain context; (2) qualitative case selection — three incidents chosen for their significance in demonstrating the framework: the DAO hack (2016, stress-testing decentralised governance), the Parity Wallet vulnerability (2017, smart contract risk and community response), and the PoS transition (2022, "The Merge"); (3) interpretive synthesis across the three cases. No original data collection; evidence is secondary (documentation, reports, existing analyses).

## 5. Key concepts

- **Biopower** (Foucault): the management of populations through normalising knowledge and institutional power — here recast as algorithmic consensus
- **Panopticism** (Foucault): surveillance that disciplines through the possibility of being watched — here recast as distributed blockchain transparency
- **Antifragility** (Taleb): systems that gain from disorder and stress, as opposed to merely being robust (resistant to stress) or fragile (damaged by it)
- **Skin in the Game** (Taleb): the ethical and practical principle that decision-makers must bear the consequences of their choices — operationalised in PoS staking
- **Adaptive decentralised power** (proposed): the synthesis concept combining the three properties described above
- **Lex cryptographia**: the idea that cryptographic code functions as a form of law — self-executing, borderless, and politically neutral (or pseudo-neutral)
- **DAOs** (Decentralised Autonomous Organisations): blockchain-native governance structures whose rules are encoded in smart contracts

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately strong as a framework proposal. The synthesis of Foucault and Taleb is novel and the three-part concept of "adaptive decentralised power" is coherent. The conclusion is explicitly research-agenda in character: the framework is proposed as generalisable beyond blockchain to distributed science communities and the sharing economy, and the authors call for future quantitative assessment of governance mechanisms and cross-platform comparison. The paper does not overclaim — it does not assert that Ethereum has solved the problems of power and ethics it identifies, but rather that it instantiates a new configuration that requires these concepts to understand.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

- Exclusive focus on Ethereum limits generalisability; the framework may not apply straightforwardly to other blockchain ecosystems (Bitcoin, Solana, etc.)
- The qualitative-conceptual approach cannot substitute quantitative validation of the antifragility and governance claims
- Real-world plutocratic tendencies undercut the "Skin in the Game" ideal: the top 5 ETH staking providers control approximately 60% of staked ETH, concentrating governance power despite the protocol's egalitarian design
- The 32 ETH staking barrier (approx. $50–100K at recent valuations) creates a significant entry threshold that the paper flags as a fairness problem but does not resolve
- The paper does not address the environmental critique of PoW (pre-Merge) or fully engage with the political economy of Ethereum's developer community

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Shares the Foucauldian power framework with **Young et al. (2025)**, **Hu (n.d.)**, **Jaros (2019)**, and **Amaral et al. (2025)**, but applies it to a technical-protocol context none of the others enter.
- The antifragility lens via Taleb is unique in the corpus and provides a useful complement to the predominantly Foucauldian toolkit.
- Connects to **Rieder & Simon (2015)** on the question of algorithmic accountability — Pavlov et al. argue Ethereum's transparency partially addresses the opacity problem Rieder & Simon diagnose in corporate algorithms, though the plutocratic concentration complicates this.
- The DAO hack case resonates with **Hu (n.d.)**'s analysis of how blockchain crises are absorbed and redeployed within governance regimes.
