      structured by fundamental rules that have isolated themselves from challenges by
      constituting modes ofinteraction inwhich suchchallenges are notonly illegitimate but
      often structurally impossible in the absence of organized political resistance.
        This model has the following advantages over the ‘false consciousness’ model of
      ideology:First,itdoesnotentailthatmembersofsubordinatedgroupshaveoverallfalse
      beliefs about their situation (or false second-order beliefs), as it operates at the level of
      concepts, not beliefs. It therefore avoids the charge of authoritarianism. Second, it
      provides a plausible account of the mechanisms of ideology. It must only assume that
      peoplewillcontinuetoadoptcertainconceptualresourcesthatarealreadywidelyshared
      in their social environment so long as those conceptual resources allow them to make
      sense of and practically come to terms with their situation. This explanation neither
      impliestheconsciousmanipulationofpeople’sbeliefsbyadominantgroupnorrelieson
      theideathatideologiesexploitcognitiveweaknessesorareexplainedbysociallyinduced
      irrationality.Third,itoffersaplausiblealternativetothefunctionalistthesis,butonethat
      stillexplainswhyoppressivesocialstructurestypicallypromoteunderstandingsthattend
      to increase their stability. This is because ideological social practices are governed by
      rules that make certain kinds of challenges inappropriate.
        Compared with the explanatory program endorsed by theories of hermeneutical in-
      justice, this model aligns with the idea that the main epistemic constraint faced by
      membersofsubordinatedgroupshastodowiththefactthatsociallysharedhermeneutical
      resources are unhelpful for making sense of their situation. However, what this model
      takes to be the central case of such a constraint is not the existence of a hermeneutical
      ‘gap’ that leads to agents’ genuine confusion or that results from the exclusion of
      membersofsubordinatedgroups,asindividuals,fromtheconstructionofhermeneutical
      resources. Rather, it assumes that the epistemic constraint in question results from the
      need to make sense of social practices that are effectively structured by the conceptual
      resources of others.

      Asanalternativetothe‘falseconsciousness’modelofideology,Ihavedevelopedamodel
      ofideologyasasociallycauseddistortionofconceptualresources.Thismodelcanmake
      senseofthebasicpremisesofideologytheorieswithoutadoptingtheundesirablefeatures

      ofthe‘falseconsciousnessmodel’.Ittakesupfromtheoriesofhermeneuticalinjusticethe
      idea that the epistemic constraints that oppressed groups face are often a matter of
      distortedconceptualresources.IncontrasttoFricker’stheory,however,itoffersawider
      and more plausible model of social power that takes epistemic constraints seriously
      withoutlocatingtheminthedefectsofmembersofoppressedgroups.Insteadofthinking
      of hermeneutical injustice exclusively in terms of exclusion, it offers a materialist ex-

      locates the source of ideological distortions in social power and recognizesthe ways in
