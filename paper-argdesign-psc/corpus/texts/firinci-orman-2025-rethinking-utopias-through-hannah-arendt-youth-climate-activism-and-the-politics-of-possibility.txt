        Recent scholarship has examined the role of utopian imagination in youth climate
      movements, drawing from Ernst Bloch’s (1995) vision of concrete utopian thinking.
      This exploration highlights the dynamics of youth activism (Arredondo-Trapero and
      Ga´ndara-Tovar 2021; Eskelinen and Lakkala 2024; Friberg 2022; McKnight 2024).
      These studies reveal that youth climate movements resist static or definitive conclu-
      sions, embodying instead an open-ended, relational quality that invites new possi-
      bilitiesandenvisionsalternativefutures(Friberg2022).Thefutureisimaginedthrough
      collective actions in various forms, as young people enact their environmental
      identities – be it through protest and resistance, free choices and participation, or
      everydaylifeperformancesandgreenlifestyles(seeFirinciOrman2022,2023).Inthis
      respect, Yates et al. (2024) highlight the role of prefiguration in utopian studies, il-

      resonating with Bloch’s concept of concrete utopias – utopias grounded in present

      Natality: New beginnings and future in the youth

      Arendt defines natality as ‘the fact that human beings appear in the world by virtue of
      birth’ (1965, 211). For Arendt, as discussed by So¨derba¨ck (2018), natality is key to
      understanding human life and action, emphasizing our capacity for new beginnings. In
      TheHumanCondition,sheassertsthatnatalityunderliesallaspectsofvitaactiva–labor,
      work,andespeciallyaction(Arendt1958a,9).BycontrastingtheGreekviewofhumanity
      as ‘mortal’ with a modern perspective that identifies humans as ‘natals’, capable of

        A significant debate in utopian theory concerns the distinction between utopian
      imagination (abstract) and utopian praxis (concrete) (Slater 2024). In future thinking,
      ErnstBloch(1995)distinguishesbetweenabstractandconcreteutopianism,emphasizing
      that utopian thought recognizes the world’s unfinished nature and the need for revolu-
      tionaryactivitytotransformthepresentintoabetterfuture.RuthLevitas(2013)further
      adds that fostering the political capacities necessary for creating alternative worlds is
      essential, moving beyond mere wishful thinking to actionable change. She suggests
      viewingutopiasasamethod.Thus,theutopianstudyofyouthclimatemovementsbuilds
      on these frameworks, remaining relational to utopian praxis.
        As an important note Bargue´s et al. (2024) reminds that Arendt’s The Human
      Condition(1958a)wasoriginallytitledAmorMundi(lovefortheworld)butwaschanged
      bythepublisher.Arendt’sconceptof‘lovefortheworld’impliesthatthroughcollective
      action, we can express our unique individuality, arising not from linear, isolated de-
      velopment but from relational openness to potential. This idea aligns with Bloch’s
      understanding of hope, celebrating human creativity and spontaneity.
        Arendt’s notion of natality, understood as the capacity for new beginnings, is par-
      ticularlyrelevanttodiscussionsontheclimateemergencyanddemandedsystemchange.
      Her depiction of brokenness in The Origins of Totalitarianism (1958b) highlights a
      profound end to the world, especially concerning the Holocaust, a sentiment echoed in
      contemporaryreflectionsonclimatecrisesasthetotalcollapseoftheplanet(Bargue´setal.
      2024). I suggest that Arendt’s concept of natality resonates with youth activism de-
      manding different world(s), serving as a source for envisioning alternative futures and
      new possibilities, which I conclude in the final section.

      andheressentialconcepts–suchaspublicaction,freedom,natality,andplurality–Iargue
      that we can better appreciate how utopian lenses shape future visions of contemporary
      youth movements.                                       
        Arendt’semphasisonactionandpluralityrevealsthatthepublicsphereisnotmerelya
      stage for debate; it is a vital space for collective agency and transformation. Youth
      activists,embodyingthisprinciple,engageinpracticesthatreflecttheirvisionsforjustice
      and sustainability. This engagement exemplifies what Yates et al. (2024) describe as
      prefigurative politics,highlighting theperformativenatureofactivism,wheretheact of
      imagining and enacting alternatives becomes central to political engagement. Notably,
      youth-led climate movements manifest Arendtian political freedom through collective,
      spontaneous public actions that challenge and resist the current global system in a
      performative and prefigurative spirit. Prefigurative politics enables activists to navigate
      the complexities of environmental challenges while fostering democratic participation
      and imaginative futures. This understanding resonates with the concept of natality,
