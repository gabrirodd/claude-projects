      Conclusion                                             
      I have argued that institutional racism faces significant problems when understood as
      adverse disproportionate impact, that there are good reasons to question its supposed
      superior explanatory power, and that there is reason to think that institutional and
      structuralreformcouldnotprovideuswithallthatweshouldwantintheracialdomain.I
      have also argued that institutional racism should not be understood simply as unjust
      institutionally-caused or -perpetuated racial inequality. In all this, I have suggested that
      because something is not an instance of institutional racism does not mean that it is
      morally in the clear. As Blum has argued, we do not face a choice between ‘calling
      something “racist” or seeing it as of no moral concern at all’.82 I have also sought to
      emphasize the continuing importance of changing hearts and minds – and not just
      institutions – in the long struggle for racial equality.
