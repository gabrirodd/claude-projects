## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens with a normative puzzle: if algorithms actively produce social realities, they should be subject to accountability — yet they largely are not. The rival mapping targets the three traditional accountability institutions (media, government, jurisprudence) and shows, through immanent critique, that each is structurally obstructed by the same three properties of algorithmic systems (secrecy, complexity, speed). The normative derivation points toward democratic governance of digital data markets but does not develop solutions — this is reserved for the full paper, which the abstract previews. The conclusion is open by design. Type 2a because the argument is analytic-reconstructive (identifying the structural conditions of failure through internal critique of existing institutions) with a clear normative thesis (algorithmic accountability is structurally impossible within current arrangements). The extended abstract format constrains the constructive move.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

If algorithms actively produce realities rather than merely describing them — shaping information flows, ranking content, placing ads, predicting user behaviour — then the modalities of those realities should be subject to scrutiny and critical reflection. Why is algorithmic accountability so difficult to achieve in practice, and what are the structural reasons for this difficulty?

---

## 4. Core argument

Algorithmic accountability is obstructed by three interlocking structural problems:

1. **Secrecy**: Internet companies protect algorithmic formulas as trade secrets and competitive advantages. The "gaming the system" justification is partially valid but also serves to prevent scrutiny of discriminatory design choices. Google's PageRank is a core product asset; transparency would erode market position. Secrecy is both a technical and a political choice.

2. **Complexity**: Even if an algorithm were made accessible, "smoking gun" evidence of hardcoded bias would be difficult to find. Algorithms function not as standalone boxes but as *networked sociotechnical assemblages* — people debate models, set target goals, clean training data, adjust parameters, choose application contexts. Discrimination and bias enter through these distributed human choices rather than as explicit programmatic directives, making accountability diffuse by design.

3. **Speed**: Online companies continuously update, test, and abolish algorithms in rapid experimental cycles. Facebook's "emotional contagion" study exemplifies this: conducted without user knowledge, it generated public outrage, a quick apology, and the removal of the specific feature — while similar experimental practices continued. In a fast-paced hit-and-miss space, accountability concerns become "downright outdated."

Together, these produce a "veritable crisis" of the three traditional accountability institutions — media, government, jurisprudence — all hampered by lack of data, expertise, and technical skills. The result: a private sector dominated by a few increasingly powerful organisations that monopolise available online data, strengthen their market position, and become *obligatory passage points* (Callon) in a networked world.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Normative-analytical: maps the structural obstacles (secrecy, complexity, speed), diagnoses institutional failure, and points toward small-scale (improving existing watchdog practices) and large-scale (democratic governance alternatives) solutions. The framework draws on STS (Callon's obligatory passage points, Gillespie on algorithms as cultural objects) and critical data studies (boyd & Crawford, Barocas & Selbst on disparate impact). The argumentative move shows that the problem is *structural*, not merely a matter of individual corporate bad faith — the accountability crisis requires systemic solutions, not just better enforcement.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Algorithmic accountability**: the capacity to render algorithmic systems answerable for their decisions and social consequences — to scrutinise their assumptions and evaluate their effects
- **Algorithmic opacity / inscrutability**: the condition in which algorithms' internal workings are inaccessible to external scrutiny, either by design (secrecy) or structural complexity
- **Sociotechnical assemblage**: the networked system of human and non-human actors within which algorithms function — accountability is distributed across this assemblage, not locatable in a single site
- **Obligatory passage point** (Callon): an actor (here, a platform) through whom all other actors must pass to achieve their goals — a structural position of power that enables monopolisation and resistance to accountability
- **Datafication**: the conversion of social life into data formats suitable for algorithmic processing — a structural transformation that precedes specific algorithmic applications
- **Algorithmic accountability reporting** (Diakopoulos): the emerging journalistic practice of investigating algorithmic systems through technical analysis and public reporting — one watchdog mechanism the paper hopes to improve

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Open and programmatic, by design. The diagnosis of structural obstruction is argued with considerable analytical force. The normative direction — democratic governance of digital data markets — is clear. The paper identifies two levels of solution (incremental improvement of watchdog institutions, more far-reaching democratic alternatives) but develops neither. The citation of ongoing Google antitrust investigations as pointing "in the right direction" already looks dated, but the structural analysis remains valid.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

As an extended abstract, the paper explicitly defers full argument development and solution evaluation to the complete paper. The diagnosis of watchdog failure is asserted (with case references) rather than evidenced systematically. Proposed solutions are named but not evaluated for feasibility or democratic legitimacy. The paper does not address the tension between algorithmic transparency and legitimate trade secrecy, or the technical limits of interpretability even when access is granted.

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Directly foundational for **Paris (2018)**: Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* and Hicks's *Programmed Inequality* document empirically what Rieder & Simon diagnose structurally.
- Connects to **Young et al. (2025)** on procedural ethics: both identify the need for transparency and scrutiny of algorithmic systems, approaching from different frameworks (accountability journalism vs. procedural ethics of game design).
- The sociotechnical assemblage concept aligns with **Hu (n.d.)**'s analysis of how blockchain governance is distributed across heterogeneous actors — both resist the "black box" model.
- **Jaros (2019)**'s "surveillance commoditism" can be read as a more radical version of this diagnosis: where Rieder & Simon focus on accountability mechanisms, Jaros argues the epistemic framework itself is colonised.
- "Obligatory passage point" (Callon) connects to **Amaral et al. (2025)** on how platforms structure the conditions of political possibility — both identify platform monopolisation as a governance problem, not just a market problem.
