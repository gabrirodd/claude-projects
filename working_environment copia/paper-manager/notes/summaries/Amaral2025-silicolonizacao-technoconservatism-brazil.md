# Amaral, Rosa & Sabariego (2025) — Da silicolonização do mundo ao tecnoconservadorismo: o renascimento do extremismo político brasileiro no século 21

**Zotero key:** NFWRC5HP  
**Date:** 2025-10-15  
**Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (theory-driven empirical / genealogical)

Combines Foucauldian genealogical methodology with empirical-historical case material on Brazilian far-right politics and Silicon Valley discourse. Both layers are necessary: the genealogy establishes the structural conditions; the empirical mapping of Brazilian technoconservatism tests and specifies those conditions.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

How has US neoliberal rationality — amplified through Silicon Valley's entrepreneurial discourse and its convergence with conservative reaction — enabled the Brazilian far right to return to power in the 21st century? What is the specific genealogy of "technoconservatism" in Brazil, and how is it structurally articulated with the international radical right and with digital platforms as both infrastructure and ideological actors?

The paper's underlying puzzle is why Brazil — rather than simply receiving Silicon Valley's discourse as cultural import — produced its own technoconservative tradition (centred on Olavo de Carvalho) that was coherently connected to, but not reducible to, the international far-right network.

## 3. Core argument

The paper argues for a structural-genealogical connection between "silicolonisation" and Brazilian technoconservatism, unfolding across two stages:

**Stage 1 — Genealogy of Silicon Valley discourse**: The discourse did not arrive fully formed. It originated in the 1970s with a countercultural, libertarian bent (Stewart Brand's *Whole Earth Catalog*, the Homebrew Computer Club, early hacker culture). Through the 1980s–90s, this discourse gradually aligned with what Nancy Fraser calls "neoliberal reactionism" — the absorption of emancipatory cultural movements (anti-institutionalism, freedom from authority, individual sovereignty) into the logic of market deregulation, corporate accumulation, and the rollback of the welfare state. By the 2000s–2010s, the same rhetorical toolkit (freedom, disruption, anti-elitism) was available for far-right appropriation: libertarian anti-statism becomes anti-democratic authoritarianism once state institutions are identified with a corrupt "establishment."

**Stage 2 — Brazilian technoconservatism**: Olavo de Carvalho, beginning in the late 1990s (initially through esoteric and astrological publications, then through political commentary), mobilised diffuse segments of the Brazilian far right and built significant visibility through a publishing market dedicated to translating, publishing, and disseminating books used as weapons in the *guerra cultural* (cultural war). His influence expanded structurally through digital platforms — YouTube, WhatsApp, Telegram — whose algorithmic amplification of outrage and engagement rewarded his confrontational, conspiratorial style. The paper maps the specific individuals, groups, and companies that constitute the Brazilian technoconservative ecosystem, showing not only their connections to the international far right (Steve Bannon, the Claremont Institute, European identitarian networks) but also their domestic infrastructure (publishing houses, think tanks, media platforms).

The genealogical claim is that "silicolonisation" — Éric Sadin's term for Silicon Valley's colonisation of imagination, subjectivity, and politics through its technological and ideological exports — is the enabling condition of Brazilian technoconservatism. The digital platform business model (engagement maximisation, algorithmic amplification, data monetisation) did not merely *carry* Olavo's content; it structurally shaped which content would flourish.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Foucauldian genealogy in two stages: (1) excavating the discursive formation of Silicon Valley's political discourse — identifying the historical forces, actors, and turning points that transformed countercultural libertarianism into neoliberal reactionism; (2) mapping the Brazilian technoconservative field — the actors, networks, publishing infrastructure, and platform dynamics that constitute it. Evidence is drawn from political history, media analysis, secondary literature on Silicon Valley ideology, and analysis of Brazilian far-right publications and networks. The genealogy deliberately avoids linear causal narrative, instead tracing the "forces at play" at each historical moment.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Silicolonização / silicolonisation** (Éric Sadin): the process by which Silicon Valley's technological exports also export its epistemological, political, and subjective frameworks — colonising imagination and governance globally
- **Neoliberal reactionism** (Nancy Fraser): the alignment of progressive cultural movements (feminism, anti-racism, LGBTQ+ rights, environmentalism) with neoliberal economic structures, which simultaneously produces a reactionary backlash that recodes the same anti-establishment rhetoric for the right
- **Technoconservatism**: the hybridisation of technolibertarian Silicon Valley ideology with traditional conservative and far-right politics — anti-statist, anti-establishment, culturally reactive, digitally organised
- **Guerra cultural** (cultural war): the far right's strategic deployment of culture — publishing, media, social platforms — as a site of political struggle, drawing on Gramscian (ironically) concepts of hegemony for counter-hegemonic purposes
- **Foucauldian genealogy**: not a history of origins but a tracing of "forces at play" — the discontinuous, conflict-laden emergence of discursive formations through historical struggle
- **Digital platforms as governance infrastructure**: platforms not merely as neutral carriers of content but as active shapers of political possibility through algorithmic design, content moderation (or its absence), and business model incentives

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately assertive and diagnostically complete. The genealogical framing is well-sustained and the connection between Silicon Valley discourse and Brazilian technoconservatism is argued with considerable historical specificity. The paper does not overclaim: it does not argue that the far right's return to power was *caused* by silicolonisation, but that the structural conditions it created were enabling. The conclusion is diagnostic rather than prescriptive — it charts the structural conditions of technoconservatism for further analytical and political work, but does not specify remedies.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

- The paper does not directly address counter-movements or forms of digital resistance within Brazil — the analytical frame remains structural/genealogical rather than agential
- The genealogical method (by design) resists precise causal attribution — the paper cannot specify exactly *how much* platform amplification explains Bolsonarism as opposed to other factors (economic crisis, evangelical growth, security discourse)
- The analysis centres on Brazil as a case but implies broader applicability (to other Latin American far-right movements, to European identitarianism) without fully demonstrating the comparison
- The mapping of the Brazilian technoconservative ecosystem, while detailed, is not exhaustive and relies on publicly available information — covert funding flows and organisational structures are not fully visible

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Methodologically closest to **Hu (n.d.)**: both use Foucauldian genealogy to analyse how digital infrastructure is co-produced with political governance, and both refuse the simple repression/liberation binary. The contrast is geopolitical: Hu examines state-directed technoconservatism in China; Amaral et al. examine market-platform-enabled technoconservatism in Brazil.
- Directly complements **Paris (2018)** and **Rieder & Simon (2015)** on platform power and algorithmic accountability: Amaral et al. show the political consequences when accountability mechanisms fail and platforms amplify far-right content.
- The silicolonisation thesis extends **Danter et al. (2016)**'s argument about American cultural specificity: not just that QS is American, but that Silicon Valley's ideology is an active export that reshapes political culture elsewhere.
- Connects to **Jaros (2019)**'s concept of "surveillance commoditism" — both diagnose a hegemonic digital-economic formation that colonises subjectivity, though Jaros focuses on epistemics and Amaral et al. on explicit political mobilisation.
