# Jaros, Milan (2019) — The Episteme of Meta-Modernity: Order, Value, and Citizenship in the Space of 'Digital Finitudes'

**Zotero key:** PKV8J65F  
**Date:** 2019-04-15  
**Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type detected: Theoretical / normative

Primarily programmatic and conceptual: proposes a new "meta-modern" Foucauldian episteme as a corrective to what the author diagnoses as "surveillance commoditism" and the epistemic crisis of value and citizenship in digital modernity. No original empirical data; the argumentative mode is philosophical-diagnostic with a strong normative dimension.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

The neoliberal division of labour, driven by digital complexification, has produced a growing gap between emergent knowledge and human systems — between knowing and being, between the human content of work and its measurable outcomes, between value as human capacity and value as price signal. The result is that most consequential decisions are now made within the "chaotic space of ephemeral price relations" manufactured by what the paper calls "surveillance commoditism." The puzzle: is it possible to construct an alternative epistemic framework — an "onto-epistemic variable" — that can ground a rational basis for defeating "digitally enhanced serfdom" and restoring value, citizenship, and human independence?

## 3. Core argument

The paper argues that advances in quantitative, empirical methodologies, rather than simply reinforcing the surveillance-commoditism complex, also open an "action space" for a fresh research agenda: recasting our past and present into *transparent, directional genealogical accounts of order generation and actualisation*. This is the proposed *meta-modern Foucauldian episteme*: a framework in which the concept of "order freed of power-hungry impositions" assumes the role of an onto-epistemic variable — a foundational unit of analysis through which knowledge production, value, and citizenship can be re-grounded outside the logic of price and surveillance.

The argument rests on several interconnected claims:
1. Complexification, fuelled by neoliberal division of labour, has "acquired a life of its own" — outpacing human systems and generating the conditions for surveillance commoditism (the colonisation of value by commodity-price relations mediated through data capture).
2. This epistemic colonisation is the key reason why the "lab & cloister" (sites of knowledge production, whether scientific or humanistic) have become subordinated to the demands of "data-rich runaway" capital.
3. A Foucauldian genealogical approach — tracing the historical emergence of "order" as a principle of social organisation — can reveal the contingency of current epistemic arrangements and open space for alternatives.
4. The necessary condition for this alternative to assert itself is a radical methodological transformation of educational and management programmes — "aimed at bottom-up ownership of, and responsibility for the making, choosing, and symbolising" — with the goal of restoring knowledge's "foundational mission of liberation by reason."

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Normative-genealogical: the paper traces the epistemic crisis through the history of neoliberal quantification and datafication, then proposes an alternative episteme. The argumentative structure is philosophical-diagnostic: identify the pathology (surveillance commoditism, the gap between knowledge and value), trace its genealogy (neoliberal complexification), propose a corrective (meta-modern Foucauldian episteme with "order" as onto-epistemic variable), specify the institutional requirements (educational/management transformation). No empirical data; the evidence is historical-conceptual. The paper's language is dense and at times programmatic in a way that requires inference to reconstruct specific claims.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Surveillance commoditism** (Jaros's proposed term): the hegemonic formation in which surveillance data is the medium through which all value is converted into price signal and fed into commodity relations — analogous to Zuboff's surveillance capitalism but emphasising the epistemic/value dimension rather than the behavioural futures market
- **Meta-modern Foucauldian episteme** (proposed): a new epistemic framework that uses genealogical method to recover "order" as an onto-epistemic variable outside the surveillance-commoditism complex
- **Order** (as onto-epistemic variable): the paper's proposed foundational concept — "order freed of power-hungry impositions" — which is meant to provide a non-commodified measure of human actualisation, though the concept remains underspecified
- **Digital finitudes**: the constraints, limits, and determinations that digital infrastructure imposes on knowledge, value, and citizenship — framed not as absolute barriers but as historically contingent formations that genealogy can reveal and challenge
- **Lab & cloister**: Jaros's term for the complementary sites of knowledge production (scientific laboratory + humanistic scholarly institution) that are both now subordinated to surveillance commoditism
- **Digitally enhanced serfdom**: the dystopian outcome the paper seeks to defeat — the reduction of citizens to data-producing serfs of surveillance capital, stripped of autonomous value and genuine political citizenship
- **Bottom-up ownership**: the educational and organisational transformation required — subjects who actively participate in the making and symbolising of value, rather than receiving it as price signal from above

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Normatively assertive in diagnosis; programmatically open in prescription. The diagnosis of surveillance commoditism and epistemic colonisation is advanced with confidence. The proposed meta-modern Foucauldian episteme is presented as a genuine alternative, but the "order freed of power-hungry impositions" concept is not sufficiently specified to function as the operative variable the paper claims it can be. The conclusion is both a research agenda and a political-educational programme — calling for transformation of educational and management institutions — but without specifying institutional pathways, mechanisms, or actors. The paper ends with an exhortation rather than a plan.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

Primarily implicit. The paper does not engage with existing critical data studies (Zuboff, boyd & Crawford), surveillance studies (Lyon, Haggerty & Ericson), or STS literature, which limits its dialogue with the fields most directly concerned with the phenomena it diagnoses. The "meta-modern" concept is ambitious but underdeveloped: it is not clearly distinguished from existing uses of "metamodernism" (the cultural-aesthetic movement) or from other post-postmodern theoretical positions. The "order" concept as onto-epistemic variable is the paper's most original claim but also its least elaborated — without more specification, it risks functioning as an empty signifier rather than an analytical tool. No empirical grounding is provided for the genealogical claims.

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Most directly aligned with **Baumgarten (2025)** in its programmatic-normative register and its call for a new critical framework adequate to the digital conjuncture. Both papers are prolegomenary rather than executory.
- The "surveillance commoditism" concept extends and radicalises **Rieder & Simon (2015)**'s diagnosis of algorithmic opacity: where Rieder & Simon focus on the institutional failure of accountability mechanisms, Jaros argues the epistemic framework itself is colonised and requires replacement.
- Connects to **Amaral et al. (2025)** on neoliberal rationality as the structural condition of digital power, and to **Hu (n.d.)** on the way digital infrastructure co-produces governance forms.
- The call for genealogical method to reveal the contingency of current arrangements is shared with **Hu** and **Amaral** — all three use Foucauldian genealogy, but Jaros applies it at the broadest epistemic level (the concept of "order" itself) rather than to specific political formations.
- The "lab & cloister" concept and the emphasis on educational transformation are unique in the corpus, pointing toward an institutional dimension that other papers do not address.
