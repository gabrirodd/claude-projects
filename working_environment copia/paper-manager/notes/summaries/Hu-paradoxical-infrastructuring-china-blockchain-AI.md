# Hu, Zichen (n.d.) — Paradoxical Infrastructuring: Genealogies of Governance and "Art of Being Governed" in China's Blockchain–AI Hypes

**Zotero key:** 2H8WDYB2  
**Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (theory-driven empirical)

Combines a substantial Foucauldian genealogical theoretical apparatus with empirical case material drawn from policy documents, journalistic sources, industry reports, and crypto-community discourse. Both layers are load-bearing: the empirical material is not illustrative but constitutive of the genealogical argument.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

How are imaginaries of decentralisation and sovereignty rearticulated through blockchain and AI infrastructures in China? Specifically: how do state actors, intermediary platforms, and civil society navigate the tension between blockchain's libertarian/cryptographic affordances and the Chinese state's centralising imperatives? And why does this tension not resolve into simple repression but generate new, internally contradictory governance formations?

The puzzle is why China's blockchain boom did not simply end with the 2021 crypto ban, but instead fed into the AI infrastructure build-out that followed — suggesting a structural continuity, not a break.

## 3. Core argument

China's trajectory from blockchain hype to AI institutionalisation is not a linear story of innovation-then-repression but a process of *paradoxical infrastructuring*: the decentralising affordances of blockchain (cryptographic trust, peer-to-peer ledgers, tokenomics) are alternately valorised, domesticated, and redeployed as building blocks within AI's centralising apparatus. The key move is that ideological contradiction — between decentralisation's libertarian promise and authoritarian state control — is not a failure or incoherence to be resolved, but a *generative governance feature*: it creates productive ambiguity that intermediary actors (fintech companies, VC firms, state-linked enterprises) exploit through what the paper calls *techno-nostalgic arbitrage* (leveraging the cultural capital of blockchain's decentralisation rhetoric to claim legitimacy in the AI space while operating within state-sanctioned centralised structures).

The paper also uses Szonyi's "art of being governed" — a theory of how subjects tactically navigate state authority not by resisting it but by creatively inhabiting its contradictions — to account for how non-state actors in China manoeuvre within blockchain/AI regulatory ambiguity.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Foucauldian genealogy (tracing discursive sedimentations and ruptures rather than searching for origins) combined with Szonyi's "art of being governed." The genealogical move consists in showing that blockchain discourse in China carries multiple embedded and conflicting historical layers — Cold War rationalities, socialist modernisation narratives, 1990s internet utopianism — that are selectively activated and deactivated as political conditions shift. Empirically, the paper maps a heterogeneous corpus: Chinese state policy documents, party-state media, fintech journalism, VC white papers, and crypto/blockchain community discourse. The analytical movement runs from macro-governmentality (state strategy, regulatory regimes) down to everyday intermediary practice and back, showing how the two levels co-produce each other.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Paradoxical infrastructuring** (proposed): the process by which technically decentralising affordances are absorbed into centralising governance regimes, with the contradiction functioning as a productive feature rather than a resolved tension
- **Governmentality** (Foucault): the rationalities and techniques through which subjects are governed and come to govern themselves
- **Art of being governed** (Szonyi): tactical navigation of state authority by inhabiting rather than resisting its contradictions — applied here to Chinese platform actors and communities
- **Infrastructural imaginaries**: shared visions of what a technology can or should do that shape investment, policy, and community formation
- **Discursive assemblages**: heterogeneous collections of texts, practices, and actors that cohere around a shared technological imaginary without being unified by a single logic
- **Infrastructural subjectivation**: the process by which technical infrastructure produces and shapes subject positions (who can speak, invest, govern, or be governed through the technology)
- **Ghost infrastructure**: blockchain systems that continue to circulate as cultural reference points (in VC pitches, state rhetoric) even after their technical operations have been banned or wound down
- **Techno-nostalgic arbitrage**: the strategic use of blockchain's libertarian cultural capital to claim legitimacy in the post-blockchain, AI-dominated regulatory environment
- **Epistemic sovereignty**: the state's project of controlling the knowledge frameworks through which digital technologies are understood and governed

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately strong and theoretically assertive. The paper confidently positions China not as a special case (authoritarian deviation from a liberal norm) but as "a particularly vivid instance" of a global condition in which decentralisation and centralisation are co-constitutive rather than opposed — a significant analytical claim. The concept of paradoxical infrastructuring is proposed as generalisable beyond China to other techno-political contexts (e.g., EU AI governance, US fintech regulation). The conclusion ends on an open analytical horizon rather than predictive claims, calling for further empirical and comparative work.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

- The internal opacity of Chinese bureaucratic deliberations makes it methodologically difficult to isolate causal mechanisms or attribute specific regulatory shifts to particular actors or events; the paper explicitly warns against such attributions
- The empirical corpus is entirely discursive (texts, media, reports) rather than ethnographic or interview-based, limiting access to lived practice and the actual experience of navigating regulatory ambiguity
- The paper explicitly flags the risk of drawing "direct equivalences" between premodern governance forms (Szonyi's historical context) and contemporary digital regulation
- Quantitative crypto-activity data (transaction volumes, wallet counts) are described as estimates with unverified precision; the paper does not rely on them heavily but uses them for contextualisation

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Methodologically aligned with **Amaral et al. (2025)** — both use Foucauldian genealogy to trace the political economy of digital infrastructure, though Amaral focuses on Brazil and the far right while Hu focuses on China's state/platform nexus.
- Complements **Pavlov et al. (n.d.)** on blockchain governance: where Pavlov analyses the protocol's internal power logic, Hu analyses the external political-regulatory environment that shapes how blockchain is deployed and domesticated.
- Connects to **Rieder & Simon (2015)** and **Paris (2018)** on platform monopolisation and the limits of governance, extending those arguments into a non-Western, state-capitalist context.
- The concept of "ghost infrastructure" speaks productively to **Jaros (2019)**'s diagnosis of surveillance commoditism: both describe how the cultural apparatus of a technology outlives its specific technical implementations.
