## 1. Paper type detected: Theoretical / normative (extended abstract)

This is an extended abstract making a normative argument about the structural limits of algorithmic accountability and proposing directions for democratic governance of digital data markets. It is empirically informed (citing specific cases — Google antitrust investigations, Facebook's emotional contagion study) but does not present original empirical research. The argument is analytical-diagnostic with a normative conclusion.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

If algorithms actively produce realities rather than merely describing them — shaping information flows, ranking content, placing advertisements, predicting user behaviour — then the modalities of those realities should be subject to scrutiny and critical reflection. Put differently: if people's actions and experiences are increasingly defined by algorithmic processes, shouldn't we be able to hold those systems accountable? The paper's puzzle is why, given this seemingly obvious normative requirement, algorithmic accountability is so difficult to achieve in practice, and what the structural reasons for this difficulty are.

## 3. Core argument

Algorithmic accountability is obstructed by three interlocking structural problems:

1. **Secrecy**: Internet companies protect their algorithmic formulas as trade secrets and competitive advantages. The justification — that disclosure would allow "gaming the system" — is partially valid but also serves to prevent scrutiny of discriminatory or bias-laden design choices. Google's PageRank, for instance, is a core product asset; transparency would erode market position. Secrecy is both a technical and a political choice.

2. **Complexity**: Even if a specific algorithm were made accessible and the necessary technical expertise available, "smoking gun" evidence of hardcoded bias would be difficult to find. This is because algorithms do not function as standalone boxes but as *networked sociotechnical assemblages* — people debate models, set target goals, clean training data, adjust parameters, choose application contexts. Discrimination and bias enter through these distributed human choices rather than as explicit programmatic directives, making accountability diffuse by design.

3. **Speed**: Online companies continuously update, test, and abolish algorithms in rapid experimental cycles. Facebook's "emotional contagion" study exemplifies this: conducted without user knowledge, it generated public outrage, a quick apology, and the removal of the specific feature — while similar experimental practices continued. In this fast-paced hit-and-miss space, accountability concerns become "downright outdated": there is no stable object to scrutinise before it changes.

Together, these three factors produce a "veritable crisis" of the three institutions that traditionally demand accountability — media, government, and jurisprudence — all of which are "hampered by a lack of data, expertise, and technical skills." The result is a private sector dominated by a few increasingly powerful organisations that monopolise available online data, strengthen their market position, and become *obligatory passage points* (Callon's STS concept: actors through whom all other actors must pass to achieve their goals) in a networked world.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Normative-analytical: the paper maps the structural obstacles (secrecy, complexity, speed), diagnoses the institutional failure they produce, and proposes directions for small-scale (improving existing watchdog practices) and large-scale (more far-reaching democratic governance alternatives) solutions. The framework draws on STS (Callon's obligatory passage points, Gillespie's work on algorithms as cultural objects) and critical data studies (boyd & Crawford, Barocas & Selbst on disparate impact). The argumentative move is to show that the problem is *structural*, not merely a matter of individual corporate bad faith — the accountability crisis requires systemic solutions, not just better regulatory enforcement of existing frameworks.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Algorithmic accountability**: the capacity to render algorithmic systems answerable for their decisions and their social consequences — to scrutinise their assumptions and evaluate their effects
- **Algorithmic opacity / inscrutability**: the condition in which algorithms' internal workings are not accessible to external scrutiny, either by design (secrecy) or by structural complexity
- **Sociotechnical assemblage**: the networked system of human and non-human actors (people, data, hardware, institutional contexts) within which algorithms function — accountability is distributed across this assemblage rather than locatable in a single site
- **Obligatory passage point** (Callon): an actor (here, a platform like Google or Facebook) through whom all other actors must pass to achieve their goals — a structural position of power that enables monopolisation and resistance to accountability
- **Datafication**: the conversion of social life into data formats suitable for algorithmic processing — a structural transformation of social practice that precedes specific algorithmic applications
- **Algorithmic accountability reporting** (Diakopoulos): the emerging journalistic practice of investigating algorithmic systems through technical analysis and public reporting — one of the watchdog mechanisms the paper hopes to improve

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Open and programmatic, by design (this is an extended abstract). The diagnosis of structural obstruction is advanced with considerable analytical force. The normative direction — democratic governance of digital data markets — is clear. The paper identifies two levels of solution (incremental improvement of watchdog institutions, more far-reaching democratic alternatives) but does not develop either: this is reserved for the full paper, which the abstract previews. The citation of ongoing EU and US Google antitrust investigations as pointing "in the right direction" already looks dated given subsequent developments, but the structural analysis remains relevant.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

As an extended abstract, the paper explicitly defers full argument development and solution evaluation. The diagnosis of watchdog failure is asserted (with case references) rather than evidenced through systematic study. The proposed solutions — both incremental and large-scale — are named but not evaluated for feasibility or democratic legitimacy. The paper does not address the tension between algorithmic transparency and legitimate trade secrecy, or the technical limits of interpretability even when access is granted (the "black box" problem).

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Directly foundational for **Paris (2018)**, which reviews the books that empirically document what Rieder & Simon diagnose structurally: Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* shows the discriminatory consequences of algorithmic opacity; Hicks's *Programmed Inequality* shows the historical roots of these structural exclusions.
- Connects to **Young et al. (2025)** on procedural ethics: both papers identify the need for transparency and scrutiny of algorithmic systems, but through different frameworks (accountability journalism vs. procedural ethics of game design).
- The sociotechnical assemblage concept aligns with **Hu (n.d.)**'s analysis of how blockchain governance is distributed across heterogeneous actors — both resist the "black box" model of algorithmic systems.
- **Jaros (2019)**'s "surveillance commoditism" can be read as a more radical version of the diagnosis Rieder & Simon offer: where they focus on accountability mechanisms, Jaros argues the epistemic framework itself is colonised.
- The "obligatory passage point" concept (Callon) speaks to **Amaral et al. (2025)**'s analysis of how platforms structure the conditions of political possibility — both identify platform monopolisation as a governance problem, not just a market problem.
