# The CCRU as an Attempt of Doing Philosophy in a Digital World

**Author:** Balić, Tvrtko
**Published in:** *Digital Humanities* (Conference on Language Technologies & Digital Humanities, Ljubljana, 2022)
**Item Key:** KDI3M2HS

---

## 1. Paper type detected

**Type 4a — Diagnostic framework via contemporary case (the CCRU as institutional and intellectual response to the Internet).**

This is a short conference paper (~6 pages, abstract format). It is primarily descriptive and expository — a landscape survey of the CCRU and its intellectual affiliations — rather than a strictly normative intervention in the critical-theory sense. The typology code Type 4 applies in a loose sense: an existing theoretical framework (postmodern theory: Lyotard, Derrida, Deleuze/Guattari, Baudrillard) is deployed against a contemporary cultural-intellectual case (the CCRU as an institutional and aesthetic response to the Internet's emergence). The case generates a partial assessment of what succeeded (Plant's gender insights, Fisher's blogging, Land's continuing influence) and what failed (the CCRU's excessive opacity, its status primarily as a "literary club" rather than a research unit). The conclusion is open and cautiously normative — the CCRU is valuable as a "valiant attempt at doing something new" whose speculative excesses may contain "oddly prophetic" moments.

Epistemic mode: primarily descriptive-historical with residual normative evaluation.

## 3. Puzzle or research question

The puzzle is stated directly: "The consequences brought about by the Internet have been immense." Natural sciences could absorb the resulting information abundance; social sciences and humanities found themselves in a new environment that was simultaneously an object of study and a transformative medium. The question is: how did one experimental collective (the CCRU) attempt to adapt philosophy to this new digital milieu — and what can we learn from that attempt?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. The CCRU's intellectual genealogy is French postmodern theory.** Lyotard (postmodern condition, metanarrative decay, the Internet as the environment Lyotard's theories describe), Derrida (dismantling boundaries between philosophy and literature; the hope for playful, ecstatic writing), Deleuze/Guattari (schizoanalysis; rhizomatic thought; deterritorialisation and reterritorialisation; accelerationist embrace of social change), and Baudrillard (simulation, simulacra, hyperreality; the Desert of the Real). Together these provide the theoretical substrate within which the Ccru's theory-fiction experiments operated.

**b. The CCRU produced theory-fiction as a new genre.** The Ccru's output — "a surreal drug fueled collection of writing about Lovecraftian demons, numerology, ghost lemurs of Madagascar" — is better understood as a literary club than a research unit, though philosophical ideas are embedded within the chaos of collective storytelling. The term "theory-fiction" (a "surreal combination of cyberpunk and Gothic horror") names the genre: philosophically dense but literarily extreme writing in which the line between fictional and theoretical claims is deliberately dissolved.

**c. Hyperstition is the CCRU's key philosophical concept.** "Hyperstitions are fictions that make themselves real, like how the concept of space travel caused space travel to come into reality." All ideas can be understood as hyperstition, using humans as "hosts that bring them into existence." This explains the importance of the artistic style for some members: writing is itself a form of magic — a material intervention in reality rather than a mere representation of it.

**d. From the CCRU emerged three divergent political strands.** Sadie Plant (cyberfeminism — the Internet offers a space without physical bodies, computer technology is inherently feminine, emancipation of machines brings emancipation of women; "in some respects, Plant proved prophetic"), Mark Fisher (blogging as digital agora and glimmer of hope against capitalist realism; Fisher's suicide noted as the consequence of depression and the contradiction in his own optimism), and Nick Land (capitalism as artificial intelligence, meme magick, Bitcoin metaphysics; "for better or worse the member of the CCRU who is most prominent today").

**e. The CCRU is relevant today because digital environments are now invisible.** "Today Internet is so ingrained in our lives that we don't even notice it anymore just as fish don't notice the water they are in." Looking back at the moment when the digital was new — and at the speculative theories it generated — can help us see with new eyes what we have normalised.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Expository genealogy followed by selective case analysis.** The paper proceeds in six numbered sections: Introduction (problem), Goal, Influences (four French theorists), "Playful and dangerous writing group" (CCRU description), Prominent figures (Plant, Fisher, Land), Concluding remarks. The method is primarily retrospective and landscape-painting: the paper does not advance a polemical thesis but introduces the CCRU to a Digital Humanities conference audience likely unfamiliar with it. The critical assessment is embedded but not foregrounded: Balić notes the CCRU was "primarily a literary club," that its philosophical content is "motifs and sources of inspiration arising from the chaos of collective storytelling," and that the "new digital agoras have since also been assimilated into the existing system" (in Fisher's sense of capitalist realism's coopting capacity). But these assessments are not defended against alternatives.

## 6. Key concepts

**Hyperstition (Ccru):** Fictions that make themselves real through feedback between idea and reality; humans are "hosts" through which ideas bring themselves into existence. The CCRU's key philosophical concept and the one with the broadest subsequent reception (connecting to meme magick, Land's Bitcoin philosophy, accelerationist politics).

**Theory-fiction (CCRU via Derrida):** A genre resulting from Derrida's aspiration to destroy boundaries between philosophy and literature. A "surreal combination of cyberpunk and Gothic horror" in which philosophical ideas are embedded within opaque, extreme literary forms. Ambiguous even at the level of literary meaning.

**Schizoanalysis (Deleuze/Guattari):** An alternative to rational thought that embraces schizophrenic abstraction — "thinking becomes chaotic yet orderly in its own way, within its own logic. Everything becomes rhizomatic." The Ccru's intellectual style is described as an attempt at schizoanalysis.

**Deterritorialisation / reterritorialisation (Deleuze/Guattari):** Deterritorialisation: the process by which social relations are altered, mutated, or destroyed. Reterritorialisation: the process by which new relations emerge. The CCRU's "revolutionary" accelerationist embrace of social change meant "celebrating deterritorialisation, whether for its own sake, motivated by a libertarian desire for freedom, or for the sake of better alternatives emerging."

**Hyperreality / simulacra (Baudrillard):** In the third order of simulacra, representation refers only to itself — "pure simulacra, something only ever referencing itself without any authentic reality behind it." The Internet is read as the fulfilment of Baudrillard's hyperreal, but for the CCRU there is hope that "from the 'Desert of the Real' will emerge something new" — an *amor fati* even without optimism.

**Meme magick:** Land-influenced online theory that Internet memes can influence reality supernaturally — the belief that Donald Trump won the 2016 election through meme magic. Connected to the earlier, theoretical concept of hyperstition but deployed in a populist far-right register.

**Postmodern condition (Lyotard):** The decay and disappearance of metanarratives — the grand explanatory stories that legitimate social orders. The Internet accelerates this process by making knowledge more available and multiplying competing narratives.

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Open and cautiously retrospective.** The conclusion notes that the CCRU is relevant because the Internet is now invisible — we have normalised what was once radically new. Looking back at early theoretical responses, including wrong-headed ones, "can prove useful." "Sometimes it happens that parts of writing prove to be oddly prophetic and in that case it is good to appreciate what we have or maybe just look at it with new eyes. And even when they seem wrong they represent a valiant attempt at doing something new." No strong normative claim is advanced; the CCRU is valued as an intellectual experiment, not canonised.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

The paper is a conference abstract / short paper and explicitly does not claim comprehensiveness. The presentation of Plant as "proving prophetic" about gender and the Internet is not qualified against more critical assessments of cyberfeminism's limits. The treatment of Land focuses on his philosophical positions (Bitcoin metaphysics, meme magick) without engaging with the racist and accelerationist political dimensions that Vinokour (2023) and O'Sullivan's accelerationism paper in this collection address at length. Fisher's death is mentioned without developing the structural argument about capitalist realism and mental health that Fisher himself made.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**CCRU genealogy:** Balić provides the most accessible introductory account of the CCRU in this corpus — its founding, its influences, its output, its divergent political legacies. It is best read alongside O'Sullivan's accelerationism paper (which provides a rigorous internal critique of the CCRU's political legacy) and O'Sullivan's *On Theory-Fiction* (which contextualises the Ccru within a broader history of theory-fiction).

**Hyperstition and "Tlön":** Balić's account of hyperstition ("fictions that make themselves real, like how the concept of space travel caused space travel to come into reality") is structurally isomorphic with what Riberi identifies as the mechanism of Borges's "Tlön" (a fiction that gradually colonises and replaces reality). This connection is not explicit in Balić's paper but is latent — and it is the intellectual core of what the "TLON, UKBAR, URBIS TERTIUS" collection as a whole appears to be exploring.

**Land, NRx, and the political right:** Balić's treatment of Land is descriptive and relatively neutral. Vinokour (2023) on Pelevin and O'Sullivan's accelerationism paper both provide more critical and politically alert analyses of Land's trajectory and its consequences. Balić's paper should be read as intellectual background rather than political critique.

**Fisher and blogging:** Balić's discussion of Fisher's optimism about blogging as a "digital agora" and its subsequent assimilation into the existing system connects to the broader corpus concern with the political potential and limits of digital public spheres. Fisher's account — "I started blogging as a way of getting back into writing after the traumatic experience of doing a PhD" — is one of the few places in this corpus where the personal experience of academic writing is directly thematised, connecting to Pezzano and Gualeni's paper on institutional constraints on philosophical writing.
