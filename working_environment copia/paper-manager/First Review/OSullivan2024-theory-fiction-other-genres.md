# On Theory-Fiction and Other Genres

**Author:** O'Sullivan, Simon
**Type:** Book (Palgrave Macmillan, 2024)
**Item Key:** NN9JRBJD

## 1. Paper type detected

**Theoretical / meta-critical monograph — closest to Type 2c (conceptual critique / distinction) in extended essayistic form, with significant genealogical and survey elements.**

This is a 2024 Palgrave Macmillan monograph, not a journal article, and it does not map cleanly onto a single typology code. The book comprises four essays (on theory-fiction, autofiction/autotheory, art writing, and a postscript on machine writing) plus an introduction and conclusion. The argumentative mode is consistently normative and constitutive — the definitions being constructed do not simply describe existing practices but participate in calling those practices forth. The closest analog in the typology is **Type 2c** (conceptual critique / distinction), where the dominant moves are genealogical reconstruction, conceptual distinction-making, and an open conclusion functioning as a research agenda. The book also draws on Type 2b (canonical thinker reconstruction — specifically Deleuze/Guattari, Laruelle, Haraway, Fisher) and on the critical survey function of Type 1 (landscape-mapping). The theory-fiction essay itself has a partially hyperstitional structure: it is about theory-fiction, but by O'Sullivan's own account it also participates in generating the scene it describes.

## 3. Puzzle or research question

The book opens with a dual puzzle. First, a descriptive-diagnostic puzzle: a range of hybrid writing practices — theory-fiction, autofiction/autotheory, art writing — are increasingly prevalent in the critical humanities and para-academic scenes, yet their distinctiveness and relationship to each other remain undertheorised. Second, a normative-performative puzzle: what does this kind of writing *do* that standard forms do not? O'Sullivan frames this as: "What does theory-fiction bring to theory? What does it add to it (if anything)?" The deeper question is about the conditions of possibility for writing that is both analytically rigorous and performatively potent — writing that, in the limit cases (hyperstition), does not just represent reality but helps bring about its own conditions of emergence. A subsidiary puzzle concerns the relationship between writing and community: how does marginal, counter-cultural, para-academic writing generate the collectives it addresses?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. These hybrid genres perform their content.** Theory-fiction, autofiction, and art writing share the quality of being performative utterances in a fairly strong sense — their form enacts what they claim. Eshun's *More Brilliant Than the Sun* reads like the music it analyses. The Ccru's nested fictions perform the hyperstitional logic they theorise. Preciado's *Testo Junkie* does not merely describe pharmacopornographic construction but stages it in real time. O'Sullivan uses Deleuze's term "dramatisation": theory-fiction invents characters and scenarios that allow concepts to be "lived," giving them more traction on a life.

**b. Theory-fiction deterritorialises the major language of Philosophy.** Following Deleuze and Guattari on minor literature and Laruelle on non-philosophy, O'Sullivan argues that theory-fiction drops the "arrogant operation" of Philosophy-with-a-capital-P — its claim to a god's-eye view — while increasing what he calls "combinatory powers." Theory-fiction is a "diagrammatic logic" that allows concepts to be untethered from their truth-value and repurposed as fictions, as "philo-fictions." This is described as "radically democratic."

**c. Hyperstition: fictions make themselves real.** The Ccru's definition is quoted directly: "reality is understood to be composed of fictions — consistent semiotic terrains that condition perceptual, affective and behaviorial responses" and "this reality can itself be re-fictioned." Hyperstition names fictions that insert themselves into reality and actualise already-present virtualities. Burroughs's cut-ups, Land's "Meltdown," Plant's *Zeros and Ones*, and Haraway's "Camille Stories" all operate this way. The retro-causality O'Sullivan describes — "the fiction works back on its conditions of emergence" — means theory-fiction is always temporally complex: it writes from the future into the present.

**d. Autofiction stages the fiction of the self.** Drawing on McKenzie Wark and Lauren Fournier, O'Sullivan argues that autofiction is not simply confessional but an investigation into how selves are produced as fictions. The reflexive move — writing yourself as a character to see yourself otherwise — is both a literary device and a political one, especially for "marked bodies" whose lives have been obscured or misrepresented. Preciado's "somatopolitical fiction," Hartman's critical fabulation, Sharpe's "wake work" are theory-fictions of the self that are simultaneously acts of decolonial and anti-heteronormative struggle.

**e. These genres are from and for communities to come.** The para-academic and minor character of these forms — published by Urbanomic, Book Works, Goldsmiths Press — means they are constituted in and through scenes. A scene is not just context but the condition of possibility for the writing itself. The Ccru pamphlets, Fisher's K-Punk blog, Harney and Moten's *The Undercommons*, Haraway's collective "Camille Stories" — all exemplify writing that addresses a community that does not yet fully exist and thereby helps bring it into existence.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

The book's method is what O'Sullivan elsewhere calls "diagrammatic": non-linear, combinatory, proceeding by juxtaposition of examples rather than logical demonstration. It assembles a constellation of texts, practices, and scenes and shows their family resemblances and productive differences.

"On Theory-Fiction" moves through ten named sections, each making a partial claim about what theory-fiction is and does. The structure is additive, not dialectical: each section adds a further dimension (dramatisation; cybernetic feedback loops; hyperstition and nesting; ethnopoetics/ecopoetics; non-human perspectives; outside optics and critical fabulation; multimodal registers; formal devices; performative texts; sites of production). Key argumentative move: Sadie Plant's *Zeros and Ones* introduces the idea of Science Fiction as retroactive reality-production ("the present was being reeled into a future which had always been guiding the past"). This is deepened through the Ccru's hyperstition concept and radicalised in the claim that some theory-fiction involves "a strange retro-causality where the fiction works back on its conditions of emergence."

"On Autofiction and Autotheory" proceeds by the "mirrors and prisms" figure: autowriting functions simultaneously as a mirror (producing recursive self-examination) and as a prism (diffracting perspectives onto other lives, making marked bodies visible). O'Sullivan warns against the purely proprietary reading that would collapse it back into liberal individualism.

"On Art Writing" proceeds by precursor-tracing (Conceptual Art, Dada, Burroughs, Ballard, Smithson) and then by a series of propositions about what makes art writing distinctive: its pitch and theatricality; its performative scripts, spells and voice; its use of non-standard registers; its embeddedness in scenes; its function as minor genre for marked bodies.

## 6. Key concepts

**Theory-fiction:** Writing that blurs the boundary between theory and fiction, or uses fiction as a method for theoretical work. Not a single genre but a family of practices including cybernetic theory-fiction (Plant, Land, Ccru), ecopoetic theory-fiction (Haraway, Tsing), critical fabulation (Hartman), sonic fiction (Eshun), and art writing that leans toward theory. Attributed primarily to Fisher and the Ccru scene but mapped much more widely.

**Fictioning:** O'Sullivan's earlier coinage (co-authored with David Burrows, *Fictioning*, 2019). Denotes treating theoretical or artistic material in a staged or fictional mode — a "fictioning of materials" that gives them a performative character. Used as a verb: to "fiction" a scene, a landscape, a past or future.

**Hyperstition:** Ccru concept, defined as "fictions that make themselves real." More precisely, reality is composed of fictions — "consistent semiotic terrains" — and hyperstitions actualise already-present virtualities by inserting themselves into those terrains. Distinguished from mere fiction by its causal efficacy. O'Sullivan connects it to Stengers on animism, Guattari on "fabulous images," and the claim that performative writing can transform reality.

**Fiction of the self:** O'Sullivan's coinage. Designates the idea that the self is already a fiction — a construction, a performance — and that autofiction stages this explicitly rather than taking self-hood for granted.

**Minor literature / minor writing:** Drawn from Deleuze and Guattari's *Kafka* (1986). O'Sullivan applies their three criteria — deterritorialisation of major language; connection to larger political circuits; collective enunciation — to theory-fiction, autofiction, and art writing. Minor does not mean small but designates writing that operates from inside a major language while working against its logics.

**Dramatisation:** Borrowed from Deleuze. Theory-fiction dramatises concepts — gives them characters, scenarios, and narratives — so they can be "lived." Contrasted with the abstract presentation of concepts in standard philosophical writing.

**Philo-fictions:** Laruelle's term from his non-philosophy project. Concepts "untethered" from their truth-value and overview function, repurposed as materials in fictional operations.

**Critical fabulation:** Saidiya Hartman's method of using fiction to explore lives obscured by dominant historical archives. Treated as a limit case of theory-fiction where fiction has a constitutive archival and political function.

**Para-academia:** Spaces of writing, publication, and thought outside standard academic institutions — blogs, zines, small presses — that produce work of theoretical seriousness. Treated as a condition of possibility for theory-fiction and art writing.

**Performative texts / performance fiction:** Writing that performs its content — where style, syntax, structure, and presentation contribute directly to the theoretical machinery. Examples: Eshun's *More Brilliant Than the Sun*; *The Undercommons*; Cixous's "Laugh of the Medusa."

**Nesting / nested fictions:** Following the Ccru's "Lemurian Time War" and Wittig's *Les Guérillières*: fictions within fictions within fictions. Allows each fiction to be seen as a fiction and ultimately one's own reality as one fiction among others. A vehicle for trafficking in "semiotic contraband."

**Sympoiesis:** Haraway's term (from *Staying with the Trouble*, 2016) for collective making — making-together across species and agencies. Haraway's "Camille Stories" is treated as the founding text of a new sub-genre of theory-fiction concerned with multi-species storytelling, explicitly counterposed to Promethean/accelerationist stories.

**Machine writing / AI writing:** Texts produced in collaboration with AI language systems (K Allado-McDowell's *Pharmako AI* is the primary example). Framed as the logical extension of theory-fiction's long interest in disrupting singular authorship, cybernetic feedback loops, and the non-human.

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Deliberately and explicitly open.** O'Sullivan offers four propositions rather than theses: (i) writing across and against genre; (ii) writing that performs its content; (iii) writing, method and collaboration; (iv) writing for communities to come. Each is framed as provisional. The strongest normative claim — that these genres constitute a "democratisation of theory" and open "a space or field of the commons" — is not qualified. But even this is attached to a scene-specific and situated account: O'Sullivan knows he is speaking primarily from London in 2024, from inside the Goldsmiths/Urbanomic/Book Works orbit.

The coda to "On Theory-Fiction" is meta-reflexive in a personal register: O'Sullivan recounts his own formative experience at the Virtual Futures conferences at Warwick in 1995–96, the rave culture/academic theory synthesis he encountered there. This self-implication in the genealogy he is constructing is itself a mild form of the autowriting he theorises.

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- **Geographical and scenic partiality:** "My account is partial and dictated by my own location in London and the UK and familiarity with a certain scene of writing." Other trajectories are possible.
- **The art-writing essay is the most exploratory:** "Even more so than the previous essays, what follows is a sampling of an ever-growing and very amorphous field."
- **The Ccru genealogy may over-determine the theory-fiction account:** O'Sullivan introduces the corrective — Haraway's feminist sympoiesis, Hartman's critical fabulation, decolonial art writing — as responses to the Promethean character of cybernetic theory-fiction.
- **The autowriting essay does not cover all relevant fields**, particularly where autofiction intersects with English literature as a discipline.
- **Class and intersectionality receive limited treatment:** O'Sullivan acknowledges working-class writing and art writing intersect in ways he cannot fully map.
- **Machine writing is a postscript, not a main essay:** Explicitly described as "on a slight tangent."

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**CCRU and hyperstition:** O'Sullivan provides the most detailed sustained academic mapping of the Ccru as a theory-fiction scene available in this format. He quotes their definition of hyperstition directly, explicates the "Lemurian Time War" essay's nested fictions logic, and connects the Ccru to both predecessors (Burroughs, Ballard, Plant, Land) and successors (Negarestani's *Cyclonopedia*, Fisher's *Flatline Constructs*, Eshun's *More Brilliant Than the Sun*). Key secondary source for any corpus paper that takes up the CCRU or hyperstition.

**Mark Fisher:** Fisher appears as the pivotal figure in the transition from the Ccru's academic/para-academic hybrid scene to the blog scene and then to mainstream critical theory publishing. O'Sullivan credits Fisher with coining "theory-fiction" in his 1999 PhD (*Flatline Constructs: Gothic Materialism and Cybernetic Theory-Fiction*).

**Accelerationism:** O'Sullivan is explicitly critical of Promethean and accelerationist readings of the Ccru (he references the *#Accelerate* reader directly). Haraway is used as a counter-figure offering "a different take on theory-fiction to, for example, the cybernetic one of the Ccru."

**Negarestani's *Cyclonopedia*:** Treated as "perhaps the key work of theory-fiction" — the most complete realisation of what the genre can be. Its density, use of diagrams, breadth of conceptual invention, and future-arriving temporal structure are all cited.

**Decolonial and feminist theory-fiction:** Extensive engagement with Hartman, Sharpe, Gumbs, Black Quantum Futurism, and the decolonial potential of art writing.

**Plant / Land dyad:** Carefully balanced. Plant's *Zeros and Ones* is credited as one of the first contemporary works of theory-fiction; Land's essays are engaged genealogically without endorsing subsequent political directions.
