# Victor Pelevin and the Aesthetics of Neoreaction

**Author:** Vinokour, Maya
**Published in:** *The Russian Review*, Vol. 82, No. 4 (October 2023), pp. 631–648
**DOI:** 10.1111/russ.12526
**Item Key:** PRTZQB4U

---

## 1. Paper type detected

**Type 3a — Single author/corpus as philosophical and political mirror (immanent critique via cultural parable, constitutive case).**

This is a normative, theoretical-interpretive paper in critical cultural and media studies. The classification as Type 3a is justified because Pelevin's fictional corpus is not brought in to illustrate a pre-formed argument about neoreaction — it is the site where the argument is generated. The paper's central concept, the "faux pill," is derived from sustained engagement with Pelevin's narrative technique; without reading Pelevin closely, the distinction between faux-pilling, red-pilling, blue-pilling, and clown-pilling cannot be articulated. The cultural case does double work: it is simultaneously the object of immanent critique (showing how Pelevin's technique is structurally complicit with the NRx discourse it ostensibly parodies) and the generative source of the paper's normative argument (about the depoliticising function of ironic-ideological discourse more broadly). The paper also has a secondary genealogical function, tracing the intellectual lineages of NRx (Land, Moldbug, Yarvin), but this genealogical reconstruction is in service of the case analysis rather than autonomous.

Epistemic mode: normative with interpretive case.

---

## 2. Move sequence

`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → genealogical_reconstruction → case_introduction → case_development → immanent_critique (embedded) → conceptual_distinction → normative_derivation → conclusion_strong`

Step by step:

1. **puzzle_diagnosis:** The problem of Pelevin's "apparently serious, apparently parodic" engagement with NRx ideology — epistemic undecidability as the opening problem.
2. **rival_mapping:** Two inadequate framings implicitly rejected: (a) Pelevin as satirist unmasking power; (b) "clownpilling" reading of his nihilism as apolitical catharsis.
3. **genealogical_reconstruction:** Intellectual genealogy of NRx — Moldbug/Yarvin's neocameralism, Land's accelerationism, the CCRU, the Singularity, Roko's basilisk, Silicon Valley transhumanism — and their shared aesthetic of horror and conspiracy.
4. **case_introduction:** Pelevin's oeuvre introduced via its "master plot" structure (the red-pill protagonist who receives a revelation that increases rather than reduces entropy) and its evolution from Soviet-specific satire toward "globally legible far-right discourse."
5. **case_development:** Extended close readings of *S.N.U.F.F.* (Kaia's speech on LGBTQIA+ rights as crypto-orgasm conspiracy), *The Lamp of Methuselah* (reptiloid feminists enforcing "inclusive" language via telepathic torture), *The Art of Light Touch* (secret Nazis behind Rainbow liberalism), *Undefeatable Sun* (Sasha's cold shower as forced blue-pilling).
6. **immanent_critique (embedded):** The case development simultaneously shows that Pelevin's mockery, far from subverting far-right discourse, structurally replicates and amplifies it. His stiob — unlike late-Soviet stiob — is "additive, projecting mockery onto its target and changing it in the process."
7. **conceptual_distinction:** The four pills: red (revolutionary revelation), blue (false consciousness), honk/clown (cheerful nihilism), **faux** (Pelevin's own — a trollish remedy that forecloses both revelation and catharsis, leaving the reader in a void).
8. **normative_derivation:** The faux pill is connected to "neutrollization" (Kurowska and Reshetnikov) — the Kremlin-developed technique of flooding information space to make meaningful political communication impossible. Pelevin's fiction "actually participates in 'neutrollization,' producing a stealth ideology that, like the stiob of late-socialist countercultures, is easily co-opted by the state."
9. **conclusion_strong:** Clear normative verdict: Pelevin is not an ironic bystander but an active contributor to a global process of political depoliticisation.

## 3. Puzzle or research question

The central puzzle is ideological undecidability: Pelevin's recent fiction adopts the terminology, conceptual apparatus, and aesthetic sensibility of Anglo-American NRx discourse with apparent fidelity — yet the adoption is "apparently parodic." Is Pelevin mocking NRx from the outside, inhabiting it from the inside, or doing something else entirely that the parodic/sincere binary cannot capture? The secondary question is political: what are the real-world effects of fiction that traffics in far-right ideology under a veil of irony?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. Structural convergence between Pelevin and NRx.** Despite occupying "seemingly disjoint" linguistic and cultural spaces, Pelevin and Land/Moldbug share: (1) a conception of political space governed by quasi-supernatural, horrorcore forces (the Cathedral, Ishtar, reptiloids, Chekists, Freemasons, gargoyles, vampires — all structurally equivalent); (2) a politics of "passivism" — the conviction that intervention in politics is futile; (3) an aesthetics of conspiracy that is pleasurably self-reinforcing rather than explanatorily powerful; (4) a deployment of far-right bugaboos (political correctness, feminism, LGBTQIA+ rights, cultural Marxism) in ways that are graphic, parodic, and functionally indistinct from sincere far-right polemic.

**b. The faux pill as Pelevin's specific contribution.** Unlike the red pill (which promises revelation) or the honk pill (which offers temporary nihilistic catharsis), the faux pill is an epistemically corrosive device. It "spins elaborate, yet hollow, conspiracy theories and forces his readers to 'discover' their hollowness alongside his characters," demonstrating "the impossibility — or futility — of assembling any unified theory of the world." The reader cannot settle into either satirical distance (the constructs are too immersive) or revelation (the revelations are too obviously hollow). The result: "the only logical position is one of total cynicism or indifference."

**c. Stiob as additive rather than subtractive.** Classical satire is "subtractive" — it unmasks, deflates. Pelevin's stiob is "additive": it projects mockery onto its targets in a way that endows them with properties they did not previously possess. This aligns with "meme magic" — the far-right troll's capacity to generate real-world effects through ironic discourse. "It all starts with culture" — the Soviet state was "fucked not by flying rockets" but by "jeans and rock music."

**d. Neutrollization as structural context.** Pelevin's technique is situated within "neutrollization" (Kurowska and Reshetnikov) — the strategy of filling information space with noise to fuel "the population's resentment of political engagement." Pelevin's novels are "not merely depicting but actually participating in" this process.

**e. The internationalisation of Pelevin's far-right vocabulary is strategic.** His recent novels deploy a dense international online vernacular — "snowflakes," "woke," "red/blue pill," "human biodiversity," "clown world," "#metoo" — "with a fluency bespeaking many hours on the Anglophone Internet." This is read as calculated repositioning from a locally specific post-Soviet satirist to a "globally legible far-right discourse-monger."

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Immanent critique via close reading embedded in genealogical reconstruction.** Vinokour does not argue against Pelevin from an external normative position; she reads his own texts against his own stated posture as a "perpetual ironist" and shows that the ironic posture cannot be sustained under analysis.

The methodological hallmark is **comparative contextualisation**: Pelevin's fictional devices (the red pill protagonist, the conspiracy cosmology, the literalised horror metaphor) are set beside structurally equivalent devices in Land's and Moldbug's essays, with extended quotation from both. The comparison is not merely thematic but functional — it shows that Pelevin and NRx perform the same rhetorical and political work.

A secondary method is **conceptual distinction-making**: the typology of "pills" (red, blue, honk/clown, faux) is derived from careful attention to what Pelevin's texts actually do to readers rather than what they claim to do. This is the paper's strongest analytical contribution.

## 6. Key concepts

**Neoreaction / NRx / Dark Enlightenment:** A far-right intellectual movement originating in Moldbug/Yarvin's 2007 blog *Unqualified Reservations*. Core commitments: opposition to democracy ("doom itself"), advocacy for neocameralism (states as businesses that own countries), anti-egalitarianism, support for "patchworks" of competing city-states. Nick Land's accelerationism adds the thesis that capitalism must be pushed to its dystopian limit.

**The Cathedral:** Moldbug's term for the putative alliance of the progressive state, elite American universities, and mainstream press. NRx's primary ideological enemy. Pelevin's rotating cast of occult controllers (Freemasons, Chekists, reptiloids, Ishtar-worshippers) functions as a fictional equivalent.

**Stiob:** A late-Soviet and post-Soviet mode of irony characterised by hyper-identification with a target discourse combined with decontextualisation. Unlike satire, stiob does not unmask or deflate; it inhabits the target discourse so completely that mockery and sincerity become indistinguishable. Vinokour's key argument: detached from a single stable target (Soviet ideology), stiob has become "available to anyone capable of parodically appropriating a locally hegemonic discourse."

**The faux pill:** Vinokour's coinage for Pelevin's specific narrative device. A trollish remedy that generates the phenomenology of revelation while systematically evacuating any content from that revelation. The reader ends up not with enlightenment or catharsis but with "the void within his constructs." Politically produces the same depoliticising effect as neutrollization.

**The honk / clown pill:** Far-right internet terminology for "cheerful nihilism" — acknowledging that "clown world" is absurd while choosing laughter over despair. Contrasted with the faux pill: the honk pill offers temporary catharsis; the faux pill forecloses even that.

**Neutrollization:** Kurowska and Reshetnikov's term for the industrialised troll-farm strategy: not promoting any specific position but flooding information space with noise to "fundamentally sabotage the ability of an ordinary internet user to separate truth from fiction," thereby depoliticising the population.

**Meme magic:** Far-right internet concept that ironic symbolic production has real-world causal power — that "politics is downstream from culture." Used by Vinokour to argue that Pelevin's ironic deployment of far-right tropes contributes to their actual circulation and potency, regardless of authorial intent.

**Accelerationism:** Land's thesis that capitalism must be accelerated toward its dystopian limit rather than reformed or dismantled. "Through the acceleration of global capitalism, the human will be dissolved in a technological apotheosis, effectively experiencing a species-wide suicide as the ultimate stimulant head rush."

**Passivism:** Moldbug's strategic recommendation for NRx adherents: reject any involvement in political activity. "He would rather drink his own piss, than shift public opinion." Corresponds structurally to Pelevin's protagonists, who uniformly discover that "intervention in local or global politics is entirely pointless."

**Roko's basilisk:** An argument that a future superintelligent AI would torture all those who knew of it but failed to help bring it into existence. "A basilisk in this context is any information that harms or endangers the people who hear it." Used to illustrate NRx's attraction to horror and catastrophist aesthetics.

**Post-Soviet Gothic** (Khapaeva): An aesthetics characterised by "the banishment of both rationality and the human being" — ghosts, vampires, werewolves populating a cyberpunk landscape. Pelevin's oeuvre is a central instance.

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Strong and normatively committed.** Vinokour does not retreat to interpretive openness. Her final verdict: Pelevin is not an ironic bystander but an active participant in a global process of political depoliticisation. "As a writer of fiction, Pelevin not only depicts, but actually participates in 'neutrollization,' producing a stealth ideology that, like the stiob of late-socialist countercultures, is easily co-opted by the state."

The rhetorical move: stiob critique turned against stiob itself. By taking Pelevin "seriously precisely as a perpetual ironist," Vinokour shows that the ironist's posture is not an escape from ideology but a form of it. "Pelevin, then, is an edgelord without edge, lording it over a discursive space whose complexity is inversely proportional to its explanatory power." No qualification is offered.

The one implicit openness: Vinokour gestures at the connection between Pelevin and "rashism" (Russian chauvinism post-Ukraine invasion) but does not develop this, leaving the specific question of Pelevin's relationship to Putinism relatively underdetermined.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- **Methodological choice:** Vinokour explicitly does not attempt to determine Pelevin's "true" ideological identity — the essay "takes him seriously precisely as a perpetual ironist," setting aside the forensic question of whether his NRx appropriation is sincere or parodic. This means the paper cannot fully answer questions of causal mechanism or political responsibility.
- **Reliance on a single reading of "neutrollization"** (Kurowska and Reshetnikov 2018) to contextualise a decade-spanning body of fiction.
- **The Overton window drift:** Notes that categories previously coded as "neutral" have migrated into right-wing discursive space in Russia since 1991 — a question that goes to the core claim but is not systematically pursued.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Accelerationism and NRx intellectual genealogy:** One of the more accessible genealogical accounts of Land-Moldbug NRx in this corpus, covering the CCRU's founding at Warwick (1995), Land's "Dark Enlightenment" essay (2012), Moldbug's neocameralism, and the Singularity connection to Silicon Valley. Directly in dialogue with O'Sullivan's accelerationism paper (which addresses the same terrain from a constructive rather than diagnostic angle) and with Balić's CCRU paper.

**Theory-fiction and hyperstition:** Land's work is inseparable from the practice of theory-fiction — writing that deliberately blurs the boundary between philosophical argument and speculative fiction. Pelevin functions as an external, non-Anglophone instantiation of the same dynamic: fiction that generates ideological effects through the machinery of irony and undecidability. The concept of "hyperstition" (ideas that make themselves real through belief) is structurally equivalent to what Vinokour calls "meme magic" and the "faux pill."

**Stiob and post-Soviet irony:** The paper's engagement with stiob connects to Boyer and Yurchak's "American Stiob" (2010), Lipovetsky's work on "inverted political correctness," and Engström's analysis of "post-irony" in contemporary Russian right-wing aesthetics.

**Post-truth and online political discourse:** Vinokour's use of neutrollization connects to the broader literature on disinformation, conspiracy theory, and the political epistemology of social media — relevant to any corpus paper on QAnon-style epistemic communities or post-truth discourse.

**The Bhatt 2020 structural parallel:** Bhatt's "White Extinction: Metaphysical Elements of Contemporary Western Fascism" (Type 3b in the TCS/PSC typology) similarly reads a far-right philosophical corpus to expose its internal logic. Both papers use Deleuzian vocabulary of minor/major becoming as normative resource. The key difference: Bhatt's case is purely conceptual-historical; Vinokour's triangulates between intellectual genealogy, literary analysis, and media theory.

**Far-right utopias (Crone 2024):** Crone's special-issue introduction (also in this corpus) addresses contemporary far-right utopianism from a political science and ethnographic angle. Vinokour's paper complements it by showing how far-right ideology circulates aesthetically and literarily, through fiction and irony rather than only through programme and practice.
