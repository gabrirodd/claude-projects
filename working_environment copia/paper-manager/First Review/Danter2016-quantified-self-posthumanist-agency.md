# Danter, Reichardt & Schober (2016) — Theorising the Quantified Self and Posthumanist Agency

**Move sequence:** `puzzle_diagnosis → genealogical_reconstruction [QS in American culture] → rival_mapping [posthumanism, economy-driven subjectivity, knowledge cultures] → framework_proposal → case_introduction [Shteyngart] → case_development → normative_derivation → conclusion_open`

---

## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens with the puzzle (what are the cultural implications of QS technology?) and immediately performs a genealogical reconstruction tracing the quantified self back to US-American cultural specificity — Benjamin Franklin, individualism, techno-euphoric self-optimisation. This reconstruction is the primary argumentative engine (Type 2b). Three theoretical frameworks (economy-driven subjectivity, posthumanism, information-age knowledge cultures) are then mapped and synthesised. Shteyngart's novel is introduced as a constitutive cultural case (Type 3a): it functions as an epistemological counter-model, not mere illustration — the paper's claim about fiction-as-alternative could not be made without close engagement with the text. The conclusion is open, calling for comparative extension.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

What are the cultural implications of quantified self technology? How do contemporary US-American novels reflect on and critically contest the qualitative changes to the human condition produced by numerical self-description? And is QS a genuinely global phenomenon, or does it emerge from a specifically American model of subjectivity — and if the latter, what are its historical roots?

---

## 4. Core argument

Two interlocking arguments:

**The genealogical claim**: The quantified self emerges from an historically specific US-American model of subjectivity rooted at least since Franklin's *Autobiography* (1791). That model emphasises individualism, economic self-optimisation, and a techno-euphoric belief in self-control and self-possession. The paper connects QS to three theoretical discourses: (1) economy-driven subjectivity (the neoliberal self as entrepreneur of the self); (2) posthumanism (QS as instantiation of Hayles's informational posthuman — pattern over presence, data over embodiment); (3) knowledge cultures of the information age (Manovich's database logic — relational, modular, combinatory — as dominant episteme). The QS is not a new invention but a contemporary expression of a deep structure of American subjectivity.

**The literary counter-model claim**: Fictional texts function as "critical systems of second-order observation" (Luhmann): they observe and comment on first-order social practices like QS. Shteyngart's *Super Sad True Love Story* constitutes an epistemological counter-model by foregrounding the *qualitative* dimension of human experience — what is lost, distorted, or rendered invisible by database logic. In doing so, fiction (re-)inscribes human agency into technologies that would otherwise eliminate it. The "technologies of the self" (Foucault) are recuperated as sites of resistance. The novel's formal choices — app-like data streams alongside intimate diary entries — reproduce the tension between database logic and qualitative experience at the level of narrative form, not only content.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Two-stage: (1) theoretical mapping — constructing the conceptual scaffold (neoliberal subjectivity, posthumanism, knowledge cultures) through engagement with Franklin, Hayles, Manovich, Foucault; (2) close reading of *Super Sad True Love Story* as a case that enacts, challenges, and reimagines the QS subject. The argumentative move is to show that the novel's formal choices reproduce the tension between database logic and qualitative experience at the level of narrative structure — the argument is about form as well as content. No empirical data on QS users or practices.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Quantified self (QS)**: the practice of tracking bodily and behavioural data for self-knowledge and optimisation
- **Technologies of the self** (Foucault 1984): practices by which subjects act on themselves to transform or understand themselves — applied to QS as both discipline and potential resistance
- **Posthuman** (Hayles): the human as informational pattern rather than embodied presence; pattern over presence; QS as one instantiation
- **Database logic** (Manovich): relational, modular, combinatory organisation of information characteristic of digital culture, privileging paradigmatic over syntagmatic structure (lists over narratives)
- **Second-order observation** (Luhmann): observation of observations — the capacity of literary systems to observe and comment on first-order social practices
- **Economy-driven subjectivity**: the neoliberal construction of the self as human capital to be perpetually invested in and optimised
- **Distributed agency**: agency spread across human and non-human actors rather than located in individual intention

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately assertive. The genealogical claim — QS as distinctively American — is argued with historical specificity and is the paper's most original contribution. The claim that literary fiction constitutes a genuine epistemological alternative (not just critique) is advanced with confidence but relies on the strength of the Shteyngart reading. The conclusion is open: the paper calls for cross-national comparison of QS literatures and further examination of how fictional forms negotiate the database/narrative tension in different cultural contexts.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

Primarily implicit. The analysis is confined to one novel and one national tradition. The genealogical line from Franklin to contemporary QS involves significant historical compression. The paper does not engage empirically with actual QS users, which would add evidence for or against the cultural specificity claim.

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Connects to **Whitehead & Hannah (2025)**: Danter et al. argue for qualitative experience as a counter to database logic; Whitehead & Hannah's SenseMaker® findings show people experience digital freedom as exactly this kind of embodied, qualitative negotiation — supporting the literary argument empirically.
- The posthumanism debate connects to **Niu (2008)**, who proposes the "post-posthuman" as a counter to Hayles's disembodied posthuman — mapping onto the literary resistance Danter et al. identify in Shteyngart.
- The American cultural specificity argument complements **Paris (2018)** and **Amaral et al. (2025)** on Silicon Valley as the source of technocratic ideology that travels globally.
