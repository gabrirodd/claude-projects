# How to Do Philosophy with Sci-Fiction: A Case of Hybrid Textuality

## 1. Paper type detected

**Type 3a — Immanent critique via cultural parable (single constitutive text as philosophical demonstration).**

Epistemic mode: normative with interpretive case. The paper is primarily theoretical and normative, in the tradition of metaphilosophy and media philosophy. Its thesis — that fiction can itself constitute philosophical academic work, and that the hegemony of the paper-format imposes a pathological cognitive and expressive closure on the discipline — is argued primarily through close analysis of a specific book, *The Clouds* (Gualeni 2023), which functions not merely as illustration but as the performative realisation and evidentiary ground of the argument. The case is constitutive: the argument for hybrid textuality cannot be reconstructed without engaging the structure of *The Clouds* itself.

**Conflict of interest note:** The primary case study (*The Clouds*) is co-authored by one of the paper's own authors (Gualeni). This is not acknowledged in the text.

## 3. Puzzle or research question

The puzzle is a paradox in the relationship between philosophy and fiction. There is historically rich and institutionally acknowledged cross-fertilisation between philosophy and literary/science fiction — Plato's dialogues, Sartre's novels, Le Guin's speculative fiction, theory-fiction texts like Negarestani's *Cyclonopedia* — yet the current institutional paradigm of academic philosophy refuses to recognise fiction as genuine philosophical work. Works of fiction may be "incidentally philosophical," but they are not treated as "an academic philosophical alternative." The question is: how did this asymmetry arise, and is it philosophically defensible?

The puzzle is grounded in a "media oblivion": "philosophers forget to ask which are the expressive and technical media that support their own everyday practices." They assume the paper-format without subjecting this assumption to the same critical scrutiny they apply to every other philosophical presupposition.

## 4. Core argument

**Layer 1: The institutional pathology of paper-format hegemony.** The professionalisation of philosophy has produced a "striking homogeneity" in which philosophical writing coincides exclusively with the paper-format — defined as "a short and impersonal report of limited results whose potential audience of readers is thinned almost to nothingness." Papers are "designed for a severely restricted readership, composed of those disembodied professional consciences who want to be informed (rather than participate in a reading experience)." Two interlinked risks:

- *The severing of content from form.* The ideas of Plato, Spinoza, Nietzsche, and Wittgenstein are "uniquely tied to the form in which they were originally presented and would not be the same if they were written in today's conventional, linear, passive, and impersonal prose." Nussbaum is cited: "literary form is not separable from philosophical content, but is, itself, a part of content."

- *A retroactive 'philosophical cancel culture.'* The hegemony of the paper-format retroactively delegitimises the great plurality of philosophical genres in the tradition (dialogues, fragments, aphorisms, confessions, systematico-geometric proofs), risking making philosophy "analphabetic as a discipline."

The authors are not arguing for the *replacement* of the paper by fiction. Their position is inclusive pluralism: institutionalisation must balance "standardisation or 'normal science' with creativity or 'breaking science'" to avoid producing "episodes of effective epistemic injustice."

**Layer 2: Fiction's specific philosophical affordances.** Drawing on Walton's make-believe theory, Ricoeur's narratology, and extended-mind cognitive theory, three affordances are identified:

- *Constitutive incompleteness.* Fiction is inevitably "poor" (Blanchot): it presents "facts, characters, and events in an incomplete manner." This poverty invites the reader "to develop their own imaginative relationship with the work" and "to develop hypotheses concerning the fictional situation." Philosophers using fiction "can deliberately choose to harness these ambiguities and gaps to force the audience into actively speculative stances."

- *Subjective and embodied cognition.* Fiction actualises philosophical ideas as "experienced fictional truths" rather than presenting them "in a disembodied and abstract fashion." Ricoeur: reading fiction "is a way of living in the fictitious universe of the work."

- *Resistance to cognitive closure.* Philosophical fiction "is not bound to a prescribed conclusion and downplays the ideal of 'cognitive closure': a general aversion to ambiguity and the tendency to remain impervious to additional information" (Djikic, Oatley and Moldoveanu 2013). Fiction's open-endedness is the corrective to the close-mindedness that the paper-format institutionally enforces.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Metaphilosophical media analysis combined with constitutive case-study.** The authors deploy media theory (Kittler, Kress and van Leeuwen's multimodality, Mersch's visual epistemology) to defamiliarise the paper as neutral container, and then turn to *The Clouds* as both a case study demonstrating the argument and a performative enactment of it. The case is not illustrative but generative: *The Clouds* is what hybrid textuality looks like in practice, and the argument about what hybrid textuality can do philosophically is inseparable from the description of how *The Clouds* actually does it.

The method also involves sustained counter-objection management. Two predictable objections are pre-emptively addressed: (a) that defending fiction-as-philosophy entails rejecting the paper, and (b) that professionalisation itself is the villain. Both are refused: the position is explicitly pluralist and the tone is conciliatory toward institutional constraints.

## 6. Key concepts

**Media oblivion** (following Kittler and Mersch): The tendency of philosophers to overlook the mediatic conditions of their own practice — specifically, the unthematised assumption that the paper-format is the transparent, neutral vehicle of philosophical thought rather than itself a medium with specific affordances and constraints. The pivot of the puzzle_diagnosis.

**Paper-format hegemony / communicative monomodality:** The institutionally enforced coincidence of philosophical work with the academic paper — "a short and impersonal report of limited results" designed for "angelic readers" who want to be "informed rather than participate in a reading experience." Term "monomodality" drawn from Kress and van Leeuwen. Two levels: "International Affairs" (exclusion of non-written media) and "Internal Politics" (exclusion of non-paper written genres).

**Philosophical cancel culture:** The retroactive delegitimisation of historically diverse modes of philosophical writing — Plato's dialogues, Spinoza's geometric method, Nietzsche's aphorisms — when the paper-format is treated as the perennial standard. A culture in which colleagues complain that Plato "could have written in a plain and straightforward manner."

**Cognitive closure** (Djikic, Oatley and Moldoveanu): "A general aversion to ambiguity and the tendency to remain impervious to additional information." The principal epistemic pathology attributed to the paper-format. Philosophical fiction is argued to be a corrective to cognitive closure because it does not prescribe a conclusion and actively exploits ambiguity.

**Hybrid textuality:** The core positive proposal. A written work combining, in a single volume written by the same author, multiple registers — narrative fiction, theoretical essays, and philosophical meta-commentary — each discussing the same philosophical ideas in "an interconnected and self-referential fashion." Extends the existing notion of "theory-fiction" but specifies a three-layer structure rather than a fusion.

**Fictive incompleteness / poverty of fiction** (Blanchot): The constitutive lack of detail in any fictional world — it "inevitably presents facts, characters, and events in an incomplete manner." Revalued as a philosophical affordance: gaps invite the reader to actively construct the fictional scenario and adopt speculative stances.

**Value capture** (Nguyen 2024): When "pregiven and standardised institutional criteria (e.g. succeeding in metrics such as citation rates, rankings, etc.) undermine the personal motivations for embarking on a professional career." Used to motivate the critique of professionalisation's effects on philosophical freedom without condemning professionalisation as such.

**The Clouds** (Gualeni 2023): The science-fiction novella at the centre of the case analysis. A philosophical sci-fi novella about Carla Mikkelsen, a climate scientist who discovers she inhabits a simulated world that can be edited and rolled back by its creators. Themes include fictioning, simulation, theodicy for simulated universes, and the "unnatural fictional trope of unhappening." Structured as: eight fictional chapters + three theoretical essays + a philosophical afterword (meta-commentary).

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Explicitly open and pluralist.** The authors resist overclaiming: "we are not hailing hybrid textuality as a synthesis of all that is good about philosophical mediation. Rather, we discussed it as an expressive possibility and a communicative strategy among many, and one whose efficiency and desirability obviously depends on its use amidst a number of contextual factors and that — to this day — remains largely undertheorized and underexplored."

The theoretical conclusion from Layer 1 is stronger: the paper_format-as-only-legitimate-philosophical-medium is indefensible given the history of philosophy and the mediatic conditions of philosophical practice. This claim is stated with conviction and not qualified. The constructive claim (hybrid textuality as an alternative) is explicitly provisional.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- **Hybrid textuality "remains largely undertheorized and underexplored"** — the paper has at most demonstrated that it is possible and philosophically advantageous, without providing a systematic account of when it is most appropriate, what its limits are, or how it should be evaluated institutionally.
- **Conflict of interest:** The primary case study (*The Clouds*) is co-authored by one of the paper's own authors (Gualeni). Not acknowledged in the text.
- **Under-engagement with objections:** The claim that fiction's emotional effects enhance rather than bias philosophical reasoning is asserted via Ricoeur, Walton, and extended-mind theory but not defended against substantive objections (that fiction is epistemically irresponsible, that thought experiments are already sufficient for non-discursive speculation).

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Theory-fiction and the CCRU:** The paper shares the interest in fiction as a philosophical method with multiple papers in this corpus — O'Sullivan's *On Theory-Fiction*, the Balić CCRU paper, and Kietzman's analysis of Neuromancer as a philosophical text. Pezzano and Gualeni are making a *metaphilosophical* argument about institutional legitimacy that the other papers presuppose but do not explicitly defend.

**Ricoeur and narrative:** The use of Ricoeur's narratology — specifically that reading fiction "is a way of living in the fictitious universe of the work" and that narrative actualises philosophical ideas as "experienced fictional truths" — connects this paper to any corpus paper that deploys Ricoeur (e.g. Whitehead 2025 on Ricoeur, freedom, and digital society, in the other collection under review).

**Science fiction as philosophical resource:** The paper provides an explicit meta-level framework for understanding what papers like Kietzman (Neuromancer / Eden and Utopia), Dalton (Neuromancer / Heterotopia), Vinokour (Pelevin / neoreaction), and Young 2025 (video games / AI ethics) are doing when they use SF narratives as philosophical cases.

**Fisher and para-academic writing:** The concern with the institutional constraints on philosophical writing connects to Balić's account of Fisher's blogging as a way of "tricking" oneself into serious philosophical work outside the academy. Both papers touch the same zone — the costs and possibilities of para-academic philosophical writing — though from different disciplinary perspectives.
