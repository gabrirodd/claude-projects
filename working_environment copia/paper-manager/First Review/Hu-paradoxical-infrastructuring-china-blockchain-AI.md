# Hu, Zichen (n.d.) — Paradoxical Infrastructuring: Genealogies of Governance and "Art of Being Governed" in China's Blockchain–AI Hypes

## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens by diagnosing a puzzle in existing accounts of Chinese blockchain governance: the simple "innovation then repression" narrative fails to capture why blockchain's discursive legacy persists and feeds the AI build-out that followed the 2021 crypto ban. A theoretical framework — Foucauldian genealogy combined with Szonyi's "art of being governed" — is established before the case analysis, giving it Type 4 structure. But the genealogical reconstruction (tracing embedded historical layers of Cold War rationalities, socialist modernisation, internet utopianism) is primary and extensive, pulling it toward Type 2b. The case material (policy documents, media, fintech journalism, community discourse) is constitutive: without it, the concept of "paradoxical infrastructuring" cannot be grounded. Conclusion is open.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

How are imaginaries of decentralisation and sovereignty rearticulated through blockchain and AI infrastructures in China? How do state actors, intermediary platforms, and civil society navigate the tension between blockchain's libertarian/cryptographic affordances and the Chinese state's centralising imperatives? And why does this tension not resolve into simple repression — why does it instead generate new, internally contradictory governance formations?

The structural puzzle: China's blockchain boom did not simply end with the 2021 crypto ban, but fed into the AI infrastructure build-out that followed. This suggests structural continuity rather than break — and demands a framework for understanding how decentralising affordances are absorbed into centralising regimes.

---

## 4. Core argument

China's blockchain-to-AI trajectory is *paradoxical infrastructuring*: the decentralising affordances of blockchain (cryptographic trust, peer-to-peer ledgers, tokenomics) are alternately valorised, domesticated, and redeployed as building blocks within AI's centralising apparatus. Ideological contradiction between decentralisation's libertarian promise and authoritarian state control is not a failure or incoherence to be resolved — it is a *generative governance feature* that creates productive ambiguity exploited through *techno-nostalgic arbitrage*: intermediary actors (fintech companies, VC firms, state-linked enterprises) leverage blockchain's libertarian cultural capital to claim legitimacy in the AI space while operating within state-sanctioned centralised structures.

Szonyi's "art of being governed" accounts for non-state actors: they do not resist state authority but creatively inhabit its contradictions, manoeuvring within regulatory ambiguity rather than against it.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Foucauldian genealogy (tracing discursive sedimentations and ruptures, not searching for origins) combined with Szonyi's "art of being governed." The genealogical move shows that blockchain discourse in China carries multiple embedded, conflicting historical layers — Cold War rationalities, socialist modernisation narratives, 1990s internet utopianism — selectively activated and deactivated as political conditions shift. Empirically, the paper maps a heterogeneous corpus: state policy documents, party-state media, fintech journalism, VC white papers, crypto-community discourse. The analysis moves from macro-governmentality (state strategy, regulatory regimes) down to everyday intermediary practice and back, showing how both levels co-produce each other.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Paradoxical infrastructuring** (proposed): the process by which technically decentralising affordances are absorbed into centralising governance regimes, with the contradiction functioning as a productive feature rather than a failure
- **Governmentality** (Foucault): the rationalities and techniques through which subjects are governed and come to govern themselves
- **Art of being governed** (Szonyi): tactical navigation of state authority by inhabiting rather than resisting its contradictions — applied to Chinese platform actors and communities
- **Infrastructural imaginaries**: shared visions of what a technology can do that shape investment, policy, and community formation
- **Discursive assemblages**: heterogeneous collections of texts, practices, and actors coherent around a shared technological imaginary without being unified by a single logic
- **Infrastructural subjectivation**: the process by which technical infrastructure produces and shapes subject positions
- **Ghost infrastructure**: blockchain systems that continue to circulate as cultural reference points (in VC pitches, state rhetoric) after their technical operations have been banned or wound down
- **Techno-nostalgic arbitrage**: strategic use of blockchain's libertarian cultural capital to claim legitimacy in the post-blockchain, AI-dominated regulatory environment
- **Epistemic sovereignty**: the state's project of controlling the knowledge frameworks through which digital technologies are understood and governed

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately strong and theoretically assertive. The paper positions China not as a special case (authoritarian deviation from a liberal norm) but as "a particularly vivid instance" of a global condition in which decentralisation and centralisation are co-constitutive — a significant analytical claim. "Paradoxical infrastructuring" is proposed as generalisable beyond China to EU AI governance, US fintech regulation, etc. The conclusion ends on an open analytical horizon, calling for further empirical and comparative work.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- Internal opacity of Chinese bureaucratic deliberations makes it difficult to isolate causal mechanisms or attribute regulatory shifts to particular actors; the paper explicitly warns against such attributions
- Empirical corpus is entirely discursive (texts, media, reports), not ethnographic or interview-based — limits access to lived practice and the actual experience of navigating regulatory ambiguity
- Risk of drawing "direct equivalences" between premodern governance forms (Szonyi's historical context) and contemporary digital regulation
- Quantitative crypto-activity data are described as estimates with unverified precision

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Methodologically closest to **Amaral et al. (2025)**: both use Foucauldian genealogy to trace digital infrastructure's political economy, though Amaral focuses on Brazil/far-right and Hu on China's state/platform nexus.
- Complements **Pavlov et al. (n.d.)** on blockchain governance: where Pavlov analyses the protocol's internal power logic, Hu analyses the external political-regulatory environment.
- Connects to **Rieder & Simon (2015)** and **Paris (2018)** on platform monopolisation and governance limits, extending those arguments into a non-Western, state-capitalist context.
- "Ghost infrastructure" speaks to **Jaros (2019)**'s surveillance commoditism: both describe how a technology's cultural apparatus outlives its specific technical implementations.
