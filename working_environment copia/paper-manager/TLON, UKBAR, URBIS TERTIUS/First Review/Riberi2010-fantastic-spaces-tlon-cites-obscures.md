## 3. Puzzle or research question

The paper asks: what is the epistemological mechanism that makes Borges's "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" cognitively powerful as a fictional construct — and does the graphic novel series *Les Cités Obscures* by Schuiten and Peeters work by the same mechanism?

The underlying philosophical question: how can a fictional world that is explicitly not real produce genuine knowledge about our own world?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. "Tlön" deploys Vaihinger's 'as if' principle.** Borges's story grounds itself in the philosophical principle 'if idealism governs reality, then…' — operationalising Vaihinger's *Philosophie des Als Ob* (1911). The 'as if' allows Borges to construct an alternative world that functions as an apperceptive instrument: by contrasting Tlön with the real world, readers gain knowledge about the constitutive features of their own reality that they could not otherwise see. "Tlön is a possible world that mirrors our knowledge of the real world, but the remarkable trait of Tlön is that it can be found interwoven in the fabric of reality."

**b. Fictional incompleteness has cognitive value.** Following Vaihinger's account of schematic fictions, Riberi argues that Tlön — like all fictional worlds — operates by selective omission: "general types are deprived of unnecessary traits to make it easier to single out constituent elements." This is not a deficiency but the mechanism of cognition itself: "all cognition is the apperception of one thing through another." Fiction makes possible a comparative apperception that would be unavailable through direct inspection of reality.

**c. Representation is always already fictional.** The map-territory paradox (Borges's 1:1 map tale) demonstrates that territory can only ever be thought through representations, and those representations are themselves maps of maps — always provisional, always unable to reach the ultimate referent. Tlön's encyclopaedia threatens to disintegrate reality not because it is false but because it reveals that reality was already a construct — a fiction posited 'as if' it were ultimate reality.

**d. The Schuiten/Peeters series deploys the same mechanism.** *Les Cités Obscures* describes a world architecturally displaced from Brussels — "a displaced reflection of our world" — using the same Tlönian logic: selective extrapolation produces a recognisable world that directs attention to cultural practices, architectural styles, and history by "superimposing transparencies." Both works are "re-cognisable worlds" operating through contrast and apperceptive defamiliarisation.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Philosophical-literary hermeneutics supplemented by Vaihingerian epistemology.** Riberi reads "Tlön" through Vaihinger's *Philosophie des Als Ob* — a framework Borges explicitly invokes in the story itself ("The fact that every philosophy is by definition a dialectical game, a *Philosophie des Als Ob*"). He then uses the epistemological framework to generate the parallel with *Les Cités Obscures*, identifying common mechanisms (schematic fiction, selective omission, re-cognisable world, transparency superimposition). The argument is inductive and comparative rather than deductive.

The key geometric analogy from Vaihinger — that a circle can be defined as a limiting case of an ellipse whose foci are separated by a distance equal to zero, placing the two figures in a relation of contrastive apperception — is applied to Tlön/Earth: "Tlön is the circle that is the ellipse; by increasing the distance between the foci, the world's constitutive traits are shown."

## 6. Key concepts

**The 'as if' principle (Vaihinger):** The formula that expresses "that reality as given is compared with something whose unreality and impossibility is at the same time admitted." Fictions constitute cognitive instruments; they advance understanding of the empirical world precisely insofar as they raise certain attributes of the real and suppress others.

**Schematic fictions (Vaihinger):** Postulations of general types "conceived as absolutely bare and deprived of all those characters of reality that might interfere with the procedure." Their epistemological value lies in the "apperceiving power they are able to prompt." Both Tlön and *Les Cités Obscures* are analysed as schematic fictions.

**Apperception (Vaihinger):** "All cognition is the apperception of one thing through another." The cognitive operation by which the unknown is subsumed to the known via a contrastive or analogical comparison. Fiction provides the secondary term (the fictional world) through which the primary term (reality) can be cognised.

**Orbis Tertius:** The "third orb" of the Borges story's title — the product of the synthesis between the fictional world of Tlön and the real world. Once the Tlönian fiction is complete enough, the world itself becomes the third orb and the distinction collapses. Read by Riberi as a figure for the provisional and replaceable nature of all representational worlds: "They are constructs, like Wittgenstein's ladder, which must be thrown away after we have climbed up it."

**Map-territory paradox:** Borges's story of the 1:1 map demonstrates that the complete coincidence of map and territory negates both — the ultimate referent abscondes. Maps can only function as representations by remaining partial. This is the epistemological horizon that both "Tlön" and *Les Cités Obscures* expose: we live in maps all the way down.

**Re-cognisable world:** Riberi's term for a fictional world that is close enough to the real to be recognisable but different enough to produce cognitive defamiliarisation. Both "Tlön" and *Les Cités* are re-cognisable worlds: "They direct our regard upon cultural practices, architectural styles and history by means of superimposing transparencies."

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Interpretively complete, normatively restrained, open.** The paper does not derive a prescriptive normative conclusion. It closes with the cognitive claim: "Through fiction we give form to these possibilities; the discourse of fiction is the locus where the realm of the possible can be contrasted with our mundane always between quotes 'world'. This contrasting operation has cognitive effects, effects of truth; after all, all cognition is the apperception of one thing through another." The conclusion is open in the sense that no agenda for alternative cultural production or political transformation is advanced — the insight is epistemological, not normative. The philosophical case is made; the reader is left with it.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Borges and epistemology:** The paper is directly relevant to any corpus paper that addresses the epistemological status of fictional worlds, alternative realities, or the cognitive function of fiction. It provides a rigorous philosophical-literary framework for understanding how Borges's "Tlön" functions, which is the founding text of this Zotero collection.

**Theory-fiction and hyperstition:** Riberi's account of "Tlön" as a fiction that gradually colonises reality and eventually replaces it is structurally isomorphic with the Ccru's concept of hyperstition — a fiction that makes itself real through temporal feedback. The difference is that Riberi's analysis is epistemological and relatively neutral (Tlön as cognitive instrument), while the Ccru's hyperstition framework is explicitly political and libidinal (hyperstition as mobilisation tool). This contrast illuminates what is added when "Tlön" is read through a political rather than purely epistemological lens.

**Les Cités Obscures as parallel case:** The comparison with Schuiten and Peeters extends the "Tlön" mechanism to a visual/graphic medium, anticipating questions about multimodal philosophical fiction that Pezzano and Gualeni address directly in their paper on hybrid textuality (also in this corpus). Riberi's analysis of architectural extrapolation as a form of schematic fiction connects to the spatial theory concerns of Dalton's Neuromancer paper.

**Rollason (2026/2009) on "Tlön" and Poe:** Riberi and Rollason both analyse "Tlön" as a philosophical-literary mechanism, but from different angles: Riberi focuses on epistemology (Vaihingerian 'as if') while Rollason focuses on intertextuality (the Poe genealogy). Together they constitute complementary close readings of the same text, Riberi supplying the philosophical grammar and Rollason supplying the literary archaeology.
