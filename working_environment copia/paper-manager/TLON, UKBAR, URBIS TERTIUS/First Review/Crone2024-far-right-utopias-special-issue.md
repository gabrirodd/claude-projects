## 3. Puzzle or research question

The opening puzzle is analytical and political simultaneously: the existing literature on the contemporary far-right is dominated by the concept of "populism" — a framework focused on electoral politics, charismatic leadership, and the people/elite binary. But outside the realm of democratic party politics, "another right has emerged, one whose radical critique of representative democracy and growth-obsessed, global capitalism speaks surprisingly well to sensibilities distinct to the 21st century." This other right — utopian, micro-experimentalist, anti-state, localised — "often escape[s] academic and media attention." The question is: how should this other right be studied, and what conceptual framework captures its distinctiveness from both 20th-century fascism and contemporary electoral populism?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. The 21st-century far right is not a return but a new formation.** The dominant interpretive frame — recycling 20th-century labels (populism, nationalism, fascism with charismatic leaders and irrational crowds) and casting the past as a prelude — misses what is genuinely novel: the "fragmentation or unravelling of 20th century mass politics with its mass parties, mass media, grand ideologies, and national public spheres." The far right surfacing today is a product of "the human destruction of the planet, neo-liberal capitalism, and apocalyptic imaginaries" — conditions distinct to the 21st century.

**b. The utopian dimension is the key to understanding the non-electoral far right.** The special issue does not reject the continued relevance of national forms of far-right populism, but argues that "the national political arena is not necessarily the only or even the most important one today." A range of novel, more localised ideological-political spaces have emerged: intentional communities, ethnic sanctuaries, secessionist bioregions, military-discipline communities, online networks, eco-villages. What distinguishes these movements is their *utopianism* — understood in the Mannheimian sense of prescribing "the radical transformation, transcendence, or elimination of one or more pervasive practices, structures or ordering principles that shape human collective life."

**c. These are "nowtopias" — lived and practiced from below.** Following Carlsson and Manning, Crone proposes that far-right utopias are not "non-places" (Thomas More's u-topos) or "not-yets" (Bloch's anticipatory hope) but "concrete" and "real" — lived, practiced, enacted utopias: "small, 'interstitial' communities, re-enactments and polities that unfold in the gaps or at the fringes of global capitalism and formalized, democratic politics." They are politically engaged — performing "politics elsewhere and otherwise" — even as they turn away from liberal democratic institutions.

**d. Three distinctive features of this utopian right.** (i) *Anti-state orientation:* Unlike 20th-century fascism (which aimed to conquer the state), this right deliberately rejects the state as a privileged site for political action — consistent with its anti-modern sentiment, since the bureaucratic nation-state is a "modern invention par excellence." (ii) *Globalised localism:* A paradox. These movements look "below the nation-state: toward small ideological communities, ethnic sanctuaries, or secessionist bioregions at the level of the local; and simultaneously toward global networks and dreams of grand, reinvigorated civilizations." This is "not simply the return of some older, modern or pre-modern localism — nostalgic fantasies of a return to small agrarian communities — but the rise of a hyper-globalized and distinctly 21st century localism: one crisscrossed by dense global networks of communication, interaction and co-optation." (iii) *Post-rational or neo-spiritual turn:* All four case papers reveal manifestations of the far-right that are critical of the "spiritual void" of an overly rationalist, materialist, techno-fetishist, and capitalist modernity. They "remythologize and reenchant social, political, and natural spaces," proposing "fundamentally different realms of experience, meaning, and temporality" through Shinto, Sufism, exorcism, paganism, or absolute transcendental truth.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Conceptual and programmatic introduction** with an embedded theoretical framework. The primary intellectual move is the deployment of Mannheim's concept of utopia (against Bloch's) as the central organising concept: where Bloch sees utopia as a universal human propensity, a dream of the otherwise present in all phenomena, Mannheim defines utopia as a radical political project prescribing the transformation or destruction of existing practices and structures. This distinction allows Crone to focus exclusively on far-right groups that are politically transformative in a radical sense rather than merely expressive of diffuse discontent.

The methodological proposal is what Crone calls "isomorphic sensibility": "a methodology of isomorphic sensibility that stays attentive to the specificity of each case" rather than providing "an overarching model or framework for analysis." Each paper combines text analysis with — depending on the case — physical and digital ethnographies, interviews, and participant observation. The analytical focus moves "from the study of the radical right in terms of thinkers, ideologies, and ideas to explore these ideologies 'as they happen'" — following Freeden's suggestion that ideology can include everyday practices.

The introduction also makes an explicit geographical and religious broadening move: the special issue deliberately goes beyond the Europe/North America focus of existing far-right studies, including papers on Japan and South America, and ranges across Sufism, Shintoism, paganism, and Native American rituals.

## 6. Key concepts

**Utopianism (Mannheim-derived):** A political project that "can be considered utopian if, and only if, it invokes or prescribes the radical transformation, transcendence, or elimination of one or more pervasive practices, structures or ordering principles that shape human collective life." Distinguished from Bloch's broader account of utopia as a universal human propensity. The special issue uses Mannheim's narrower definition to focus on far-right groups that radically oppose the prevailing order.

**Nowtopia:** Carlsson and Manning's term. Not a "non-place" (More) or "not-yet" (Bloch), but a currently lived, enacted utopian experiment — "practiced, enacted, lived utopias: small, 'interstitial' communities, re-enactments and polities that unfold in the gaps or at the fringes of global capitalism and formalized, democratic politics." Distinguishes the studied movements from both 20th-century totalitarian utopias and from electoral populism.

**Real utopias (E. O. Wright):** Wright's concept (Wikipedia as example) of lived alternatives to prevailing capitalist economy. Crone imports this from the left to apply to the right: these far-right utopias are "real" in the same sense — enacted in practice, not merely imagined.

**Isomorphic sensibility:** Crone's methodological term for an approach that "stays attentive to the specificity of each case" — combining concepts and gazes of political theory and intellectual history with the sensibilities of cultural studies and political ethnography — without imposing an overarching model. The methodological complement to the ideological and ontological "pluriverse" the cases represent.

**Ethnopluralism:** A concept deployed by several of the far-right movements studied — the idea that distinct ethnic groups have distinct homelands and cultures that must be preserved, framed as a pluralist rather than hierarchical ideology (despite its obvious political valence). Associated especially with the French Nouvelle Droite (GRECE, Alain de Benoist).

**Globalised localism:** Crone's diagnosis of a paradox in contemporary far-right utopianism — movements that look inward toward local ethnic sanctuaries, eco-villages, and traditional communities while simultaneously operating in global networks of communication, co-optation, and inspiration. "Far-right utopian imaginaries may grow out of local/regional cosmologies, yet their practical manifestations often mirror, connect and inspire across continents."

**Post-rational / neo-spiritual turn:** The observation that all four cases studied reveal manifestations of the far-right that are critical of liberal epistemology and seek alternative modes of experience through religious, "irrational," or occult spiritualities (Shinto, Sufism, exorcism, paganism, transcendental truth). A "more fundamental rejection of liberal epistemology" than mere disagreement with liberal values or institutions.

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Open — research-agenda function.** The introduction does not close with a strong normative verdict or a prescriptive policy implication. It closes by summarising what the four cases collectively reveal: anti-state orientation, globalised localism, post-rational spirituality. These are offered as diagnostic observations, not as a typology or a model. The final paragraph makes the prescriptive point implicit: that the utopian far-right "inscribes itself in a much broader rejection of liberal forms of experience and meaning" — this is presented as a finding to be engaged and not merely observed. But no explicit political or scholarly agenda is advanced beyond the special issue itself.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

Crone acknowledges several limits:

- **Scope:** The special issue focuses on only four cases — deliberately modest, not aiming for an exhaustive overview or an inductively derived typology. "We wanted to open an inquiry into the large variety of ideas and practices on the utopian end of the far-right spectrum."
- **The borders between electoral and utopian right are "porous and permeable":** The sharp analytical distinction between utopian far-right movements and electoral far-right populism is acknowledged as a heuristic, not an empirical fact.
- **National forms of far-right populism are not rejected as irrelevant:** The special issue explicitly notes that it does not claim the utopian far-right is the only or most important form — it is a complement to, not a replacement for, populism studies.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Accelerationism, NRx, and far-right utopias:** Crone's special issue and the papers engaged with accelerationism and NRx (O'Sullivan, Vinokour, Balić) address overlapping phenomena from different disciplinary angles. O'Sullivan's "myth-science" paper diagnoses the intellectual genealogy and aesthetic politics of NRx as a form of far-right ideological practice; Vinokour analyses how that aesthetic circulates through literary fiction; Crone addresses the lived, enacted dimensions of far-right utopianism through political ethnography. The three approaches are complementary: O'Sullivan provides the philosophical genealogy, Vinokour the aesthetic analysis, Crone the political sociology of practice.

**Globalised localism and techno-politics:** The observation that contemporary far-right utopianism is paradoxically both localised and globally networked — "crisscrossed by dense global networks of communication, interaction and co-optation" — resonates with several themes in the broader corpus on techno-politics: the use of digital networks by far-right communities, the role of platforms in connecting previously isolated local movements, and the mobilisation of anti-modern sentiments through hypermodern media. Papers in the corpus on algorithmic platforms and political communication (Rieder 2015; Cheney-Lippold 2011) provide the platform-side analysis that Crone's community-side analysis presupposes.

**Post-rational spirituality and the rejection of liberal epistemology:** Crone's observation that the contemporary utopian far-right is not merely anti-liberal values but anti-liberal epistemology — seeking alternative, "irrational" or occult modes of knowledge (Shinto, Sufism, exorcism, paganism) — connects to broader corpus concerns with epistemic communities, post-truth discourse, and the politics of knowledge. Papers on QAnon (Baća 2024 in the TCS/PSC corpus, Type 4a), post-truth (Baier 2023), and the digital fragmentation of the public sphere (Sevignani 2022, Staab-Thiel 2022) all address aspects of this broader epistemic crisis from which the utopian far-right draws.

**Vinokour (2023) on Pelevin:** Vinokour's paper analyses how far-right aesthetics circulate through literary fiction and produce depoliticisation through irony. Crone's paper analyses how far-right ideology is enacted in practice through communities, rituals, and lived experiments. Together they cover the aesthetic and the practical dimensions of the same political phenomenon — the proliferation of far-right utopianism outside electoral party politics in the 21st century.

**The "Tlön" connection:** Crone's "nowtopias" — micro-utopian laboratories enacting alternative realities within the interstices of existing society — have a structural resonance with the Borgesian "Tlön" mechanism (a collectively authored fictional world gradually colonising and replacing reality). This is not an explicit connection Crone makes, but it is arguably the deep affinity that explains why this paper is included in a collection named after Borges's story.
