       This book, composed of an introduction and thirteen sections or chapters, presents
       an overview of the political vision articulated by Léopold Sédar Senghor across his
       extensive body of writings. In the introduction, Yohann C. Ripert demonstrates a
       thorough command of Senghor’s work, as well as a sustained and thoughtful engage-
       ment with the intellectual activity of the poet-president. His analysis reflects both
       scholarly rigor and a deep familiarity with Senghor’s philosophical and political
       concerns. The chapters that follow consist of English translations of selected texts
       drawn from Liberté volumes 1, 2, 3, and 4. These translations are carefully presented
       and supplemented with numerous quotations, which serve to clarify key concepts
       and facilitate the reader’s understanding of the original documents. By contextual-
       izing Senghor’s ideas and preserving the richness of his language, Ripert makes these
       foundational texts more accessible to an English-speaking audience while remaining
       faithful to their original intent.                    
         The five texts selected from Liberté I collectively position Black culture at the
       center of a global intellectual and cultural dialogue. Through these writings, Senghor
       articulates a vision in which Black cultural expression is no longer confined to a
       regional or racial framework but is instead understood as a vital contributor to world-
       wide debates on identity, creativity, and human values. From West Africa, presented
       as both a geographical and symbolic source of Black inspiration and energy, to the
       complex and multifaceted society of the United States of America, these texts reflect
       the breadth of Senghor’s cultural horizon. In his analysis, Ripert offers a nuanced
       exploration of the contexts in which Senghor’s observations emerge, as well as the
       intellectual environments to which they respond. Ripert does not treat these texts as
       isolated literary or political statements; rather, he situates them within the broader
       framework of Senghor’s engagement with ongoing global discussions, particularly

       those concerning the definition and function of culture. By doing so, Ripert highlights
       Senghor’s role as both a thinker and a mediator between different cultural worlds.
         One of the central contributions of Ripert’s work lies in his interpretation of Seng-
       hor’s concept of culture. According to him, Senghor understands culture not as a fixed
       or static inheritance, but as a dynamic interaction between human beings and their
       environment. He therefore summarizes this vision as “a racial reaction of Man on
       his milieu, striving toward an intellectual and moral equilibrium between both Man
       and milieu” (p. 2). This definition underscores the reciprocal relationship between
       individuals and their surroundings, emphasizing adaptation, creativity, and balance.
       Through this perspective, Black culture is presented as a living force, shaped by his-
       torical experience yet constantly renewed through dialogue with the world. Ripert’s
       reading makes clear that Senghor’s thought remains relevant today, particularly in
       contemporary debates on cultural diversity, globalization, and the search for ethical
       and intellectual equilibrium in an interconnected world.
         The second major theme highlighted by Ripert is more overtly political and draws
       on three texts from Liberté II, and one text from Liberté IV. In these writings, Seng-
       hor appears deeply motivated by a concern for the well-being of the people. Accord-
       ing to Ripert, Senghor’s political thought is characterized by an effort to reconcile
       Marxism with humanism, two traditions that might seem opposed but which Senghor
       sought to harmonize. This synthesis forms the foundation of his key political concept
       of African socialism. For Senghor, socialism is not merely an economic or ideologi-
       cal system, but a human-centered project grounded in ethical values, solidarity, and
       respect for human dignity. His approach emphasizes dialogue, cooperation, and cul-
       tural recognition rather than class struggle or rigid doctrinal positions. Ripert shows
       that this vision underpins Senghor’s political proposals and actions, particularly his
       advocacy for a federalist model of governance.        
         As a political outcome, Senghor promotes federalism as an alternative to bal-
       kanization. He views federalism as a system capable of granting greater power, rec-
       ognition, and responsibility to local communities while preserving broader political
       unity. This position must be understood within the historical context of the 1960s, a
       period marked by independence movements and the early stages of decolonization in
       Africa. During this time, balkanization led to the creation of numerous small states,
       often lacking real sovereignty and economic viability. These states were frequently
       the result of arbitrary colonial borders, drawn without regard for social, cultural, or
       historical realities, as exemplified by the Berlin Conference of 1885.
         To support his argument, Senghor agrees with the thought of Claude Lévi-Strauss,
       whom he cites to emphasize the importance of cooperation among peoples and that
       human progress depends on collaboration, and that differences between groups,
       rather than being obstacles, are the very conditions that make cooperation both nec-
       essary and fruitful (p. 74). Through this lens, Senghor’s political vision emerges as
       both pragmatic and profoundly humanist.               
         Ripert then brings back to light Senghor’s prophetic, poetic, and political vision,
       which is not a doctrine of struggle and combat in a world already at war. He offers
       a virtually original perspective on humanity and its destiny, both new and ancient,
       tracing the future. This Senghorian vision of federalism and convergence, oriented
       towards a civilization of the universal, seems to be, to this day, the only vision offered

         It is noteworthy that the theme of Negritude, so frequently and almost associated
       with the figure of Senghor, also appears in the work of Ripert. However, its pres-
       ence does not correspond to what one might initially expect when approaching a text
       that engages with Senghorian thought. Rather than treating Negritude as a central
       concept, Ripert integrates it into a broader and more complex intellectual frame-
       work. In this sense, Negritude is not developed as a closed ideology or as a purely
       racial or identitarian discourse, but instead emerges as one element within a wider
       reflection on cultural interaction. In Ripert’s work, the problematic of Negritude,
       articulated in Liberté 3, is inseparable from the larger context of Francophonie. This
       shift in perspective allows Negritude to be understood not merely as an affirmation
       of Black identity, but as part of a dynamic exchange between cultures within the
       French-speaking world. Francophonie, in this regard, becomes a space of dialogue,
       negotiation, and mutual influence, rather than a hierarchical or unidirectional system.
       Negritude thus participates in a transnational and intercultural discourse that extends
       beyond its original historical and geographical boundaries.
         At the same time, Ripert situates Negritude within a broader reflection on cul-
       ture itself. Culture is conceived not as a fixed or essentialized entity, but as a living
       and evolving process shaped by encounters, exchanges, and transformations. This
       approach resonates strongly with Senghor’s own vision of cultural openness, particu-
       larly his emphasis on métissage. For Senghor, métissage is not merely a biological or
       aesthetic concept, but a cultural and political one, grounded in the belief that civiliza-
       tions grow richer through contact and mutual recognition.
         From this perspective, Ripert’s choice to engage with Senghor and Negritude
       appears particularly relevant. In a multicultural society marked by diversity and
       mobility, Senghor’s openness to métissage offers a valuable framework for rethink-
       ing identity, belonging, and political coexistence. Ripert’s interpretation highlights
       the contemporary relevance of Senghorian thought by emphasizing its capacity to
       address the challenges of cultural pluralism and globalization. Consequently, Negri-
       tude, as presented in Ripert’s work, is less a doctrine of difference than a call for
       dialogue, rooted in a humanistic and inclusive vision of culture. For him, métissage
       in politics is “a process not so much akin to mixing (mestizo, from the Latin mixtus)
       as it is to weaving (tissage, from the Latin textere) racial and cultural threads: a
       ‘mixthreading’ that preserves each thread while forming an organized whole. (p. xiii)
         This exploration of culture following Ripert and laying on Senghor, compel-
       lingly emphasizes the distinctive role of culture in politics and in what Senghor calls
       the “civilization of the universal.” The modern world has largely been shaped by a
       rhythm imposed by the ambiguous triumph of this technologically advanced civili-
       zation has intensified antagonisms and deepened existential unease, pushing them
       to a critical threshold. Therefore, according to Senghor, by restoring a central place
       to intuition, art, and creative action, it can replenish the world’s depleted reserves
       of meaning and energy. Artistic expression, in particular, functions as a vital force
       capable of countering the sterility of purely instrumental reason. Through art and
       symbolic naturalism, such as the Fodéba Keita African Ballet, humanity can recover

       dimensions of experience that have been marginalized or neglected by modern tech-
       nological civilization. (p. 93)                       
         For Senghor, this cultural reorientation is not merely aesthetic but profoundly
       human and transformative, and interpreted through Ripert as a path toward a more
       balanced and humane universal civilization, one capable of integrating technical
       mastery with spiritual depth and political vitality. Ripert’s book not only honors the
       legacy of Senghor, but also attests to the enduring global interest he continues to
       inspire, particularly beyond the boundaries of the Francophone world and strictly
       cultural discourse.
