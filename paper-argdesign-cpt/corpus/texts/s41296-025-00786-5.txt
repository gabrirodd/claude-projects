       REVIEW                                                
                                                             
       Politicizing political liberalism: on the containment of
       illiberal and antidemocratic views                    
                                                             
       Gabriele Badano and Alasia Nuti, Oxford University Press, 2024, 224
       pp., ISBN: SBN: 9780192859310                         
                                                             
       Corrado Fumagalli1                                    
                                                             
                                                             
       © The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Nature Limited 2025
                                                             
                                                             
       Rising antidemocratic forces and intolerant rhetoric have prompted theorists to
       reconsider an old question: How should democratic institutions respond to internal
       threats to their stability? One aspect of this debate has long been especially striking.
       Until recently, despite John Rawls’ enduring influence in normative political theory,
       Rawlsian scholarship has largely ignored this challenge.
         Much of the renewed attention stems from a rediscovery of a footnote in Politi-
       cal Liberalism, where Rawls (2005, p. 64) writes that the existence of unreasonable
       doctrines ‘is itself a permanent fact of life’, and that ‘this gives us the practical task
       of containing them—like war and diseases—so that they do not overturn political
       justice’. Building on this, Gabriele Badano and Alasia Nuti argue that political liber-
       als should deal with the pressing task of addressing unreasonable doctrines and their
       supporters. Politicizing Political Liberalism thus provides a novel theory of liberal
       democratic self-defence (p. 6).                       
         The book is organized into two main parts. While in the first three chapters Badano
       and Nuti breathe fresh life into Rawlsian political liberalism, the book saves its most
       practical claims for the final three chapters, where it tackles the weakening of demo-
       cratic life head-on. In times when there is a broad recognition of the need to address
       the rise of diverse antidemocratic forces, readers may be tempted to skip the first part
       and turn directly to the dos-and-don’ts of democratic self-defence. That would be
       a mistake. The innovation introduced to political liberalism is anything but minor.
       While it is possible to read the normative arguments as stand-alone proposals, doing
       so blunts some of the nuances that contribute to this book’s scholarly weight. It
       remains rare to find scholarship so firmly rooted in a philosophical tradition while

       also demonstrating how that very tradition must be revised in order to make practical
       propositions viable.                                  
         The general goal of the first part is to show that Rawlsian political liberalism has
       internal philosophical resources for thinking about real-world liberal democracies,
       moving beyond standard disputes about the tenability of a hypothetical well-ordered
       society. That these philosophical resources emerge from within the paradigm is no
       small matter. And this underscores one of the book’s main contributions. Unlike
       many political theorists, who challenge Rawls’ conception of reasonableness for
       being exclusionary or poorly descriptive, this book aims to demonstrate that, even
       while remaining faithful to political liberalism’s core commitments, there is room to
       engage meaningfully with the messy reality of contemporary politics.
         To do this, they proceed in three steps. In Chap. 1, they contrast their view with
       that of some political liberals, who argue that the primary task of political liberalism
       is to demonstrate that legitimate and stable liberal institutions are possible despite the
       fact of reasonable pluralism. Badano and Nuti do not deny that responding to reason-
       able pluralism is a defining concern of political liberalism. They deny that reasonable
       pluralism is unique to a well-ordered society. It also exists in what they term mini-
       mal liberal societies (societies that, while falling short of the social and economic
       harmony of a well-ordered society, have protected civil and political liberties for a
       prolonged period), as well as in other possible configurations that fall between this
       lower bound and a perfectly just society. The main difference is numerical: within
       a well-ordered society, nearly all citizens are reasonable. If reasonable pluralism is
       present in non-ideal contexts, Badano and Nuti argue that the purpose of political
       liberalism goes beyond demonstrating the internal consistency of an ideal model. It
       should also consider how progress toward justice can be made under conditions of
       reasonable pluralism. Here, containment becomes more than a means of stability. It
       is understood as a way to increase the number of citizens committed to reasonable
       doctrines, thereby fostering incremental progress.    
         Lowering political liberalism’s level of idealization brings two issues into focus.
       First, less-than-ideal societies may contain many unreasonable citizens. This is the
       main concern of Chap. 2. The central question is whether, even within the paradigm
       of political liberalism, fundamentally committed to public justification under reason-
       able pluralism, it can ever be acceptable to coerce some citizens without justifica-
       tion. Badano and Nuti answer affirmatively. Second, less-than-ideal societies still
       house groups facing deep-rooted patterns of marginalization. For members of such
       groups, the demands of public reason can be overwhelming, unfairly constraining
       their capacity to contribute to political change. In Chap. 3, Badano and Nuti contend
       that demands originating in the fundamental moral requirements of public reason,
       such as reciprocity, apply only when others observe those same requirements in their
       dealing with us. If they do not, the normative burdens we bear are, at least in part,
       lifted. Groups that have suffered systematic exclusion and marginalization, having
       been refused reciprocal recognition, may therefore justifiably resort to alternative
       avenues to make themselves heard.                     
         Having clarified that political liberalism can apply to non-ideal scenarios, and that
       the model of public reason is sufficiently flexible to permit action against the unrea-
       sonable while safeguarding the agency of marginalized groups, the book proceeds to

       its political intervention. Badano and Nuti maintain that liberal institutions should
       strive to cultivate reasonable citizens. They also accept that, in the face of the most
       pressing threats, they may legitimately limit fundamental rights. However, engage-
       ment with these issues is rather brief and peripheral to the book’s main concerns.
         A key point of the argument is to shift the focus of disputes about democratic self-
       defence away from institutions alone and toward a broader set of actors. This shift
       is not without precedent. At certain points, the shift in focus appears motivated by
       considerations of effectiveness. State-driven harsh and soft containment measures,
       Badano and Nuti argue, have proven insufficient (pp. 111–112). And still there is no
       evidence that their proposals are more effective. This is further complicated by the
       fact that supporters of unreasonable views may be less susceptible to influence than
       the book sometimes seems to assume. Yet, the book’s central aim is not to identify the
       best response to the rise of unreasonable citizens, but rather to demonstrate that polit-
       ical liberalism can accommodate forms of action that may seem difficult to reconcile
       with the ideal of public reason. In view of this, the second part of the book outlines
       the duties that fall to ordinary citizens, political parties, and subnational administra-
       tive units when addressing the rise of unreasonable citizens.
         In Chap. 4, Badano and Nuti argue that in both physical and virtual spaces, such
       as pubs, family gatherings, and Facebook chats, ordinary citizens bear an imperfect
       duty to persuade friends, peers, and relatives who support right-wing parties toward
       greater reasonableness. In Chap. 5, they contend that when a reasonable conception
       of justice loses public support, political parties, local activists, think-tank members
       and journalists have a duty to modify the agenda they present to voters, perhaps by
       introducing alternative yet still reasonable conceptions of justice. In Chap. 6, the
       authors argue that subnational administrative units, insofar as they exercise self-
       government, have duties to contain unreasonableness. They have a duty to ‘refuse
       to cooperate with the central government when it is (at least partially) run by parties
       and leaders pursuing an unreasonable agenda’ (p. 163). They also have a duty to
       ‘establish and foster in the present those social relations that are characteristic of a
       well-ordered future society’ (p. 172).                
         There is no doubt that the justification of these duties represents a significant con-
       tribution to the literature on democratic self-defence. This three-tier framework opens
       several new avenues for research. In this respect, this book is one of the most com-
       pelling attempts to adapt Rawlsian political liberalism to the reality of contemporary
       politics. This virtue, however, comes with drawbacks. Badano and Nuti’s book also
       illustrates the challenges inherent in such a philosophical endeavour. From the intro-
       ductory pages to the concluding remarks, they claim that political liberalism should
       be made more explicitly political. I believe they succeed in achieving this objective.
         That said, this research program translates into a set of moral duties. Readers may
       thus come away with the sense that, rather than presenting a more politicized politi-
       cal liberalism, the book offers a moralized theory of democratic self-defence. This
       theory is anchored in the ingenious idea of repurposing Rawls’s cursory observa-
       tions on containment. Yet, whether there is sufficient real-world agreement on the
       conditions for deploying containment measures is a complex question. It is one thing
       to acknowledge the remote theoretical possibility that even a well-ordered society
       may at some point require treating unreasonable doctrines like wars and diseases. It

       is quite another to claim that, here and now, there exists consensus on the level of
       threat facing our democratic societies. Even with these concerns, the book stands
       as a remarkable achievement. Politicizing Political Liberalism constitutes a signa-
       ture contribution to contemporary normative political theory. It is essential reading
       for scholars of political liberalism, theorists of democratic self-defence, and anyone
       interested in critically reflecting on their role in deteriorating democratic societies.
