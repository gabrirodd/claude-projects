       In a world marked by turbulence and uncertainty, the relevance of nonideal theory and
       practical philosophy has never been more pressing. In the aftermath of the pandemic,
       societies continue to grapple with structural inequalities and fragile institutions,
       while wars in Europe and the Middle East remind us of the persistence of violence
       and geopolitical instability. At the same time, political polarization and democratic
       backsliding strain the capacity of governments to maintain legitimacy and authority.
       Against this backdrop, nonideal theory provides essential tools for understanding
       how individuals and institutions ought to act when justice, stability, and consensus
       are precarious rather than assured.                   
         Thomas Fossen adopts a novel perspective on the well-trodden topic of political
       legitimacy. Instead of analyzing the concept of legitimacy in abstracto to subsume the
       state’s right to rule under some Hohfeldian category, or investigating various norma-
       tive theories to pinpoint the ultimate source of such a right (e.g. consent, democracy,
       stability, public reason), Fossen’s primary objective is to critique all source-seeking
       theories. He argues that they embody a misguided form of ‘normativism’ that fails to
       account for legitimacy in the context of real-world discourses and practices.
         Fossen argues that normativist approaches wrongly assume that legitimacy can
       be adequately grounded on fixed criteria that determine whether a regime has a right
       to rule. He contends that these approaches, whether moral or realist, universalist or
       contextualist, all fail by remaining disengaged from the practices that give legitimacy
       meaning in non-theoretical contexts. For normativists, legitimacy judgments are typ-

       and the surrounding world.                            
         The book consists of two main parts. In Part I (Chaps. 1–3), Fossen develops a
       theoretical foundation for his approach, focusing on legitimacy and judgment as key
       concepts. In Part II (Chaps. 4–6), he examines the various activities involved in mak-
       ing legitimacy judgments in practice.                 
         Chapter 1 provides a comprehensive overview of legitimacy theory, categorizing
       major conceptions seeking to ground the state’s right to rule under the broad label
       of normativism. Fossen introduces his alternative model, which he terms pragma-
       tist, reframing legitimacy as an activity that engages with lived contexts rather than
       abstract principles. Crucially, this approach prioritizes the concept of judgment itself,
       as he explains: ‘instead of treating judgment as an afterthought, we make it our cen-
       tral theoretical concern’ (p. 34).                    
         The rest of Part I redefines the concepts of legitimacy and judgment to support a
       pragmatist framework. In Chapter. 2, Fossen draws on Robert Brandom’s philosophy
       of language to bridge the normative-descriptive divide, advocating an understand-
       ing of legitimacy rooted in situated perspectives that are both critical and attuned
       to political reality. As he puts it, ‘for political authority to be legitimate (according
       to someone) is for it to be appropriate to take it to be legitimate (from that perspec-
       tive)—and not just for it to be taken as legitimate (from others’ point of view)’ (p. 61).
       Chapter 3 clarifies that this view does not equate to a simplistic and arbitrary strand
       of subjectivism. Instead, Fossen presents judgment as an ongoing, intersubjective
       practice that continuously shapes and sometimes challenges our understanding of
       political reality. In essence, he argues that sound judgment, far from being arbitrary,
       is achieved through attunement to the complex realities of political life.
         Part II expands on this non-traditional view of judgment, exploring how it better
       aligns with actual practices of legitimating authority and what it means to ‘judge
       well’. Chapters 4–6 address, respectively, judgment in relation to political authori-
       ties, the identity of the judging individual, and the temporal context in which judg-
       ment occurs. Chapter 4 argues that defining a regime’s nature (e.g. as a parliamentary
       democracy or as an imperialist entity) is an essential part of judgment itself, not
       merely a descriptive exercise that precedes it. Chapter 5 positions legitimacy as an
       ‘existential predicament’, reflecting on how one’s stance towards a regime is bound
       up with one’s personal and collective identity. As such, this stance must align with
       one’s self-conception, which is fluid and open to transformation (or even dissolution)
       within legitimating practices. Chapter 6 highlights that legitimacy judgments are
       always shaped by specific times and events, underscoring Fossen’s call for legitimacy
       theory to adopt an ‘open-ended practical relation to events’ (p. 157). These chapters
       together deepen the concept of ‘judging well’, moving beyond reality attunement to
       emphasize virtues like responsiveness and integrity.  
         Fossen ends by exploring how legitimacy theory might transcend the normativist
       approach of codifying normative grounds for the state’s right to rule, suggesting that
       the enriched understanding of judgment he offers could, in future work, be paired
       with a deeper analysis of the power relations within which such judgments are made.

       His project is both timely and refreshing in its ambition. We sympathize with the
       general aim of the book to shift political theory away from abstract, source-seeking
       accounts of legitimacy and toward a more practical, nonideal engagement with politi-
       cal life. Fossen’s work seems to be taking political theory in a similar direction to
       other fields which, in our view, is a good thing. Just as philosophy of science has
       moved from rarefied conceptual debates to a contextualized and practice-oriented
       field, now often framed as history and philosophy of science, political theory appears
       to be undergoing a similar transformation. Fossen’s book exemplifies this shift: it
       demonstrates how treating legitimacy as an activity of judgment, rather than as a
       deduction from fixed principles, can render political philosophy more responsive to
       the realities it seeks to illuminate.                 
         While the book’s ambition is laudable, its execution leaves important gaps. Fossen
       insists that his pragmatist account avoids collapsing into subjectivism, yet he is not
       always clear in marking the distinction between his position and other non-normative
       views. At times, his rejection of ‘codification’ seems to sweep too broadly, grouping
       together a wide range of theories without adequately showing how his alternative
       departs from them in a principled way. This lack of theoretical clarity is compounded
       by a relative scarcity of citations, which makes it difficult to situate his arguments
       within existing debates and assess what is genuinely novel. Moreover, many of his
       central claims echo themes familiar from standpoint epistemology, but he doesn’t
       explicitly draw on that corpus of work or adopt its terminology. The result is a frame-
       work that feels underdeveloped, leaving readers unsure of how much is new insight
       and how much is a restatement of ideas already circulating in adjacent literatures.
         Despite Fossen’s stated commitment to practicality and situatedness, his style of
       writing remains highly abstract, at times undermining the accessibility and force of
       his argument. He frequently discusses concrete historical and political cases – such
       as the Red Army Faction’s terrorist actions in West Germany throughout the 1970s,
       the 2011 Egyptian revolution, and ongoing debates about the legitimacy of monarchy
       in The Netherlands – yet it is often unclear how these examples are meant to advance
       his theoretical claims. More sustained engagement with such episodes, perhaps even
       devoting whole chapters to moments of political transformation, could have illus-
       trated more vividly his central claim that legitimacy must be judged as it is actually
       contested and understood. As it stands, his use of examples tends to be illustrative
       rather than analytic, leaving readers to do much of the work of connecting the dots.
       Without deeper empirical grounding the distinction he draws between pragmatism
       and contextualism remains somewhat elusive.           
         A further tension in Fossen’s account concerns the relationship between descriptive
       and normative claims. If legitimacy is treated primarily as a sociological or historical
       phenomenon, it becomes difficult to see how his framework can deliver genuinely
       normative conclusions. History offers many examples of legitimate regimes being
       overthrown and illegitimate ones persisting, which suggests that more is required
       than simply tracing how legitimacy judgments are made. Fossen risks throwing the
       baby out with the bathwater by stripping political philosophy of its normative task
       and transforming it into a form of political science. To his credit, he does insist that
       his view is normative, grounding legitimacy judgments in the perspectives of those
       internal to a regime. Yet this raises further questions: while positionality matters,

       surely external scrutiny remains vital. Would it really be sufficient, for example, to
       leave the judgment of Nazi Germany solely to those within it? Fossen does acknowl-
       edge in his conclusion that external judgments are not without value, but he insists
       that internal perspectives are primary (p. 195). This stance highlights the originality
       of his approach, but also the unease it provokes about whether it abandons too much
       of what political philosophy is meant to do.          
         Fossen’s book offers an ambitious and thought-provoking challenge to dominant
       approaches in legitimacy theory, urging political philosophers to take judgment and
       situated practices more seriously. While the book’s general orientation toward a more
       practical, nonideal form of theory is welcome and needed, its abstract style, lack of
       engagement with historical cases, and limited citation of adjacent literatures leave
       parts of the argument underdeveloped. One way of reading Fossen’s project is as
       an application of standpoint epistemology to political theory, reorienting the field
       toward positionality and lived perspective. Yet without explicit engagement with that
       tradition, the reader is left to draw these connections themselves. The result is a book
       that points in an intriguing and fruitful direction, even if it does not always fully sub-
       stantiate or situate its claims.
