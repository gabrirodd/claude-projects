       Contemporary Confucian political philosophy has become one of the most dynamic
       fields in comparative political theory because it presses on questions that liberal the-
       ory often treats as settled: whether democracy is the only game in town, whether
       political equality is a requirement of legitimacy, whether human rights must be
       autonomy-grounded, and whether global justice must be framed through the nation-
       state/individual dichotomy. The question of what Confucian political philosophy is
       for—scholarly reconstruction, popular guidance, or institutional imagination—has
       therefore become an active fault-line, especially once ‘democracy’ is treated alter-
       nately as a value, an institutional package, and a cultural idiom (Angle, 2018, p.
       47). Yong Li’s Confucian Comparative Political Philosophy enters this space with
       unusual self-awareness. Rather than advancing a single unified position, it maps and
       clarifies ongoing debates in what Li calls ‘contemporary Confucian comparative
       political philosophy’—a choice that is both the book’s central virtue and the source
       of a few moments where readers may want more.         
         Li is explicit from the outset that his approach is issue-centered rather than fig-
       ure-centered, and that he will not seek to ‘defend a unified position on the issues
       discussed’ (pp. 2–3). Rather, his ‘primary effort is to explore their positions and argu-
       ments and to clarify their disagreements and challenges’ (p. 3). The result reads less
       like an ‘argument book’ than a bridge text that stages and adjudicates disputes across
       camps (Confucian democrats and meritocrats, perfectionists and anti-perfectionists,
       nationalists and cosmopolitans). In a field often marked by strongly programmatic
       interventions, this modesty is not evasive; it is a valuable scholarly choice.
         The book’s methodological chapter (ch. 2) is one of its strongest. Li distinguishes
       scholars who ground their justificatory claims in textual readings, those who appeal
       to historical and legal practices, and those who argue normatively without leaning on

       text or history (p. 7). Most importantly, he insists that textual evidence often under-
       determines contemporary normative conclusions. Discussing equality and inequality,
       Li writes: ‘we cannot argue for either…merely based on textual analysis’ (p. 9). This
       is a sober point in a literature where disputes can appear interpretive while remaining
       fundamentally normative. Yet Li also rejects an overly demanding methodological
       requirement: ‘A convincing defense of a particular Confucian political ideal does not
       require all three justifications’ (p. 14). Text, history, and normativity can reinforce
       one another, he argues, but none demands the others.  
         The trade-off is that methodological modesty sometimes shades into methodologi-
       cal restraint. Li is consistently good at discerning when texts and precedents cannot
       decide between rival positions; he does not always indicate what would count as
       sufficient to settle the dispute. That matters because many Confucian arguments in
       these chapters implicitly rely on claims about social practice, civic education, and
       institutional feasibility that cannot be handled by normative comparison alone.
         Chapters 3 and 4 address the central axis of contemporary Confucian political
       theory: moral and political equality, democracy, and meritocratic or mixed regimes.
       Li’s style here is deliberately explicative and adjudicative. For readers entering the
       field, this careful sorting of principles and institutions is genuinely useful. Never-
       theless, the cost of this cartographic posture is tempo. Specialists may find that the
       book sometimes reads like an exceptionally clear, seminar-like survey: positions are
       laid out, objections reconstructed, replies assessed, and verdicts remain provisional.
       Yet this is also the book’s distinctive service. As John Dunn (1968, pp. 85–86) sug-
       gests in a classic defense of intellectual ‘cartography’, a map is valuable precisely
       because it is selective: it does not reproduce the territory, but makes it more easily
       navigable. In a literature where many contributions are deeply invested in one camp,
       Li’s willingness to ‘keep debates alive’ (p. 34) yields a synoptic view that is hard to
       find elsewhere.                                       
         Still, Li occasionally moves swiftly when shifting from description to evaluation,
       as when he turns from democracy and meritocracy as institutions to democracy and
       meritocracy as cultures. After arguing that early Confucian texts support both demo-
       cratic and meritocratic readings, Li proposes that we ‘rely on the normative debate
       between democracy and meritocracy’ (p. 53). He then concludes that ‘democracy as
       a culture is more desirable than meritocracy as a culture’ (p. 54). The direction of
       the argument is plausible, but the conclusion depends on contestable assumptions
       about how democratic culture relates to solidarity and belonging, and about whether
       meritocratic culture must amount to morally objectionable domination. In a litera-
       ture where ‘democracy’ is routinely parsed as institution, principle, and culture—and
       where Confucian contributions often hinge on precisely such distinctions—this tran-
       sition would benefit from a more explicit accounting. 
         Chapter 5 is more constructive. Li argues not only that Confucianism is compatible
       with human rights, but that it can offer an alternative foundation for human dignity
       grounded in ‘human moral capacities’ (p. 67). Methodologically, he adopts a ‘thin
       concept’ of human rights as protections (p. 67). This allows him to sidestep familiar
       impasses about whether rights require autonomy as their foundation. He also regis-
       ters the key internal complication: the tension in Confucianism between common
       humanity and differential treatment based on moral achievement (p. 67). The chap-

       ter’s main virtue is clarity about scope: Li offers a defensible proposal rather than
       an overextended claim to have derived a comprehensive Confucian rights doctrine.
       However, the ‘thin-but-fast’ tendency appears here too: some argumentative steps are
       too brisk, inviting obvious questions about boundaries, authority, and moralization.
         Chapter 6 contains the book’s most explicitly negative verdict: Li concludes that
       ‘there is no convincing solution to the challenges faced by Confucian perfectionism’
       (p. 82). The chapter’s most portable contribution is his formulation of two challenges:
       an ‘accommodation challenge’ (public values must be thin enough to coexist with
       pluralism) and an ‘intelligibility challenge’ (they must be thick enough to remain rec-
       ognizably Confucian) (p. 91). As a dilemma, this is sharp and clarifying; it captures a
       real pressure faced by non-liberal perfectionisms in pluralistic societies.
         The difficulty is that Li sometimes treats ‘liberal neutrality’ as a default point of
       comparison against which perfectionisms must justify themselves. He claims, for
       instance, that Stephen Angle’s moderate perfectionism ‘fails to convince political lib-
       erals’ because it ‘does not adhere’ to neutrality, and that promoting Confucian values
       is ‘unfair to other traditions’ (p. 94). But why should ‘convincing political liberals’
       function as the decisive test? The accommodation/intelligibility dilemma risks ele-
       vating political liberalism to the position of implicit yardstick for moral doctrine—
       despite the fact that liberal ‘neutrality’ is itself contested and often experienced as
       non-neutral. If neutrality is not settled ground but one contestant among justificatory
       ideals, then Li’s dilemma looks less like a decisive objection to Confucian perfection-
       ism than a general problem about the moral pedagogy of the modern state.
         Chapter 7 is the book’s most illuminating demonstration of comparative recon-
       struction. Li argues that Tianxia offers an alternative framework to the nationalism/
       cosmopolitanism opposition in global justice (p. 105). He begins by clarifying points
       of deep disagreement—about basic moral agents, distributive scope, and the real-
       ism/idealism divide—and insists that we cannot consistently affirm the core commit-
       ments of both sides (pp. 105–106). He then distinguishes narrow and broad senses of
       Tianxia and reconstructs multiple Tianxia lineages, resisting both romantic cosmo-
       politan readings and simplistic empire-talk.          
         Li’s most compelling move is to interpret Tianxia as a form of statist cosmopoli-
       tanism that prioritizes states without endorsing ‘unqualified equality’, leans instead
       toward sufficientarian distributive aspirations (p. 109). The conceptual payoff is
       substantial: Tianxia becomes neither straightforward nationalism nor cosmopolitan-
       ism because it combines hierarchical order among states with an ultimate horizon of
       Datong and ‘Tianxia as a grand union’ (p. 110).       
         Li also distinguishes a ‘strong thesis’ (Tianxia is better) from a ‘weak thesis’
       (Tianxia is different), defending mainly the latter (p. 112). Yet the challenges he raises
       reveal where the Tianxia debate is now most philosophically interesting. Li suggests
       it is ‘strange’ to ascribe benevolence to states because ‘a state is not a person’ and
       lacks virtues (p. 112). But that claim comes too quickly. States are not persons in the
       ordinary sense, yet political theorists routinely treat them as agents with capacities,
       commitments, and institutionalized dispositions. If Tianxia is criticized for lacking
       a plausible motivational mechanism, the right target may be less virtue-language as
       such than the absence of an account of how institutional design, incentives, and forms

       of political leadership could produce reliable patterns of responsibility without slid-
       ing into imperial domination.                         
         Li’s book succeeds on its own terms. It offers a lucid, methodologically self-
       aware, and fair-minded map of contemporary Confucian comparative political phi-
       losophy for readers who want to understand what is at stake in debates over equality,
       democracy, rights, perfectionism, and global justice. Its modesty is not a hedge but a
       coherent posture, and its most valuable contribution may be precisely its willingness
       to keep debates open rather than prematurely closing them (p. 34). The costs of this
       posture are also real: parts of the book read like expertly curated debate-mapping, and
       some normative transitions—especially where ‘democracy as culture’ and ‘liberal
       neutrality’ do heavy work—would benefit from a more methodical treatment. Even
       so, Confucian Comparative Political Philosophy is an excellent bridge text, and its
       Tianxia chapter in particular shows how comparative reconstruction can open new
       conceptual space without pretending to have resolved debates that remain, in Li’s apt
       phrase, ‘ongoing yet insightful’ (p. 3).
