       Over the last two decades, there has been a significant expansion in the corpus of
       work and writings on B.R. Ambedkar. These studies have been crucial and signifi-
       cant in building an intellectual framework of Ambedkarism (praxial philosophy on
       human condition); however, they suffer from certain limitations. Much of the existing
       literature is biographical, some are descriptive and historical readings of Ambedkar,
       and others trace the emancipatory politics in his writings. Very few works, however,
       take an analytical approach or move beyond the life narrative paradigm to examine
       the philosophical strands in Ambedkar’s thought and action. Even the more analytical
       studies tend to be scattered, and they often rely on a narrow set of Ambedkar’s most
       celebrated texts, leading to what can be called a ‘thin interpretation’. In this context,
       Valerian Rodrigues’s Ambedkar’s Political Philosophy: A Grammar of Public Life
       from the Social Margins is a significant intervention. Shifting beyond conventional
       readings of Ambedkar, this book provides a systematic, close, and in-depth examina-
       tion of Ambedkar’s political philosophy, making it one of the most authoritative texts
       on his thought.                                       
         One of the most distinctive features of this book is its ‘thick hermeneutics’, or
       a sustained and careful interpretation of Ambedkar’s arguments in pursuit of his
       normative vision. This approach makes it an essential read for anyone interested in
       Ambedkar’s ideas, politics, and philosophy. The book also has a strong pedagogical
       value: it provides a systematic and coherent reading of Ambedkar’s thought, reflect-
       ing the intellectual labour Rodrigues has devoted over decades of research and writ-
       ing. (Rodrigues, 2002, 2019) The novelty of the book lies in how it excels on multiple
       fronts, whether with regard to the arguments, overall structure of the text, and its
       broader contributions.

         Because the book focuses on Ambedkar’s political philosophy, Rodrigues care-
       fully examines the very constituents of the ‘political’. For Ambedkar, this sphere is
       not confined to abstract principles alone but is deeply shaped by social, economic,
       cultural, ethical, and moral dimensions. Rodrigues shows that Ambedkar’s vision of
       the political extends well beyond the conventional vocabulary of liberty, equality,
       justice, and fraternity. It also draws centrally on concepts such as insaniyat (human-
       ity), maitreya (friendship), and karuna (compassion): ideas often marginalized in
       mainstream political theory. Rodrigues further highlights how Ambedkar engaged
       with political processes and institutions through a lens that always accounted for
       social life and lived experience. In this way, the ‘political’ for Ambedkar appears as
       an intricate web of social, economic, cultural, civilizational, and moral relations, an
       insight Rodrigues captures by drawing attention to sites of meaning that are often
       overlooked.                                           
         These arguments unfold across ten chapters, supported by archival depth, engage-
       ment with primary sources, and dialogue with existing scholarship. The book begins
       with an introduction situating Ambedkar in his epistemic context, tracing formative
       influences such as the Buddha, Kabir, Jotirao Phule, and John Dewey. This opening
       frame helps the reader see the distinct humanist currents that shaped Ambedkar’s
       thought.                                              
         Rodrigues organizes the book into two broad ‘inter-texts’. The first six chapters
       focus on Ambedkar’s key concepts and values, while Chaps. 7 to 9 turn to politi-
       cal processes and institutions. The opening chapter, ‘Reading Texts and Traditions’,
       presents Ambedkar as a rigorous and rational thinker, engaged in sustained herme-
       neutical inquiry. Chapters 2, 3, and 4, on caste and untouchability, on what it means
       to be human, and on equality as the foundation of justice, are central to Ambedkar’s
       understanding of the human condition and his normative imagination. In many ways,
       they embody the spirit of both the thinker and the book. Placing these chapters at
       the outset allows the reader to grasp the philosophical core before moving into more
       applied debates. Chapter 5, on nationalism, reveals Ambedkar’s nuanced position on
       the nation-state, nation-building, and community politics: issues that remain pressing
       today, especially as competing ideologies attempt to appropriate his legacy. Chap-
       ter 6 offers a detailed reading of Ambedkar’s view of democracy, showing how he
       regarded social and economic democracy as essential foundations. Rodrigues also
       carefully distinguishes democracy from related concepts, clarifying how Ambedkar’s
       vision was both distinctive and expansive.            
         In the second ‘inter-text’, Rodrigues turns to Chaps. 7, 8, and 9. Chapter 7 distin-
       guishes Ambedkar’s thinking from dominant frameworks such as liberalism, social-
       ism, and Marxism. Rodrigues emphasizes Ambedkar’s distinctive understanding of
       power and the state, an analysis further developed in Chaps. 8 and 9. These chapters
       are particularly relevant today, amid widespread debates about constitutional crisis.
       They are also central to Ambedkar’s political philosophy, rooted in his experiential
       epistemology and his role as chair of the drafting committee of India’s constitution.
       Together, they provide readers with a ringside view of both Ambedkar’s imagination
       and the constitutional history of modern India.       
         Finally, Chap. 10 examines Ambedkar’s reflections on religion, its role in shaping
       human sociality, and his distinctive interpretation of Buddhism as the foundation of

       the modern public. The book’s overall structure and organization deserve attention:
       the chapterization and schema firmly hold together Ambedkar’s worldview, high-
       lighting his lifelong quest for dignified selfhood and social justice. This is, by far, one
       of the few works that not only discuss Ambedkar but also explicitly establish him as
       a political philosopher, through the ways of ‘thick interpretation’ over ‘thin’.
         Although the book centers on Ambedkar’s political philosophy, Rodrigues con-
       sistently places him in conversation with other thinkers, traditions, and ideologi-
       cal currents. This comparative approach highlights Ambedkar’s unique position
       in global debates. One of the book’s notable achievements is its inclusiveness: it
       presents Ambedkar’s arguments through an intersectional lens. Too often, studies
       of canonical thinkers neglect their positions on gender. Rodrigues, by contrast, fore-
       grounds Ambedkar’s engagement with the gender question and demonstrates how
       his perspective helps to ‘engender’ the political. For example, the chapter on ‘Being
       Human, Constitutionalism, and Rule of Law’ brings Ambedkar’s gendered con-
       cerns into sharper focus. The text’s use of gender-inclusive language is especially
       commendable.                                          
         The book’s density and richness are also worth noting. Each argument is care-
       fully substantiated, and the footnotes often read like miniature essays, providing
       intertexts and invitations for further exploration. Yet this density does not become
       overwhelming. Rodrigues succeeds in breaking down clusters of concepts, distin-
       guishing between related ideas, and clarifying Ambedkar’s interlocutions and con-
       trasts. For instance, the book carefully parses distinctions between social, economic,
       and political democracy; between morality and ethics; between living and worthy
       living; between individual and collective agency; between fraternity and sociality;
       and between parliamentary and representative democracy. This kind of conceptual
       precision is rare and often neglected. Despite its intellectual rigor, the book remains
       accessible. Rodrigues writes with attention to a wide audience, not only scholars and
       specialists but also students and general readers curious about Ambedkar. The peda-
       gogical value of the text is significant: it introduces complex philosophical terrains
       in a way that is rigorous yet approachable, making it a resource for teaching as well
       as research.                                          
         Ambedkar’s Political Philosophy is essential for anyone seeking to understand
       Ambedkar in depth. Moving beyond reductive portrayals of him as merely an ‘Indian
       thinker’ or as a leader of the socially marginalized, Rodrigues invokes Ambedkar as
       a global, cosmopolitan intellectual and philosopher. While Ambedkar’s context was
       grounded in India, Rodrigues highlights the universalism in his philosophy, showing
       how his interventions spoke directly to enduring questions of the human condition.
         The book also reclaims the distinctiveness of South Asian political thought. By pre-
       senting Ambedkar’s ideas as systematic and coherent, Rodrigues challenges the view
       that non-western traditions are derivative of Western philosophy. Instead, Ambed-
       kar’s thought demonstrates how non-western thinkers define and engage with the
       world in their own terms. The ‘migratory’ and ‘circulatory’ nature of ideas becomes
       central here: Ambedkar’s intellectual life shows that concepts did not merely flow
       from west to east but moved and transformed across multiple directions. The book
       foregrounds indigenous concepts and values that Ambedkar placed at the heart of the

       political, enriching our understanding of the grammar of egalitarian and just public

         Rodrigues’s thick interpretation also strengthens Ambedkar studies as an episte-
       mological field. By situating Ambedkar as a thinker of universal dignity, equality, and
       justice, Rodrigues shows how his work speaks powerfully to today’s crises. As the
       Therigatha observes of the ‘depravities that ooze out of the world’ (Hallisey, 2015,
       p. 5), insistence on human worth, insaniyat (humanity), and fraternity reminds us of
       the foundations on which democracies must stand. In an era marked by war, constitu-
       tional fragility, populism, and the normalization of religious and cultural orthodoxy,
       Rodrigues’s recovery of Ambedkar’s vision is both timely and urgent. It also serves
       as an act of intellectual resistance at a moment when Ambedkar is frequently appro-
       priated by those whose politics he directly critiqued.
