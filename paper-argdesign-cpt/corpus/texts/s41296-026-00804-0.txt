       REVIEW                                                
                                                             
       We are free to change the world: Hannah Arendt’s lessons
       in love and disobedience                              
                                                             
       Lyndsey Stonebridge, London: Hogarth, 2024, p. 368, ISBN: 978-
       0593229736                                            
                                                             
       Martial Fanga1

       Lyndsey Stonebridge’s We Are Free to Change the World: Hannah Arendt’s Lessons in
       Love and Disobedience is a profound and timely exploration of Hannah Arendt’s life
       and thought, offering a compelling argument for the enduring relevance of Arendt’s
       work in navigating the political and existential challenges of the twenty-first century.
       Published in 2024 by Hogarth, an imprint of Random House, Stonebridge’s book
       positions Arendt not as a relic of the past but as a vibrant and necessary companion
       for understanding and resisting the resurgent forces of authoritarianism, mendacity,
       and dehumanisation that mark our current era (Miller, 2019). The book’s central the-
       sis is that Arendt’s unique perspective, forged in the crucible of 20th-century totali-
       tarianism and exile, offers crucial insights into the nature of freedom, the dangers of
       thoughtlessness, and the vital importance of human connection and political action in
       a world seemingly bent on its own destruction. Stonebridge argues that Arendt’s les-
       sons in love and disobedience are not abstract philosophical concepts but hard-won
       strategies for survival and resistance in dark times. Stonebridge structures her argu-
       ment by weaving together biographical details of Arendt’s life with in-depth analysis
       of her key philosophical and political concepts (Bernstein, 2020).
         Stonebridge organizes her exploration of Arendt’s thought into ten thematically
       interconnected chapters, each focusing on a specific facet of Arendt’s philosophy and
       its relevance today. The book begins with an overview of Arendt’s life, setting the
       stage for understanding how her personal experiences shaped her ideas. Subsequent
       chapters delve into concepts such as love, disobedience, and the human capacity for
       freedom, weaving biographical anecdotes with philosophical analysis. Each chapter
       stands as a self-contained examination, yet they collectively build a coherent narra-
       tive emphasizing Arendt’s enduring lessons for contemporary political life. The final

       chapters synthesize these themes, offering practical reflections on how Arendt’s ideas
       can inform resistance and resilience in the face of modern crises. Stonebridge’s clear,
       accessible prose and logical progression make the complex trajectory of Arendt’s
       thought both comprehensible and compelling, guiding readers through a nuanced
       understanding of her work’s relevance today.          
         Stonebridge’s methodology is a significant strength of the book, integrating bio-
       graphical narrative with philosophical analysis in a way that illuminates both. She
       moves seamlessly between Arendt’s personal experiences and her complex theo-
       retical arguments, demonstrating how the former shaped the latter. This approach
       is enhanced by Stonebridge’s interdisciplinary engagement, drawing on history, lit-
       erature, and even psychoanalysis (though she is critical of its application to Arendt’s
       life) to enrich her analysis. By discussing Arendt’s relationships with figures like
       Walter Benjamin, Karl Jaspers, and Heinrich Blücher, and by referencing literary
       works by authors such as Goethe, Kafka, and Rilke, Stonebridge effectively situates
       Arendt within a broader intellectual and cultural landscape. The inclusion of detailed
       notes and references to Arendt’s primary works and correspondence allows readers
       to delve deeper into the sources.                     
         The book’s strengths lie in its accessibility, its passionate engagement with
       Arendt’s ideas, and its timely relevance. Stonebridge’s prose is lucid and engaging,
       making complex philosophical concepts understandable to a broad audience. Her
       personal connection to Arendt’s work, evident throughout the book, infuses the anal-
       ysis with a sense of urgency and conviction. The book’s focus on the practical lessons
       of Arendt’s thought how to think, love, and disobey makes it particularly valuable for
       readers grappling with contemporary political challenges. Stonebridge’s willingness
       to engage with the controversies surrounding Arendt, particularly the reception of
       Eichmann in Jerusalem, adds to the book’s critical depth. Her nuanced discussion of
       Arendt’s limitations, such as her perspective on race in America, demonstrates intel-
       lectual honesty and strengthens the overall argument. 
         While the entire book offers valuable insights, certain chapters stand out for their
       novelty and originality. Chapter Four, ‘How to Think Like a Refugee’ (pp. 80–123), is
       particularly groundbreaking. Stonebridge’s emphasis on statelessness as a structural
       problem of modernity, rather than a mere consequence of war, offers a powerful and
       timely perspective. Her use of vivid examples, such as the Jewish cemetery in Beirut
       and the Walter Benjamin memorial, effectively conveys the human cost of displace-
       ment and the enduring relevance of Arendt’s critique of human rights in the absence
       of citizenship. This chapter offers a fresh and critical lens through which to view
       contemporary migration crises, moving beyond humanitarian concerns to address the
       fundamental political questions of belonging and the ‘right to have rights’. Stone-
       bridge’s analysis of ‘holes of oblivion’, a concept Arendt introduces in the Origins of
       Totalitarianism, as a means of rendering people superfluous is particularly chilling in
       its contemporary resonance.                           
         Another standout chapter is Chapter Five, ‘How to Love’ (pp. 124–168). Stone-
       bridge’s exploration of Arendt’s understanding of love, particularly her engagement
       with Saint Augustine and her reinterpretation of volo ut sis, is both intellectually
       stimulating and emotionally resonant. By presenting love not as a mere private emo-
       tion but as a ‘world-creating’ force (quoting Arendt’s notes on love), Stonebridge

       offers a compelling counterpoint to the dehumanising forces Arendt analysed. The
       chapter’s discussion of Arendt’s social life in New York highlights the practical ways
       in which she cultivated connection and community, demonstrating that love is not
       just a theoretical concept but a lived practice essential for human flourishing.
         Despite its many strengths, the book does have some minor weaknesses. While
       Stonebridge acknowledges Arendt’s limitations, such as her views on race, a more
       extended engagement with these critiques, perhaps in a dedicated section, could
       have further strengthened the analysis. Additionally, while the book effectively high-
       lights the contemporary relevance of Arendt’s thought, a more explicit discussion
       of specific policy implications or strategies for applying Arendt’s lessons to current
       political challenges could have been beneficial. For example, exploring potential
       Arendtian approaches to addressing statelessness and migration would have added
       another layer of depth.                               
         Stonebridge’s book fits within a broader literature on Hannah Arendt, engaging
       with established scholarship while offering original interpretations. She builds upon
       the work of biographers like Elisabeth Young-Bruehl and scholars exploring Arendt’s
       engagement with key figures in philosophy and political theory. She differentiates
       herself by emphasizing the practical and existential dimensions of Arendt’s thought,
       highlighting her relevance as a guide for living and acting in turbulent times. Her
       interdisciplinary approach and focus on biographical context set her work apart. The
       book dialogues with studies examining the connections between Arendt’s work and
       contemporary issues such as authoritarianism and migration. By centering her anal-
       ysis on love and disobedience, Stonebridge offers a fresh perspective on Arendt’s
       enduring legacy.                                      
         The target audience for We Are Free to Change the World is broad. It appeals to
       those interested in political philosophy, history, and biography, as well as to readers
       seeking to understand and respond to current political crises. The accessible prose
       and engaging narrative make it suitable for both academic and general readers. Stu-
       dents of political science, philosophy, and history will find it a valuable introduction,
       while activists and engaged citizens will find inspiration.
         In conclusion, Lyndsey Stonebridge’s We Are Free to Change the World is a
       groundbreaking and essential contribution for contemporary times. By weaving
       biography and philosophy, Stonebridge presents Hannah Arendt as a courageous
       thinker whose insights provide a vital antidote to modern political and existential
       crises. The lessons in love, disobedience, and thinking are grounded in her personal
       experiences of totalitarianism and exile, offering a compelling framework for under-
       standing freedom, thoughtlessness, and human connection. Her nuanced engagement
       with Arendt’s strengths and limitations, coupled with an interdisciplinary approach,
       makes this a profoundly enriching read. Emphasizing Arendt’s insights into stateless-
       ness and the right to have rights, Stonebridge underscores her enduring relevance and
       offers a timely call for action. As Stonebridge powerfully concludes, ‘We are free to
       change the world and to start something new in it’ (Arendt, 1972, p. 5), a message of
       hope that resonates deeply amid current global upheavals.
