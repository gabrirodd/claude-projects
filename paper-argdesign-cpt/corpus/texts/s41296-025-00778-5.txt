       In In the Shadow of Minority Rights, Sagnik Dutta offers a subtle reframing of how
       political agency is practiced and imagined in contemporary India, where Muslim
       women navigate a landscape shaped by legal pluralism, ethical commitments and
       communal suspicion. Rather than treat the liberal state as the locus of justice, Dutta
       attends to how Muslim women, situated within lived traditions of Islamic ethics and
       the interpretation of constitutional norms in India, enact political agency through
       embodied practice. Drawing from fieldwork with Muslim women activists affiliated
       with the Bharatiya Muslim Mahila Andolan (BMMA) and the Mohalla Committee
       Movement Trust (MCMT) in Mumbai, Dutta shows how their everyday ethical prac-
       tices enable marginalized Muslim women to reimagine political life on moral terms.
         Dutta tells a story of Indian democracy that is not just a system of institutional
       guarantees but an evolving terrain of ethical-political negotiation shaped by plural
       traditions and contested spaces. Rather than begin with the liberal state as the locus
       of rights or recognition, Dutta situates political life in the dense, everyday practices
       of Muslim women who draw simultaneously on Quranic ideals and constitutional
       commitments. These women do not simply navigate the space between religion and
       the law; they remake both through ethical labor. The pluralism that emerges as a
       consequence is not an abstract value or state policy; it is a lived condition, negoti-
       ated through affect, space, and obligation in the shadow of communal violence and
       majoritarian surveillance. The book reframes Indian democracy as an assemblage of
       ethical grammars and minoritarian lifeworlds. For comparative political theory, the
       implication is profound: to understand pluralism in postcolonial contexts, we must
       attend to how normative worlds are constructed from below, not merely accommo-
       dated from above.

         Building on this methodological starting point, Dutta’s central argument unfolds
       through an analysis of how Muslim women reconstitute political agency. At the
       heart of this claim is a refusal to see their agency as reducible to formal rights
       claims or oppositional protest. This approach complicates dominant narratives of
       Muslim women’s agency in democratic India, which have often cast them either as
       victims in need of legal protection or as exceptions to liberal secular norms. Instead,
       the book foregrounds how Muslim women activists generate situated forms of moral
       and political credibility through practices of deliberation, restraint, and compassion
       in the everyday life of public spaces.                
         Dutta’s account invites us to rethink what constitutes political agency and moral
       legitimacy in diverse postcolonial democracies. This is especially resonant when
       placed in the longer arc of postcolonial legal and feminist contestation in India. A
       key turning point in this arc was the Shah Bano case in the 1980s, when a Mus-
       lim woman’s demand for post-divorce maintenance sparked a politically charged
       national legal battle on who can speak on behalf of Muslim women in India. The
       controversy revealed deep tensions between constitutional promises of gender equal-
       ity, religious freedom and minority rights (Menon, 2004; Rajan, 2003). Yet it also
       opened up a space in which Muslim women began to articulate their claims through
       both Islamic ethical reasoning and the language of constitutional justice. Dutta car-
       ries forward this legacy, showing how those dual commitments are not mutually
       exclusive but mutually sustaining.                    
         Chapters 2, 3, 4, and 5 each develop this argument in distinct registers. Chapter 2
       emphasizes how ethical concepts like insaf (justice) and barabari (equality) struc-
       ture everyday engagements between Muslim women and the Indian state. It lays
       the conceptual groundwork for Chapter 3 by demonstrating that these ideals are not
       abstract principles but grounded in ethical commitments, such as fairness in adjudi-
       cation, humility in judgement, and responsiveness to community needs, all practices
       enhanced through relational learning. Chapter 3 then builds on this by showing how
       these ethical commitments are mobilized in community-based dispute resolution
       spaces, such as neighborhood or mohalla committees and BMMA-leg legal clinics,
       where women reinterpret both Islamic and constitutional vocabularies in the resolu-
       tion of concrete disputes. Women activists reconstruct both traditions by reinterpret-
       ing them in practice: invoking Quranic verses alongside constitutional principles,
       translating religious obligations into civic claims, and treating the authority of both
       as contingent upon their capacity to deliver justice. In doing so, they create a hybrid
       normative vocabulary that allows them to draw upon overlapping moral registers of
       legitimacy in navigating disputes and seeking redress.
         Chapter 4 deepens this account by examining how Muslim women navigate
       the carceral state in their everyday lives, particularly through encounters with
       policing, surveillance, and bureaucratic institutions. These experiences gener-
       ate a mode of political subjectivity marked by “estranged attachment,” in which
       women engage the state and religious authority not through full identification or
       rejection, but through critical, affective negotiation shaped by fear, distrust, and
       ethical improvisation. The book’s ethical arc culminates in Chapter 5, which fore-
       grounds raham (compassion) as a political and epistemic mode of justification.
       Raham, developed by women activists through their ethical actions, provides an

       alternative grammar of judgment grounded in ethical reasoning and relational
       obligation rather than abstract legal rights. These practices emerge within a con-
       text that is at once enabling and constraining. On the one hand, plural constitu-
       tional ideals offer space for ethical agency. On the other hand, the securitization
       and communalization of Muslim life in India continually threaten to foreclose
       it. Rather than resigning to these constraints, Dutta reveals how Muslim women
       activists negotiate rights through spatial, affective, and ethical registers that defy
       the limits of liberal political theory.               
         This attention to ethical labor not only reframes agency, but also challenges dom-
       inant assumptions in political theory about where normativity resides. Rather than
       treat institutions or doctrines as the primary bearers of political value, Dutta places
       everyday ethical practice at the center of political life. He portrays constitutionalism
       not as a static legal form or institutional promise, but as an ethical horizon, inter-
       preted, inhabited, and justified through daily life alongside Islamic values. The result
       is a methodological invitation: to theorize from practices that are affective, embod-
       ied, and historically saturated, rather than from idealized models of rights or auton-
       omy. In this, Dutta aligns with scholars like Saba Mahmood (2012) and Talal Asad
       (2003), yet extends their insights into the domain of constitutional and plural legal
       life. The book makes a strong case for understanding pluralism not as state tolerance
       of difference, but as a terrain of negotiated obligations and shared moral grammars.
         Dutta’s reframing of political agency moves beyond dominant liberal accounts
       that tether empowerment to autonomy or state recognition. Instead, he aligns with
       feminist scholarship that insists on the legitimacy of forms of agency grounded in
       restraint, relationality, and ethical responsibility (Abu-Lughod, 2013; Gandhi, 2014;
       Honig, 2016). The women in his account reinterpret religious and civic obligations
       in ways that defy both patriarchal control and secular-liberal prescriptions.
         Scholars have long argued that political life in postcolonial democracies must be
       theorized from the terrain of the governed, where legitimacy arises not only from
       state institutions but from enduring ethical life-worlds (Chatterjee, 2006). Dutta’s
       work resides productively within this tension. Yet one may ask whether Dutta’s
       emphasis on the ethical and everyday risks overlooking structural dimensions of
       justice. While the book offers compelling portraits of grassroots activism, it is less
       clear how these practices translate into broader transformations in law or policy.
       Can embodied ethics alone sustain a politics of equality and justice in the face of an
       increasingly authoritarian state and the shadow it casts over minority rights? These
       questions echo longstanding concerns in political theory about how recognition and
       cultural politics must be complemented by structural redistribution and institutional
       change (Getachew, 2019; Honig, 2016).                 
         This book traces how women craft political authority through restraint, delibera-
       tion, and care, from the plural moral worlds central to India’s democratic life. For
       comparative political theory, the lesson is clear: ethical-political life outside the lib-
       eral state is not merely a site of critique, but a generative ground for reconstruct-
       ing global political thought from traditions too often treated as peripheral or merely
       empirical.
