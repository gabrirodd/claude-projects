       Protecting democracy in Europe: pluralism, autocracy and
       the future of the EU                                  
                                                             
       Tom Theuns, Hurst Publishers, London, 2024, 336 pp., ISBN
       9781911723745                                         
                                                             
       Benjamin A. Schupmann1

       Democracy is widely understood to be under threat today. Less obvious is the extent to
       which the European Union itself bears responsibility among its members. In Protect-
       ing Democracy in Europe, Tom Theuns argues that the EU’s institutional responses
       to democratic erosion have been inadequate, making it complicit. He examines what
       more the EU can legitimately do to defend democracy within member states, advanc-
       ing a series of mutually reinforcing measures ranging from containment to, in extre-
       mis, dissociation from antidemocratic states. Theuns offers an ambitious account of
       how the EU can better respond to democratic backsliding, combining institutional
       analysis with normative argument to make a compelling case for treating democratic
       self-defence as a problem that extends beyond the nation-state. While I am broadly
       sympathetic to this project, his account is marked by a tension between the substan-
       tive democratic values he seeks to defend and the limits he places on the legitimate
       means of doing so.                                    
         Theuns’ book is grounded in the recognition that democratic backsliding within
       the EU threatens the Union’s normative foundations. Article 2 of the Treaty on Euro-
       pean Union (TEU) articulates those foundations in terms of human dignity, democ-
       racy, the rule of law, and respect for human rights (p. 72), which Theuns treats as
       constitutive of democratic legitimacy at both the national and transnational levels.
       When a member state backslides into autocracy, it undermines not only its own legiti-
       macy but the normative basis of European integration itself.
         Although antidemocrats are most responsible for democratic backsliding, Theuns
       argues that the EU is also complicit in two related ways. First, EU institutions exhibit
       a form of membership fatalism: after accension, a state’s membership is treated as
       irreversible, even as it slides into autocracy (pp. 22 ff.). Second, this fatalism encour-

       ages depoliticized responses: the EU relies on dialogue, monitoring, and legal pro-
       cedures that presuppose good faith, rather than treat antidemocrats as a genuine
       political threat. As a result, the EU fails to use existing tools to constrain antidemo-
       crats, including infringement proceedings, financial conditionality, and political iso-
       lation (p. 43).                                       
         Theuns proposes several measures to help the EU respond to democratic back-
       sliding. The first set centres on containment: limiting the capacity of autocrats to
       entrench themselves while preserving democratic inclusion. Economic measures,
       such as financial conditionality and the suspension of EU funds, limit autocrats’
       material benefits from membership (p. 115). Legal and institutional measures deny
       leadership roles and agenda-setting power within EU institutions (p. 137). Political
       measures withhold symbolic recognition and legitimation (p. 144). Crucially, Theuns
       argues these measures must be paired with positive interventions to cultivate plural-
       ism, including financial support and recognition for opposition actors, independent
       media, universities, and civil society (p. 153).      
         If containment fails, Theuns considers whether Article 7 TEU provides a legiti-
       mate response to democratic backsliding – and forcefully rejects it. Article 7 permits
       the suspension of a member state’s voting rights in the Council in response to a
       serious breach of Article 2’s fundamental values. Drawing on debates about mili-
       tant democracy, Theuns argues that this measure is normatively indefensible because
       it contradicts the democratic values it purports to defend (p. 83). If democracy is
       conceived minimally, as equal political co-authorship, suspending a member state’s
       voting rights deprives its citizens of political equality while they remain subject to
       EU law (p. 76). For that reason, Theuns concludes that Article 7 must be abandoned.
         Given his rejection of Article 7, the radicality of Theuns’ next move is striking. If
       containment fails, he argues, the EU’s still-democratic members must dissociate from
       autocratic members – either by expelling them or by withdrawing and reconstituting
       the Union (pp. 191–196). While acknowledging serious legal and practical obstacles,
       Theuns presents dissociation as a tragic necessity: a political solution that overcomes
       membership fatalism and preserves the EU’s fundamental values, at the cost of insti-
       tutional rupture.                                     
         Protecting Democracy in Europe makes an impressive contribution to debates on
       democratic self-defence by combining detailed institutional analysis with sustained
       normative argument about how democracies may legitimately respond to internal
       threats. Although focused on the EU, the book offers a general framework for how
       transnational institutions can respond to democratic backsliding without undermining
       their commitments. By recognizing that autocratization creates negative externalities
       for other democracies, Theuns shows why democratic states cannot afford to tolerate
       antidemocrats within shared institutional orders and why collective responses are
       normatively required.                                 
         While Theuns offers a compelling diagnosis of democratic backsliding as a trans-
       national threat, his normative framework is undercut by a tension. The nature of the
       threat he seeks to address – the ‘illiberal democracies’ of Fidesz’ Hungary and PiS’
       Poland – is defined in terms of the substantive values of Article 2. Yet when evaluat-
       ing defensive measures, Theuns relies on a thinner conception of democracy centred
       on equal political co-authorship. This creates a mismatch between the values he aims

       to defend and the norms used to assess legitimate means. Contemporary antidemo-
       cratic regimes tend to preserve political rights and democratic procedures formally,
       while hollowing out democracy’s substantive conditions – especially human dignity,
       minority rights, the separation of powers, and the rule of law (pp. 37–41). By focus-
       ing on equal co-authorship, Theuns risks overlooking the deeper harms posed by
       what Urbinati (2019, p. 14) describes as ‘radical majoritarianism’.
         Theuns is right to approach Article 7 through the lens of militant democracy and to
       treat restrictions on political rights as an issue of democratic legitimacy. However, by
       assessing Article 7 through a thin, procedural conception of democracy, he concludes
       that it undermines democratic legitimacy because it violates equal chance and the
       all-subjected principle (pp. 76–78). When democracy is understood in thicker terms
       – encompassing human dignity, fundamental rights, and the rule of law – temporary
       restrictions on political rights can be justified as a means of preserving democracy
       rather than a departure from it (Schupmann, 2024). From this perspective, Article 7
       appears as an extension of the values it seeks to protect. Theuns’ rejection of it thus
       reflects a normative imbalance: the democratic values he defends are thick, but the
       evaluative standard he adopts is too thin to sustain them.
         This normative imbalance becomes most apparent by contrasting Theuns’ rejec-
       tion of Article 7 with his endorsement of dissociation. If severing political and legal
       ties with an antidemocratic member is normatively acceptable, it is unclear why a
       temporary restriction of political rights within the Union is not. Dissociation entails
       a permanent loss of membership, depriving citizens of political voice at the EU level
       and the legal and institutional protections membership provides. In contrast, Article 7
       is a limited sanction: it temporarily suspends political rights while preserving mem-
       bership and the EU’s capacity to influence a regressing regime. From the standpoint
       of proportionality and harm, Theuns’ preference for dissociation requires greater
       justification.                                        
         For subjects of a regressing regime, dissociation throws the baby out with the bath-
       water. While expulsion or reconstitution may preserve the EU’s integrity, it does so
       by abandoning those citizens of autocratic regimes committed to democratic values.
       Those most affected are the most vulnerable: minorities, journalists, and the demo-
       cratic opposition. By severing their access to the EU’s legal, economic, and sym-
       bolic protections, dissociation leaves them exposed to rights violations and weakens
       transnational constraints on autocratic power. This undercuts Theuns’ most important
       contribution: recognition of the positive role transnational institutions can play.
         A further difficulty with Theuns’ rejection of Article 7 is its status as a democrati-
       cally authorized constitutional precommitment. Member states consented to Article
       7 ex ante as a condition of EU membership, reflecting a collective decision to bind
       themselves to the values of Article 2 – and to accept sanctions for their violation. To
       dismiss that precommitment risks reducing democracy to immediate majority rule,
       marginalizing the constitutive values of democratic legitimacy.
         Moreover, continued EU membership preserves legal and institutional pathways
       for democratic recovery after antidemocrats lose power. As Scheppele (2025) argues,
       transnational law can function as a ‘rule of law writ large’, providing a scaffold and
       a normative justification for post-autocratic reconstruction. Dissociation forecloses

       these possibilities. By preserving membership while politically sanctioning antidem-
       ocrats, Article 7 preserves the EU’s capacity to contribute to democratic renewal.
         Today, the greater danger to democracy’s survival lies less in overreach than in
       democrats’ hesitation: the reluctance to confront antidemocrats before they entrench
       themselves and hollow out democratic essentials. The past two decades of democratic
       backsliding make clear that democrats can no longer afford to conceive of political
       rights restrictions as categorically illegitimate. Militant measures may be necessary
       to safeguard human dignity, basic rights, and the rule of law. Protecting Democracy
       in Europe addresses exactly this predicament. By broadening the debate about demo-
       cratic self-defence to include the role of transnational institutions, Theuns makes a
       serious and timely contribution. In the process, he presses democrats to reexamine
       how far they are willing to go to defend democracy’s substantive commitments.
