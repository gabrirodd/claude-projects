       Tam’s Communitarianism: Politics, Society and Public Policy is a timely and essen-
       tial intervention in contemporary political and social discourse. It offers a compelling
       synthesis of communitarian thought, moving beyond abstract philosophical debates
       to demonstrate its practical applicability in addressing pressing societal challenges.
       Tam, a seasoned commentator on political and social issues, positions communitari-
       anism not as a nostalgic yearning for a bygone era but as a dynamic and forward-
       looking framework centred on the ideal of inclusive communities. The book’s central
       arguments revolve around three core principles: mutual responsibility, cooperative
       enquiry, and citizen participation. Tam meticulously illustrates how these principles
       can be applied across various sectors – state, business, and voluntary – to foster a
       more just, secure, and flourishing society. Providing a rich context, the book traces
       the historical roots of communitarian ideas, highlighting their enduring relevance in
       navigating the complexities of modern life, marked by the twin threats of market-
       driven fragmentation and authoritarian rigidity.      
         The book’s main arguments are systematically developed across its ten chap-
       ters, each building upon the foundational principles introduced in the initial sec-
       tions. Chapter 1, ‘What is communitarianism?’ (pp. 1–28), lays the groundwork by
       identifying the key sources of communitarian ideas, clarifying misconceptions, and
       introducing the core concerns with developing communities that enable members to
       support and learn from one another. Chapter 2, (pp. 29–60), delves into the practi-
       cal application of communitarian principles, demonstrating how communities can
       effectively address the problems they face. Chapter 3, (pp. 61–88), highlights the
       importance of cooperative enquiry in learning, drawing on the insights of thinkers
       like Dewey and Freire. Chapter 4, (pp. 89–118), examines the productive, civic, and
       moral dimensions of work. Tam critiques prevailing state authoritarian and market

       structural involvement of sectional interests (pp. 91–93). Chapter 5, (pp. 119–148),
       presents a community-based approach to security that integrates both force-based
       and support-based measures. Chapters 6, 7, and 8 extend the application of commu-

       engages with the common criticisms levelled against communitarianism, addressing
       concerns about homogeneity, power dynamics, and the practicalities of implementing
       communitarian principles.                             
         Tam’s methodology is noteworthy for its integrated and interdisciplinary approach,
       which significantly enhances the review’s depth and breadth. He seamlessly weaves
       together insights from political philosophy, sociology, and economics, even draw-
       ing on historical examples from diverse cultures. For instance, in discussing the his-
       torical development of communitarian ideas in Chap. 2 (pp. 35–40), Tam references
       figures ranging from ancient philosophers like Mozi and Aristotle to Enlightenment
       thinkers and 19th-century cooperative movements. This broad historical sweep, com-
       bined with contemporary case studies in chapters like ‘Rethinking Work’ (Chap. 4,
       pp. 100–110) and ‘Communitarian Civil Society’ (Chap. 8, pp. 215–225), provides
       a rich tapestry of evidence supporting his arguments. The inclusion of global case
       studies demonstrates the universal applicability of communitarian principles, moving
       beyond a purely Western-centric perspective. Furthermore, Tam’s engagement with
       pedagogical features like diagrams illustrating conceptual differences (mentioned in
       the book summary) suggests a methodology aimed at clarifying complex philosophi-
       cal concepts for a wider audience, bridging the gap between abstract theory and prac-
       tical application.                                    
         The book’s strengths are numerous. Tam’s ability to synthesise diverse strands
       of communitarian thought into a coherent and accessible framework is a significant
       achievement. The emphasis on ‘inclusive communities’ as the ideal provides a clear
       and compelling vision, moving beyond the potential pitfalls of traditional, poten-
       tially exclusionary, community models (Chap. 1, pp. 20–25). The detailed applica-
       tion of communitarian principles across different sectors – education, work, security,
       government, enterprise, and civil society – demonstrates the practical relevance and
       versatility of Tam’s framework. The inclusion of concrete case studies throughout
       the book serves to illustrate the feasibility and positive outcomes of communitar-
       ian approaches in real-world settings (see, for example, Chap. 4, pp. 100–110, and
       Chap. 8, pp. 215–225). Furthermore, Tam’s direct engagement with and refutation of
       common criticisms in Chap. 9 (pp. 235–266) strengthens the book’s arguments and
       anticipates potential objections. The book’s pedagogical features, such as the time-
       line and diagrams, enhance its utility as a learning resource.
         While the entire book offers valuable insights, certain chapters stand out for their
       novelty and originality compared to other studies in the field. Chapter 5, ‘Rethinking
       Security’ (pp. 119–148), is particularly innovative. While traditional approaches to
       security often focus on state-centric measures and military power, Tam’s community-
       based approach, integrating both force-based and support-based security, is a refresh-
       ing and crucial contribution. His emphasis on cooperatively setting up processes
       for threat communication, detection, and harm rectification, along with the inclu-
       sion of restorative justice, offers a more holistic and human-centred understanding

       of security (pp. 121–128). Another distinctive chapter is Chap. 7, ‘Communitarian
       enterprise’ (pp. 179–206). In a world dominated by discussions of corporate social
       responsibility often framed within individualist market logic, Tam’s argument for
       businesses to be managed with the informed engagement of all stakeholders – inves-
       tors, suppliers, workers, and customers – is a powerful and potentially transformative
       idea (pp. 181–190). This goes beyond mere ethical considerations to advocate for a
       fundamental shift in the governance structure of enterprises, viewing them as integral
       parts of the wider community with responsibilities that extend beyond maximising
       shareholder profit.                                   
         Despite its many strengths, the book does have a few weaknesses. While Tam
       effectively refutes many common criticisms in Chap. 9 (pp. 235–266), some read-
       ers might desire a deeper engagement with the practical challenges of implementing
       large-scale communitarian reforms in complex, diverse societies. For instance, while
       the book provides compelling examples of local and regional initiatives, scaling these
       up to national or global levels presents significant logistical and political hurdles that
       could benefit from more detailed exploration. Furthermore, while the book advocates
       balancing individual freedom and community responsibility, the precise mechanisms
       for resolving potential conflicts between individual rights and community norms in
       specific contexts could be further elaborated. While Tam emphasises the importance
       of ‘uncoerced’ interaction (Chap. 1, p. 20), the potential for social pressure or the tyr-
       anny of the majority within communities, even inclusive ones, warrants careful con-
       sideration and perhaps more explicit safeguards. While the book acknowledges the
       ‘problematic issues of power’ within communities (Chap. 9, p. 237), a more in-depth
       analysis of how power dynamics might be addressed within a communitarian frame-
       work, particularly concerning marginalised groups, could strengthen the argument.
         Engaging with Tam’s arguments provides valuable insights into the potential
       of communitarianism as a guiding philosophy for navigating the challenges of the
       twenty-first century. His emphasis on mutual responsibility and cooperative enquiry
       offers a compelling alternative to the prevailing individualism that often characterises
       contemporary societies. By highlighting the interconnectedness of individuals within
       communities, Tam reminds us that our well-being is inextricably linked to the well-
       being of others. His framework provides a powerful antidote to the fragmentation
       and isolation that can result from unchecked market forces and the erosion of social
       bonds.                                                
         The book fits well within a broader literature that critiques the limitations of both
       liberal individualism and state socialism. It builds upon the work of prominent com-
       munitarian thinkers like Amitai Etzioni, Michael Sandel, and Alasdair MacIntyre,
       while also drawing on a wider range of intellectual traditions. Tam’s work can be
       seen as extending the practical application of communitarian ideas beyond the purely
       philosophical realm, offering concrete policy proposals and examples of success-
       ful implementation. It stands in contrast to purely theoretical works by providing a
       roadmap for how communitarian principles can be translated into tangible social and
       political change. Compared to other studies that might focus solely on the philosophi-
       cal underpinnings of communitarianism (e.g., Sandel’s Liberalism and the Limits of
       Justice), Tam’s book distinguishes itself by its explicit focus on politics, society, and
       public policy (as indicated in the title and throughout Chaps. 3–8). The target audi-

         In conclusion, Henry Tam’s Communitarianism: Politics, Society and Public
       Policy is a groundbreaking and essential work that offers a compelling vision for
       a more just and flourishing society. By synthesising diverse communitarian ideas
       and demonstrating their practical application across various sectors, Tam provides
       a powerful framework for navigating the challenges of the twenty-first century. The
       book’s emphasis on inclusive communities based on mutual responsibility, coopera-
       tive enquiry, and citizen participation offers a vital alternative to the limitations of
       both individualism and authoritarianism. Tam’s work serves as a crucial reminder
       that the pursuit of individual well-being is intrinsically linked to the health and vital-
       ity of the communities to which we belong, and it provides a roadmap for building a
       future where belonging and shared responsibility are at the heart of our political and
       social life.
