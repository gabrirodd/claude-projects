# Deviation notes — CPT corpus

Papers that resist clean typology or sit at type boundaries.

---

## T1/T2 boundary: case constitutive vs. case as medium

**White (2025) — Imagining democratic insurgency**
- Classified: T1
- The 9-move grammar (including counter_objection_response and reflexive move) puts it formally in T1.
- But: The historical case (democratic enthusiasm as political sublime) is recruited to support a pre-formed Arendtian-Kantian framework. The framework is not derived from the case — it is applied to it.
- What this reveals: The length of a move sequence is not a reliable indicator of T1 vs. T2. The real criterion is the direction of the argument: does the case generate the framework (T1) or does the framework interpret the case (T2)?

## T1/T4 boundary: interpretive case vs. qualitative evidence

**Roch, Balinhas, Mondon (2025) — Polarisation narrative**
- Classified: T1 (normative_with_qualitative_empirical, but with T1 move grammar)
- The discourse analysis includes content frequency counts (NYT articles; academic publications) — borderline quantitative.
- The normative critique of polarisation discourse could be made without the counts; the counts support but do not constitute the argument.
- What this reveals: The T1/T4 distinction depends on whether empirical evidence is recruited as *grounds* for the claim (T4) or as *illustration* of a conceptually prior claim (T1). Roch et al. are formally T1 because the framework_proposal precedes the empirical application.

**Prison abolition exchange — Terwiel et al. (2026)**
- Classified: T1
- Carceral statistics (Prison Policy Initiative data; ICE detention figures) are present.
- But: The abolitionist argument is normatively prior — the statistics are recruited to document the scope of the problem, not to ground the abolitionist framework itself.
- What this reveals: In politically committed normative work, empirical evidence functions as *documentation* of a problem rather than *grounds* for the normative conclusion. This is a recognisable argumentative move in critical theory: the statistics serve the diagnosis, not the framework.

## T5/T2 boundary: implicit vs. explicit normative assessment

**Arlen (2025) — Democratic elitism: the founding myth of American political science**
- Classified: T5
- The genealogical reconstruction of democratic elitism's role in founding American political science carries an unmistakeable critical charge.
- The title ("founding myth") announces a debunking project, but the text stops short of stating what the alternative should be.
- What this reveals: Genealogy in political theory is rarely normatively neutral. The choice of what to reconstruct, and the framing of the reconstruction as "myth-making," is itself normatively loaded. T5 captures the formal absence of a normative conclusion; it cannot capture the normative charge embedded in the genealogical method itself.

**Manga (2025) — Senghor: writings on politics**
- Classified: T5
- The implicit normative claim (Senghor deserves more Anglophone attention; negritude political thought is undervalued) is present but never stated.
- This is structurally different from Arlen: where Arlen's normativity is embedded in the critical framing, Manga's is embedded in the mere act of introduction (what one chooses to introduce is a normative act).

## Papers in a class of one

**Bonotti & Nwokora (2025) — Free speech and social change**
- Classified: T4
- The only paper with an explicit method_justification move.
- The most methodologically elaborate paper in the corpus: double immanent critique, counter_objection_response, reflexive move, strong conclusion.
- Sits between T1 (in its argumentative elaboration) and T4 (in its evidential grounding).
- What this reveals: A research article that takes both empirical grounding and normative rigour seriously will tend to have the longest move sequences and the most complex internal grammar. This is methodologically exemplary but also genre-atypical for CPT.

**Rodrigues (2025) — In the shadow of minority rights**
- Classified: T4
- The only paper with genuine ethnographic evidence (fieldwork with Muslim women activists in Mumbai) as the primary object.
- In the corpus, this is classified as a book review — but it reads more like a research article that happens to review a book.
- The ethnographic material is generative, not illustrative: the reviewed book's argument is assessed against fieldwork, not against other theoretical positions.

## Critical exchanges as a genre problem

All three critical exchanges in the corpus (Buxton et al. 2025; Terwiel et al. 2026; Machin et al. 2026) are treated as single profiles, suppressing the internal variation across authors. Each exchange contains:
- A lead piece that does the primary argumentative work
- Responses that modify, extend, or challenge the lead piece
- A reply (sometimes)

The Buxton et al. Shklar exchange is the most internally heterogeneous: five contributors have distinct normative positions on Shklar's liberalism of fear. Treating it as a single fully_normative profile loses the agonistic structure. A more granular analysis would profile each contribution separately.

The Terwiel et al. prison abolition exchange is the most unified: all seven contributors share the abolitionist normative premise; they differ on aspects of the framework (democracy, disability, decolonisation, prefiguration). The single profile classification is less distorting here.

## Move sequence anomaly

**Martel (2026) — Disembodiment**
- Move sequence begins: `interpretive_proposition → case_introduction → ...`
- This is the only paper in the corpus that opens with `interpretive_proposition` rather than `puzzle_diagnosis`.
- The reviewer opens with an enthused normative verdict ("a brilliant and highly timely book") rather than a problem or gap — which is itself a rhetorical move (the reviewer's judgment functions as the opening claim, not a diagnosis of a problem).
- The schema vocabulary handles this with `interpretive_proposition` but it is worth noting as a genre variation: the advocacy review opens with verdict, not puzzle.
