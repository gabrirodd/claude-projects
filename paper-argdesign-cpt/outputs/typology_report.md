# Argument typology — Contemporary Political Theory corpus

## Corpus overview
- **51 papers analysed**
- **Year range**: 2025–2026
- **Journal**: Contemporary Political Theory (Springer/Palgrave)
- **Genre breakdown**: 35 book reviews (4pp), 2 review essays (4–6pp), 3 full-length research articles (19–21pp), 3 longer articles (19–20pp), 3 critical exchanges (28pp, 5–7 authors each)
- **Intellectual tradition**: The corpus represents political theory and political philosophy in the Anglo-American tradition, with strong strands of critical theory, feminist/postcolonial theory, deliberative democracy, egalitarianism, and comparative political theory. The journal bridges normative political philosophy and critical/radical approaches.

---

## Epistemic landscape

The corpus is overwhelmingly normative: of 51 papers, 30 (59%) deploy a normative argument via an interpretive case (book, film, event, text), 9 (18%) are fully normative without any case or empirical material, and 7 (14%) combine qualitative empirical evidence with normative argument. Only 5 (10%) are purely interpretive without an explicit normative conclusion.

```
Epistemic mode distribution (n=51):
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┬─────┬───────┐
│ Mode                                         │  N  │   %   │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────┼─────┼───────┤
│ normative_with_interpretive_case             │  30 │  59%  │
│ fully_normative                              │   9 │  18%  │
│ normative_with_qualitative_empirical         │   7 │  14%  │
│ purely_interpretive_hermeneutic              │   5 │  10%  │
│ normative_with_quantitative_empirical        │   0 │   0%  │
│ primarily_empirical_qualitative              │   0 │   0%  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┴─────┴───────┘
```

What this reveals about the field's methodological self-understanding: *Contemporary Political Theory* operates overwhelmingly within the normative paradigm, treating political theory as a discipline whose primary output is normative argument. The near-absence of quantitative or primarily empirical work signals that the journal does not understand itself as social science. The dominance of `normative_with_interpretive_case` — especially visible in book reviews — reflects the genre: a review reads the reviewed book as a case that confirms, complicates, or challenges a normative position. Even the 5 purely interpretive pieces carry implicit normative stakes; they are the outliers, not the norm.

---

## Common epistemological ground

All 51 papers share several background assumptions regardless of their specific type: (1) normative claims require argumentative justification, not empirical demonstration alone; (2) intellectual disagreement is the engine of knowledge — almost every paper positions itself against some identified rival; (3) interpretation of texts, events, and cases is never neutral — it is always in service of a claim; (4) the concepts deployed (freedom, democracy, alienation, sovereignty) are treated as politically contested rather than given, so definitional labour is always substantive. The field's implicit methodology is therefore one of *motivated conceptual reconstruction*: the analyst identifies a gap or distortion in the existing theoretical landscape, introduces conceptual resources (from philosophy, history, or empirical material used interpretively) that reframe the issue, and derives a normative conclusion that advances the debate.

## Type 1 — Thick normative argument with constitutive case

### Core logic (L1 — broadest level)
The paper identifies a theoretical or political problem that cannot be resolved within existing frameworks, recruits a case (text, event, historical episode) that does genuine argumentative work, and derives a strong normative conclusion from the interaction of framework and case. The case is not decoration; it changes what the argument can claim.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → framework_proposal → case_introduction → case_development → immanent_critique → normative_derivation → conclusion_strong`

*Puzzle_diagnosis* names the gap or crisis. *Rival_mapping* surveys inadequate responses. *Framework_proposal* introduces the new conceptual tools. *Case_introduction* and *case_development* apply the framework to specific material. *Immanent_critique* exposes the limits of rivals from within the case. *Normative_derivation* draws the political-theoretical conclusion. *Conclusion_strong* states the claim without hedge.

### Detailed grammar (L3 — finest level)
The logic of Type 1 is that the case is not selected to illustrate a pre-formed point but to generate theoretical insight. The paper needs the case to show something that the framework alone cannot demonstrate. This means the case_development section cannot be summarised without loss — it produces new content rather than applying existing content. The move from case_development to normative_derivation is the most theoretically dense moment: it is here that the paper makes its core argumentative bet. Type 1 papers almost always deploy immanent_critique somewhere in the sequence because they must show not only what their framework achieves but what rivals cannot see. The strong conclusion is a feature: these papers close with a clear claim about what democracy requires, what justice demands, or what politics should abandon.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: normative_with_interpretive_case (or normative_with_qualitative_empirical when evidence is more than illustrative)
- Evidence role: generative (constitutive of the argument)
- Case type: political_event, philosophical_text, or historical_event
- Move count: 8–9 (longest in the corpus)

### Canonical examples

**Race still matters — Ghumkhor, Gillespie, McMorrow (2025)**
Why canonical: The paper performs the complete 9-move grammar with the most explicit logical articulation of the sequence. Conspiracy theories function as the case through which (post-)racial ideology is diagnosed and abolitionist race theory is derived. The case is constitutive: the argument cannot be formulated without reading through the specific conspiracy discourse.

**Reframing restitution — Zolkos (2026)**
Why canonical: Uses Esposito and Mbembe as the framework recruited to interpret restitutive justice speech acts. The refractory speech acts are constitutive cases that generate the normative claim about persons-things binary. Strong normative conclusion. 8-move sequence with full immanent critique of the legal discourse.

**Hans Jonas and Sophocles — Folit-Weinberg & van 't Klooster (2026)** (variant)
How it deviates: The immanent_critique of Jonas precedes the case_introduction, reversing the standard order. This makes the case (Sophocles) arrive as a positive alternative rather than a site of critique.

**Prison abolition exchange — Terwiel et al. (2026)** (qualitative empirical variant)
How it deviates: The empirical material (carceral statistics, movement analysis) supplements the normative argument rather than replacing it. Epistemic mode shifts to normative_with_qualitative_empirical.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 1 differs from Type 2 most sharply in the *role* of the case. In Type 2, the case can be summarised without changing the argument; the argument would survive its removal (with some loss of concreteness). In Type 1, removing the case would leave the normative claim ungrounded — it would be assertion rather than argument. The diagnostic sign: if the case_development section does more than apply a pre-formed framework, it is Type 1. If it merely confirms the framework, it is Type 2.

## Type 2 — Standard normative review: case as medium

### Core logic (L1 — broadest level)
A book or event is introduced, developed, and assessed from a normative standpoint. The case is the medium through which the normative argument is conducted, not the source of the argument. The reviewer's position is established before or alongside the case, not derived from it.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → case_introduction → case_development → immanent_critique → conclusion_open`

*Puzzle_diagnosis* locates the book in a debate or identifies what is at stake. *Case_introduction* presents the reviewed work. *Case_development* reconstructs its central argument(s). *Immanent_critique* assesses strengths and limits from within the reviewer's normative commitments. *Conclusion_open* leaves the debate open, directing the burden of further argument to the reader.

### Detailed grammar (L3 — finest level)
Type 2 is the dominant form in the corpus (roughly 25 papers). It is the home genre of the book review and is characterised by three features. First, the opening normative framing precedes and frames the case; it does not emerge from it. Second, the immanent_critique is directed *at* the reviewed book from a position the reviewer already holds; it reveals the book's limits rather than revising the reviewer's framework. Third, the conclusion is invariably open: the reviewer does not claim to have settled the question. The variation within Type 2 is substantial: some reviewers align closely with the reviewed book (advocacy reviews: Berg-Sørensen, Martel, Gökarıksel); others produce genuine critical engagement (Abolafia, Tampio, Jing, Schupmann, Niesen). The distinction is in the strength and direction of the immanent critique — but the underlying grammar is the same.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: normative_with_interpretive_case (and occasionally fully_normative for reviews that argue independently of the case)
- Evidence role: illustrative (decorates or tests a position already held)
- Case medium: philosophical_text is most common; political_event and historical_event also appear
- Move count: 4–6 (typically 5)

### Canonical examples

**Reconsidering cosmopolitanism and forgiveness — de Jongh (2025)**
Why canonical: Clean 6-move sequence with a framework_proposal derived from assessing the reviewed book. The reviewer advances an Arendt/Derrida-based alternative rather than merely expositing.

**Remapping sovereignty — Bruyneel (2025)**
Why canonical: The reviewer performs genuine immanent critique of an Indigenous political thought text, showing where the reviewed book's framework fails by its own standards, then leaves the normative question open.

**Imagining democratic insurgency — White (2025)** (extended variant)
How it deviates: White's paper is 9 moves — it is more elaborate than a standard Type 2 review, including counter_objection_response and a reflexive move. It sits at the boundary between Type 2 and Type 1.

**Freedom for All — Berg-Sørensen (2026)** (minimal variant)
How it deviates: The shortest form — 4 moves, no immanent critique, purely expository. The advocacy variant of Type 2.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 2 and Type 3 are separated by the presence of a case. Type 3 papers argue in purely conceptual space; their rivals are theoretical positions, not books or events. Type 2 and Type 1 are separated by what the case does: medium (T2) vs. constitutive (T1). The Abolafia review nearly crosses into T1 because it performs a reflexive move (meta_reflexive_qualification) — but the argument could still survive without the specific case of Landemore's book.

## Type 3 — Fully normative conceptual argument

### Core logic (L1 — broadest level)
The paper argues entirely within conceptual-normative space. It identifies a gap or distortion in the theoretical literature, introduces conceptual distinctions, performs immanent critique of rival positions, and derives a normative conclusion — without recourse to empirical evidence or an interpretive case doing substantive argumentative work.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → conceptual_distinction → immanent_critique → normative_derivation → conclusion`

*Puzzle_diagnosis* identifies the theoretical problem. *Rival_mapping* surveys the inadequate responses. *Conceptual_distinction* introduces the analytical tools. *Immanent_critique* shows rivals' internal failures. *Normative_derivation* draws the positive claim. Conclusion varies (strong or open).

### Detailed grammar (L3 — finest level)
Type 3 is the 'pure' mode of political theory in the analytic tradition: argument without case, theory without empirical tether. This does not mean the papers are ahistorical — Pierer (2026) engages deeply with Marxist tradition — but the argumentative structure moves entirely within the conceptual register. The crucial move is *conceptual_distinction*: this is what enables the paper to claim it has not merely asserted a position but shown it. The distinction does work that the rival cannot undo without abandoning their own premises. The Buxton et al. Shklar exchange is unusual in that a 5-author critical exchange operates in fully normative mode — each contribution develops a framework position without the need for an external interpretive case.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: fully_normative
- Evidence role: absent
- Case type: none (or if present, functioning only as background reference)
- Move count: 5–8

### Canonical examples

**Normative sociology and normative behaviorism — Lægaard (2025)**
Why canonical: The most methodologically self-conscious paper in the corpus. Lægaard argues at the meta-level about the relationship between empirical social science and normative political theory — entirely without empirical evidence, using conceptual distinction to chart a research agenda. The double immanent_critique (two successive rounds) is the paper's distinctive feature.

**Hybrid alienation — Pierer (2026)**
Why canonical: Full 8-move sequence in purely normative mode. The synthesis of Marxist alienation theory with feminist political economy and political ecology is achieved entirely through conceptual reconstruction. No empirical data; the argument about reproductive labour and the Anthropocene is normative throughout.

**Judith Shklar exchange — Buxton et al. (2025)** (multi-author variant)
How it deviates: Critical exchange format means multiple normative positions are held in tension rather than resolved. The conclusion is an open research agenda rather than a strong normative claim.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 3 and Type 2 are separated by the presence of an interpretive case doing substantive work. Several book reviews in this corpus are classified as Type 3 rather than Type 2 (Rozenburg, Hyde & Saracino, Royer, Fumagalli, Marneros) because the reviewed book functions merely as a reference point or label — the reviewer argues a position that could be stated without reference to the specific book.

## Type 4 — Empirically grounded normative argument

### Core logic (L1 — broadest level)
The paper uses qualitative empirical evidence — interviews, discourse analysis, ethnography, policy analysis — as a constitutive (or near-constitutive) element in a normative argument. The normative conclusion is reached partly through the empirical material, which cannot simply be removed without changing the argument.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → empirical_presentation → immanent_critique → framework_proposal → normative_derivation → conclusion`

*Puzzle_diagnosis* locates the empirical and normative stakes. *Rival_mapping* surveys inadequate theoretical responses. *Empirical_presentation* introduces the evidence. *Immanent_critique* deploys the evidence against rivals. *Framework_proposal* articulates the positive position informed by the evidence. *Normative_derivation* draws the conclusion. The move from empirical to normative is the critical transition.

### Detailed grammar (L3 — finest level)
Type 4 papers share a characteristic structural tension: they must negotiate the transition from empirical findings to normative conclusions, and they do so in different ways. Bonotti & Nwokora (2025) is the most methodologically transparent: it includes method_justification and two rounds of immanent critique before the framework proposal, explicitly arguing that qualitative evidence about social change grounds the normative claim about free speech. Rodrigues (2025) is the most empirically ambitious: ethnographic fieldwork with Muslim women activists in Mumbai generates the normative critique of liberal minority rights frameworks — the evidence is genuinely constitutive. Roch et al. (2025) sits at the boundary: quantitative-adjacent discourse analysis (content counts of 'polarisation' in NYT; academic publication frequency) supports a normative critique of the polarisation narrative, but the normative argument could be made without the counts.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: normative_with_qualitative_empirical
- Evidence role: supportive to constitutive (varies within type)
- Empirical methods: discourse analysis, ethnography, policy analysis, qualitative case analysis
- Move count: 5–9

### Canonical examples

**Free speech and social change — Bonotti & Nwokora (2025)**
Why canonical: The most methodologically elaborate paper in the corpus. Includes method_justification (rare in the corpus). Qualitative evidence about social change processes (individualism, migration, post-truth, cognitive bias) is explicitly recruited to ground a normative public-reason approach to free speech. Double immanent critique and counter_objection_response.

**In the shadow of minority rights — Rodrigues (2025)**
Why canonical: Ethnographic fieldwork (BMMA, MCMT activists in Mumbai) is not merely illustrative but generative of the normative claim about decolonising gender and liberal minority rights frameworks.

**A critique of the polarisation narrative — Roch, Balinhas, Mondon (2025)** (boundary variant)
How it deviates: The quantitative-adjacent evidence (newspaper content analysis) is supportive rather than constitutive — the normative critique of polarisation discourse could stand without the counts. Sits near the T1/T4 boundary.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 4 and Type 1 are separated by the nature of the case: Type 1 uses an interpretive case (text, event) through a hermeneutic relationship; Type 4 uses qualitative empirical evidence through an evidential relationship. The test: would the paper describe its material as "evidence for" its claim (T4) or as "a reading of" its material (T1)?

## Type 5 — Interpretive reconstruction without normative conclusion

### Core logic (L1 — broadest level)
The paper reads a text, an intellectual life, or a historical tradition with the aim of understanding or reconstructing it — without drawing an explicit normative conclusion about what ought to be done or what political theory should claim. The interpretive work is the contribution.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → case_introduction → case_development → genealogical_reconstruction → conclusion_open`

*Puzzle_diagnosis* frames the interpretive question (often: why has this figure or tradition been misunderstood or neglected?). *Case_introduction* presents the text or figure. *Case_development* reconstructs the argument or trajectory. *Genealogical_reconstruction* (when present) situates the subject in intellectual-historical context. *Conclusion_open* leaves the normative implication to the reader.

### Detailed grammar (L3 — finest level)
Type 5 is the smallest type in the corpus (5 papers) and the most internally heterogeneous. Kellner (Pollock biography) and Engelking (Kojève biography) are pure intellectual history with no normative ambition. Manga (Senghor review) is expository, presenting an underread corpus to Anglophone audiences. Tang (Arendt poems) is the most purely literary: the review of a poetry collection performs close literary reading without political-theoretical argument. Arlen (Democratic elitism) is the most normatively adjacent: the genealogical reconstruction of democratic elitism's founding role in American political science carries an implicit critique, but it is not stated as a normative conclusion. This normative adjacency makes Arlen the most interesting Type 5 paper: it hovers at the boundary between T5 and T2.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: purely_interpretive_hermeneutic
- Evidence role: absent (interpretive only)
- Case type: philosophical_text, literary_text, or historical_event
- Normative stake level: low (typically)

### Canonical examples

**Friedrich Pollock — Kellner (2025)**
Why canonical: Pure intellectual biography, no normative claim. Genealogical reconstruction of Pollock's role in the Frankfurt School without argument about what the Frankfurt School should have done differently.

**What remains (Arendt poems) — Tang (2025)**
Why canonical: The most purely interpretive piece in the corpus. Literary close reading of Arendt's poetry; no political theory argument is derived.

**Democratic elitism — Arlen (2025)** (normatively adjacent variant)
How it deviates: The genealogical reconstruction of democratic elitism in American political science carries an unmistakeable critical charge — the normative implication (democratic elitism is a founding myth that should be exposed) is present but unstated. Sits at the T5/T2 boundary.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 5 and Type 2 are separated by the presence of an explicit normative assessment. If the review states what the reviewed book's limits are from the reviewer's normative position, it is T2. If it reconstructs without assessing, it is T5. The boundary is genuinely porous: Manga (Senghor) is T5, but the implicit normative claim (Senghor deserves more Anglophone attention) is present — it simply does not resolve into a theoretical position.

## Deviation map

### Papers that partially fit multiple types

**Imagining democratic insurgency — White (2025)**
Fits T1 and T2. The paper's 9-move sequence (including counter_objection_response) gives it the grammar of T1, but the reviewed historical case (enthusiastic democratic insurgency) is used to support a pre-formed Arendtian framework rather than to generate it. Classified T1 on formal grounds; substantively closer to T2.

**A critique of the polarisation narrative — Roch, Balinhas, Mondon (2025)**
Fits T1 and T4. The discourse analysis has a quantitative dimension (content counts) that would push toward T4, but the normative output is a framework proposal (T1). The evidence is illustrative of a normative critique rather than constitutive of it.

**Prison abolition exchange — Terwiel et al. (2026)**
Fits T1 and T4. The carceral statistics are deployed in support of the abolitionist framework but are not constitutive of it — the normative argument for prison abolition is prior to the evidence. Classified T1 because the move sequence has the full T1 grammar and the conclusion is strong.

**Arlen (2025) — Democratic elitism**
Fits T5 and T2. The genealogical reconstruction carries an implicit normative charge that nearly pushes it into T2, but the paper never states what the correct alternative is.

### Papers that resist typology

**Bonotti & Nwokora (2025) — Free speech and social change**
This is the most methodologically distinctive paper in the corpus: it includes method_justification (no other paper does), performs two rounds of immanent critique, uses qualitative evidence about structural social changes as genuine grounds for a normative claim, and concludes with a strong conclusion. No other paper in the corpus has this grammar. It is classified T4 but sits in a class of one.

**Buxton et al. (2025) — Judith Shklar exchange**
A critical exchange in fully normative mode — unusual because critical exchanges in this corpus tend to use interpretive cases (prison abolition exchange, imagination exchange). The Shklar exchange is a 5-author conceptual debate that looks more like a symposium in analytic philosophy than a critical exchange in political theory.

### What the deviations reveal

Two live tensions in the field's methodology are visible in the deviations. First, the tension between *richness* and *closure*: papers that have the most analytically elaborate grammar (Bonotti & Nwokora; White; Roch et al.) tend to be the hardest to classify, because elaboration disrupts genre conventions. The field rewards complexity, but the norms of genre (article, review, exchange) impose structural constraints that complexity strains against. Second, the tension between *normative ambition* and *hermeneutic care*: Type 5 papers are those where the interpreter restrains the normative impulse in favour of fidelity to the material — but almost none of them fully succeed in suppressing the normative charge. The implicit normativity of interpretation in political theory is pervasive enough that genuinely neutral reconstruction is nearly impossible to sustain.


## Methodological notes

**Confidence levels**: The typology assignments for the 35 book reviews are high-confidence (the genre constraints make classification relatively stable). The 3 critical exchanges and 8 longer articles have medium confidence: their multi-authored or longer-form structure means that internal variation is suppressed by treating them as single profiles.

**Most uncertain profiles**: Arlen (T5/T2 boundary), Roch et al. (T1/T4 boundary), White (T1/T2 boundary), Boscov-Ellen (T2 because the empirical_presentation in the review is borrowing from Fraser's own empirical work, not independent evidence). The Hyde & Saracino review is classified T3 rather than T2 because the reviewer argues a position about legitimacy theory that is independent of the reviewed book.
