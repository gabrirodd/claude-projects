## 3. Puzzle or research question

How are neoreactionary (NRx) political ideas — those of Yarvin, Land, Thiel, and Friedman — being materially instantiated in socio-technical architectures, specifically in Urbit? What does Urbit reveal about the post-neoliberal politics of exit and the concept of "dynamic geography"? The paper argues there is a lacuna in existing literatures on AI, blockchain governance, and sovereignty: none has engaged with the radical NRx theories of "redecentralization" and exit that are being inscribed into software.

---

## 4. Core argument

NRx is not merely a fringe ideological curiosity but a politically and technically consequential formation. Its core logic — replacing democratic voice with sovereign exit, and nation-states with "gov-corps" in a competitive patchwork — is being materially inscribed into Urbit's architecture. Urbit is analysed as a **hyperstitional object**: a fictional-technical assemblage that bootstraps its own reality by instantiating NRx governance principles in code. The paper's central claim is that Urbit's "redecentralization" is a contradiction in terms: despite its libertarian rhetoric of user sovereignty, galaxy ownership is concentrated among Tlön Corp (~37%), employees, and Thiel's venture capital, reproducing hierarchical property relations under a cryptographic guise. The only constitutional right in Urbit is exit; there is no democratic voice. The paper concludes that NRx architectures of exit are politically dangerous precisely because they oversimplify — aligning governance with computational "first principles" — and because they possess a "memetic, almost infectious quality."

## 5. Key concepts and their definitions

**Exit / Voice / Loyalty (Hirschman 1970):** NRx inverts Hirschman's framework. Democratic voice is slow and distorting; warm loyalty blocks efficient correction; exit becomes the normatively superior mechanism. Land cites Friedman: "free exit is so important that it [is] the only Universal Human Right."

**Gov-corp:** A society run as a business (corporation). Regulated not by citizens' voice but by their capacity to exit as consumers in a free market for governance. Yarvin's core institutional proposal.

**Patchwork:** Yarvin's "new operating system for the world" — thousands of sovereign mini-states, each governed by a joint-stock corporation, with no voice, only exit. "The design is all 'exit,' no 'voice.'"

**Dynamic geography:** Patri Friedman's material architecture of exit. Modular seasteads that can detach and join other floating cities, forcing governments to compete for mobile citizens. Smith and Burrows' term for this spatial logic.

**Hyperstition:** Land's concept, central to this paper. "A positive feedback circuit including culture as a component... by their very existence as ideas, [hyperstitions] function causally to bring about their own reality." Distinguished from superstition (merely false belief). Examples: fictional cyberspace contributing to investment that made it real; seasteads as cultural-political strategy even if impracticable; Urbit itself. The Dark Enlightenment is Land's "hyper-reflexive attempt at constructing a hyperstitional object."

**Redecentralization:** Yarvin/Urbit's term for rebuilding the internet from scratch to reverse the centralisation wrought by MEGACORP (Google, Facebook). In practice, Smith and Burrows show, it reproduces concentrated ownership.

**Computationalist ontology:** Yarvin's worldview — "rational, rule-bound, and solvable... software and hardware are the dominant metaphors for society." Governance is an engineering problem solvable from first principles.

**The Cathedral:** NRx term for the progressive institutional complex (universities, civil service, media) that inculcates "false belief" in democracy. An inverted Althusserian ISA.

**Based Deleuzianism:** Murphy's (2019) term for Land's position — a reactionary reading of Deleuze/Guattari that strips out emancipatory content and retains antihumanism and deterritorialisation in service of right-accelerationism.

## 6. On the Borges connection

The paper cites Tseng (2019) on the naming of Tlön Corp:

> *"'Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius,' the Borges story where Yarvin's company gets its name, describes a secret society, Orbis Tertius, that architects an entirely new world, Tlön, by publishing an encyclopaedia describing it. Over time, bits of this fictional world begin to emerge in the real world, consuming it, such that '[t]he world will be Tlön.'"*

This is the paper's explicit acknowledgement of the hyperstitional logic embedded in the Borges reference. Yarvin's naming of Tlön Corp is not decorative but programmatic: it declares that Urbit is the encyclopaedia that will produce a new world.

## 7. On Urbit's architecture (technical details relevant to the paper)

- **Three layers (as of 2019):** Arvo (OS and kernel); Azimuth (identity layer, based on Ethereum); Aegean (abstract network of decentralised societies).
- **Address hierarchy:** 256 galaxies → 65,280 stars → ~4 billion planets. Ships are finite cryptographic assets accruing reputation and value. "If Bitcoin is money, and Ethereum is law, Urbit is land."
- **Galaxy ownership (concentrated):** Tlön ~37%; Urbit.org 19%; Tlön employees 15% (Yarvin owns 24 galaxies); outside investors 13%; donors 14%.
- **Constitution:** Modelled on Roman and Anglo-American structures (consulate, senate, congress, assembly). "Exit is the only constitutional right."
- **Kelvin versioning:** Descending releases toward absolute zero when protocols "congeal" — an inbuilt conservatism against protocol change.
- **50,000 lines of code.** No patents; positions itself as next open-source superstructure.
