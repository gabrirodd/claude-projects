## 3. Puzzle or research question

A gap in comparative Poe-Borges scholarship: the Poe influence on Borges has been studied extensively but confined to detective fiction, *Arthur Gordon Pym*, and Poe's literary theory. The possible relationship between Poe's Gothic tales of terror and Borges's metaphysical fictions has been almost entirely ignored. The paper asks: what substantive intertextual parallels and divergences can be identified through a side-by-side reading of "The Fall of the House of Usher" (1839) and "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" (1940/1944)?

---

## 4. Core argument

**a. Structural parallel:** Both narratives enact the gradual encroachment of a parallel reality on everyday reality. In Poe, the narrator is progressively drawn into Roderick Usher's delusional world; in Borges, the planet Tlön seeps into and eventually displaces the actual world. In both cases the mechanism is experiential immersion — the narrator cannot resist the alternative universe's logic.

**b. Textuality as medium:** In both texts, the alternative reality is constituted and transmitted through textuality — libraries, encyclopaedias, learned volumes. Usher's library (twelve real books, one invented: *The Mad Trist*) operates under what Poe calls the "character of phantasm"; Borges's narrator immediately echoes this in describing Uqbar's literature as being "of fantasy" (*de carácter fantástico*). The paper argues this phrase functions as an overt intertextual signal — Borges gesturing back to Poe via this shared formulation. The encyclopaedic dimension latent in Usher's library becomes explicit and structurally central in "Tlön," where the narrative turns on the First and Second Encyclopaedia of Tlön (forty volumes, then one hundred).

**c. Covert intertextual chain in "Tlön"'s closing sentence:** The reference to Browne's *Urn Burial* creates a spiralling chain of intertextuality — Poe → Browne → Suetonius → Ovid → Homer — that Borges covertly inserts as a half-hidden tribute to his American precursor. The key link: the exact passage of Browne that Borges and Bioy Casares actually translated in 1944 contains the epigraph Poe used for "The Murders in the Rue Morgue." This is argued as evidence of Borges's deliberate, semi-concealed homage to Poe embedded in the very act of narrative closure.

**d. Divergence on disintegration vs. replacement:** Poe's parallel world is catastrophic and disintegrative (house, family, and library all disappear into the tarn — Lawrence's "disintegrative vibration"). Borges's is reintegrative: Tlön does not destroy the world but replaces it, substituting a more ordered, imaginary reality for the chaotic real one. Poe's parallel reality is pathological (delusional, paranoid in a Freudian sense); Borges's is cognitive and almost utopian — orderly enough that the narrator asks, "How could one do other than submit to Tlön?"

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Comparative close reading combined with intertextual source tracing.** The paper proceeds through:

- **Structural comparison:** identifying parallel narrative moves (gradual encroachment, textual mediation) across both stories
- **Lexical / phrase-level comparison:** identifying specific verbal echoes ("character of phantasm" / "*literatura de carácter fantástico*")
- **Source tracing:** following the chain of references (Browne → Poe epigraph → Borges/Bioy translation → Quevedo) to demonstrate that Borges's closing sentence contains a covert intertextual link to Poe
- **Bibliographic annotation:** cataloguing Usher's library in detail (twelve real texts across seven national literatures and four languages) to argue that Poe's encyclopaedic ambition directly prefigures Borges's explicit encyclopaedism

The paper is not theory-driven in the normative sense; it invokes secondary critics (Bonells, Romano Hurtado, Lawrence, Freud, Foucault, Bloom) instrumentally to support specific interpretive claims, not to constitute a framework.

## 6. Key concepts

**"Character of phantasm":** Rollason's title phrase, drawn from the passage in "Usher" where the narrator describes Roderick's reading as being "in strict keeping with the character of phantasm." The term denotes the mode in which an alternative, fantastical world is constructed through and mediated by textual/literary objects. Also functions as the linking phrase between the two texts, since Borges echoes it precisely in describing Uqbar's literature.

**Parallel reality / parallel universe:** The shared narrative and conceptual structure — an alternative world (the House of Usher's atmosphere; the planet Tlön) that gradually encroaches on and finally displaces or destroys the familiar world.

**Disintegrative vibration** (from D.H. Lawrence, 1924): Lawrence's characterisation of the destructive, apocalyptic tendency in Poe. Used by Rollason to mark the key divergence from Borges: where Poe's alternative reality is disintegrative (ending in total collapse), Borges's is reintegrative (ending in ordered substitution).

**Intertextuality:** Used in its standard literary-critical sense — the network of textual references, echoes, and borrowings connecting texts. The paper's final claim is that Poe's most lasting legacy may be less his Gothic destructiveness than his "dynamic of intertextuality," which he shares with Borges.

**Encyclopaedism / encyclopaedic erudition:** The shared practice of both writers of assembling vast, eclectic collections of (real or invented) knowledge — Usher's library, the Cyclopaedia, the Encyclopaedia of Tlön. Rollason traces a developmental line from Poe's latent encyclopaedism to Borges's explicit and structurally constitutive version.

**Simulacrum of Borges as narrator** (Romano Hurtado): The narrator of "Tlön" as "a simulacrum of Borges himself, not quite author, yet not quite character either."

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Moderately open.** The paper does not conclude with a definitive thesis statement. It closes with a tentative re-prioritisation: in the contemporary context of the Internet, Wikipedia, and the rapid diffusion of knowledge, the most significant contribution of Poe may prove to be not his Gothic disintegrative power but the "dynamic of intertextuality" he shares with Borges. This is phrased as a suggestion ("may yet prove to be"), deliberately leaving the final evaluation open. The specific empirical claims — that the books in Usher's library are real, that Borges and Bioy actually translated the chapter of Browne containing Poe's epigraph — are argued with high confidence and documented bibliographically, and constitute the paper's most certain results.

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

No formal limitations section, consistent with the genre. Implicit constraints:

- The paper is exclusively focused on two texts and cannot claim to account for the full range of Poe's Gothic tales or Borges's metaphysical fictions.
- The Poe-Quevedo-Borges connection is argued partly on the basis of what "Borges may not have known" — the triangulation through Quevedo may not have been a conscious influence.
- The paper does not situate either text in its full reception history.
- Rollason flags Clive Bloom's error in claiming most of Usher's library is invented, as a cautionary note about relying on older editions.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Complementary to Riberi (2010):** Riberi and Rollason both analyse "Tlön" as a philosophical-literary mechanism. Riberi focuses on epistemology (Vaihingerian 'as if'); Rollason focuses on intertextuality (the Poe genealogy and library archaeology). Together they constitute complementary close readings of the same text.

**The corpus's founding text:** "Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" is the text that names this collection. Rollason's analysis unpacks its literary mechanics and genealogy; O'Sullivan's accelerationism paper and Balić's CCRU paper take up its hyperstitional logic in a theoretical register; Riberi's paper analyses its epistemological structure. The collection is collectively an archaeology of "Tlön" and its afterlives.

**Contemporary resonance (Tlön → cyberspace):** Rollason's closing gesture — connecting Tlön's encyclopaedic fantasy to Wikipedia and the Internet — anticipates the digital-cultural concerns of several other papers in the collection (Kietzman on Neuromancer, Dalton on heterotopia and digital space, Balić on the CCRU and the Internet). The "Tlön" mechanism (a collectively authored alternative world gradually colonising and replacing reality) is recognised by multiple papers in the collection as structurally resonant with digital culture.
