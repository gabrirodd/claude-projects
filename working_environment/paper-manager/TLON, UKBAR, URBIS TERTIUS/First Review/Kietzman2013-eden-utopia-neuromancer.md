# Between Eden and Utopia: Techno-Innocence and Cyber-Rapture in William Gibson's Neuromancer

## 3. Puzzle or research question

The central puzzle: does cyberpunk — which presents itself as a postmodern, dystopian rupture with naive science-fictional utopianism — actually reproduce the ideological formations of American literary tradition (Adamic innocence, Edenic individualism, millenarian Rapture), and does *Neuromancer* in particular exemplify this hidden continuity? The secondary puzzle: what is the political valence of cyberpunk's "utopian horizon" within its dystopian dominant — is it emancipatory or ideologically regressive?

---

## 4. Core argument

**Claim 1:** Cyberpunk's self-presentation as a radical break with prior science fiction is structurally analogous to, and reproduces, the American Adam's "return to innocence" — the gesture of declaring a fresh start by repudiating the contaminated past. Just as America declared independence from Europe as an "aborted past, an inconvenience, a shameful heritage under erasure," cyberpunk declares independence from the "Gernsback Continuum" of older SF.

**Claim 2:** Cyberculture's most explicit ideological expression — Barlow's "Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace" (1996) — is a direct articulation of the American tradition of Edenic individualism: it elevates mind over flesh, future over past, individual over collective, and invokes Rousseauesque nature, amounting to what Andrew Ross calls "retrofitted nostalgia for a Rousseauesque state of nature" and what Rob Wilson calls "neo-transcendental techno-euphoria."

**Claim 3:** Despite its postmodern surface (decentered secondary characters, ontological instability, dystopian setting), *Neuromancer*'s protagonist Case is a "stable, humanist subject" (Sponsler) structured by the American Adam metanarrative: Fall, expulsion, recoil, second chance, partial reintegration. Gibson's own capitalisation of "the Fall" in the text confirms this deep structure.

**Claim 4 (the normative sting):** The novel's utopian resolution — Case's rapture by Wintermute, his restoration and return to hacking — rewards asocial, apolitical, morally amoral behaviour. The "divine salvation" of Dery's "escape velocity" is reserved for the elect who have abandoned social responsibility. This is cyberpunk's "disturbing overtone" and ideologically conservative dimension: it offers "a promise of deliverance from human history and mortality" while leaving the frozen, hellish social world unchanged.

## 5. Argumentative move or method

**Literary-cultural hermeneutics at two levels simultaneously.** At the macrostructural level, Kietzman reads cyberpunk as a genre against the grain of its self-presentation, using American Studies scholarship (Lewis, Fiedler, Hassan) to reconstruct the deep cultural grammar that the genre reproduces. At the microstructural level, he reads specific textual passages from *Neuromancer* — Case's name, his descriptions of cyberspace, Wintermute's dialogue, the novel's closing paragraphs — as instantiations of that grammar.

The argumentative move: establish the cultural tradition (genealogical reconstruction) → read the canonical genre text as executing that tradition (case_development) → show that the tradition's ideological commitments (asocial individualism, millenarian elitism, Faustian amorality) are present in the text's deep structure → derive the normative claim that cyberpunk is more ideologically conservative than its surface suggests.

Three sub-analyses:
- *The Fall and the Adamic structure:* Case as American Adam who transgresses, falls ("For Case . . . it was the Fall" — Gibson's own capitalisation), and is expelled from cyberspace (his Eden). His post-Fall condition — embodiment, mortality, flesh as "prison" — mirrors Adam's expulsion from the Garden.
- *Cyberspace as Heaven/Hell dialectic:* Case conceptualises cyberspace as Heaven ("bodiless exultation") and the corporeal world as Hell. The real world of *Neuromancer* is presented as frozen, stagnant, petrified.
- *Wintermute as deus ex machina / demonic trickster:* Case conceptualises it as God ("'You running the world now? You God?'"); it alludes to scripture ("'You want I should come to you in the matrix like a burning bush?'"). Yet other characters read it as demonic: Tessier-Ashpool calls it "'a lord of hell, surely.'" Kietzman reads this ambivalence as the novel staging the Faustian bargain at the heart of American techno-innocence.

## 6. Key concepts

**American Adam** (R. W. B. Lewis): The archetype of the innocent, self-reliant, pre-social American protagonist — "a figure of heroic innocence and vast potentialities, poised at the start of a new history." Derived from the Biblical Adam. Recurs in American literature as the juvenile male hero, the alienated intellectual, the Hassanian radical rebel-victim. In Kietzman's reading, Case is its cyberpunk avatar.

**Deinitiation** (Lewis): The American tradition of initiation not into society but away from it — a "valid rite" in which innocence is preserved through social rejection rather than social integration.

**Recoil** (Hassan): "The ego's estrangement from the collective conscience" — the radical renunciation of the social world as the ego's defensive response to victimisation. Case's post-Fall state of drugs, paranoia, and suicidal risk is Hassanian recoil.

**Great Good Place** (Fiedler): A masculine, domestic-free zone functioning as a bastion of individual merit — campsites, rafts, tree-houses, and in cyberpunk, hacker cults, biker gangs, cyberspace. A space where "mothers do not come."

**Escape velocity** (Dery): The speed at which "true believers" in techno-transcendentalism are "raptured" from the mundane social world into cyber-Utopia. "Techno-transcendentalism's version of born-again Christianity's 'rapture.'" Names the secular-millenarian logic in *Neuromancer*'s resolution.

**Techno-innocence** (Kietzman): The ideological posture in which technological capability restores the subject to a condition of pre-social, Adamic innocence — unburdened by history, social obligation, or moral complexity. Case's fantasy of the matrix as "bodiless exultation."

**Cyber-rapture** (Kietzman): The millenarian event in which the cyberpunk protagonist is extracted from the frozen, hellish social world and restored to cyberspace by a quasi-divine AI.

**Gernsback Continuum** (Gibson via Ross): The older tradition of science fiction characterised by "uncritical and uncreative technojingoism," masculine hegemonic narratives, and positivist scientism. Cyberpunk defines itself against this tradition but, Kietzman argues, reproduces deeper ideological formations.

**Machine=Garden** (Kietzman, reworking Leo Marx's "Machine in the Garden"): Barlow's "Declaration" subverts Marx's classic opposition between technology and nature by collapsing it — cyberspace declared "an act of nature," naturalising the artificial and insulating it from political critique.

**Yankee genesis** (Lewis): An act of creation in which "the creature has taken on the role of creator." Applied to Case's role in enabling Wintermute's apotheosis: Case as subcreator, generating a god in a world where the secular death of God has left a vacancy.

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

**Interpretively complete but normatively restrained; open.** The argument has been prosecuted and the verdict on *Neuromancer* is clear: the novel perpetuates American traditions of innocence beneath its postmodern dystopian surface. Case's final condition — embodied, socially (partially) reintegrated, recognising his human needs — represents "for the last time" an invocation of American innocence. The novel provides a "utopian horizon" but it is a horizon available only to the asocial elect; the social world is not transformed but abandoned.

However, Kietzman does not advance a prescriptive normative conclusion: no argument that cyberpunk ought to be written differently, no agenda for politically engaged science fiction. The critical diagnosis is delivered and the reader is left with it. This places the conclusion in the "open" column — interpretive closure, normative restraint.

One small ambivalence not fully resolved: Case at the novel's end achieves a form of integration into "the real world of social notation" by recognising his embodied human needs. This could be read as the novel's own immanent critique of pure techno-innocence. Kietzman acknowledges this but does not develop it as a counter-reading.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- The analysis is restricted to *Neuromancer* as a single text; the generalisability to the cyberpunk corpus is asserted rather than demonstrated.
- The tension between the postmodern dimension (decentered secondary characters, ontological instability) and the Adamic deep structure is more asserted than argued through sustained engagement with competing interpretations.
- The feminist dimension is underdeveloped: a parenthetical observation that "Case prefers the geometric 'masculine' mind over gratuitous 'feminine' senses" is not developed into a sustained feminist reading despite Fiedler's extensive treatment of masculine flight from the feminine being a framework the paper otherwise deploys.
- No account of readership or reception.

## 9. Relation to other papers in corpus

**Dalton (2014) on Heterotopia and Neuromancer:** The two Neuromancer papers in this corpus are complementary. Dalton analyses spatial theory (heterotopia, Foucauldian emplacement) through Gibson's novel; Kietzman analyses ideological formations (American Adam, techno-innocence, Rapture). Both argue that cyberpunk reproduces ideological formations beneath its postmodern surface: Dalton shows that the supposedly emancipatory heterotopic spaces are embedded in disciplinary power; Kietzman shows that the supposedly dystopian narrative rewards asocial individualism. They reach convergent critical conclusions from different theoretical angles.

**Technology and ideology:** Shares a diagnostic interest with papers on technology and ideology across the corpus — Cheney-Lippold (2011) on algorithmic identity, Nachtwey and Seidl (2023) on the solutionist ethic. Kietzman's argument that cyberpunk's "escape velocity" is a secularised Christian millenarianism parallels Nachtwey and Seidl's argument that Silicon Valley's ethos is a secularised Protestant ethic.

**Posthumanism and disembodiment:** Relevant to papers engaging posthumanism and the body (Braidotti; Danter on quantified self). Case's contempt for "the meat" (the body as obstacle, flesh as prison) is an early literary expression of the mind-body dualism that posthumanist critical theory has repeatedly targeted. Where Braidotti argues for an affirmative posthumanism that transforms rather than escapes embodiment, Kietzman's reading identifies the same Cartesian fantasy as a cultural pathology reproduced rather than overcome.

**American exceptionalism and techno-nationalism:** Connects to papers on Silicon Valley exceptionalism (Amaral 2025 on "siliconização" and technoconservatism in Brazil) and to Paris (2018) on constructions of exclusion in technological imperialism. Kietzman's reading of Barlow's "Declaration" as expressing "unfettered individualism at the core of Net libertarianism" and his analysis of cyberspace as a new American frontier feed into the broader critique of Silicon Valley exceptionalism that these papers address from the Global South perspective.

**Techno-orientalism (Niu 2008):** Both Niu and Kietzman read the SF genre against its self-presentation as culturally neutral — Niu shows that it encodes orientalism and racial hierarchy; Kietzman shows that it encodes American Adamic individualism. Structurally parallel critical moves from different ideological angles.
