# Whitehead & Hannah (2025) — New Figurations of Freedom: Ricoeurian Perspectives on the Digital Society

**Zotero key:** 9LVII4BH | **Date:** 2025-09-30 | **Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type: Mixed — Type 5b (normative argument with constitutive empirical analysis) with Type 2b kernel

**Move sequence:** `puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping [surveillance capitalism / governmentality / posthumanism] → immanent_critique [each fails the embodied subject] → framework_proposal [Ricoeur] → method_justification [SenseMaker®] → case_development [empirical findings] → normative_derivation → conclusion_open`

---

## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens by diagnosing a gap in existing critical frameworks for digital power: all of them either presuppose an idealised subjective autonomy that digital systems threaten (surveillance capitalism, algorithmic governmentality) or dissolve the subject altogether (anti-subjectivist genealogy, posthumanism). The rival mapping is followed by an immanent critique: each framework is shown to fail the embodied, negotiated character of actual human experience. Ricoeur's phenomenology is then proposed as the corrective. A SenseMaker® empirical study is introduced and justified methodologically — the empirical findings are constitutive, not merely illustrative: the normative claim about "negotiated consent" cannot be made without them. This is Type 5b (constitutive critical analysis) with a strong Type 2b kernel (Ricoeur is genuinely reconstructed for contemporary application, not merely cited).

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

Existing critical frameworks for analysing digital power — surveillance capitalism, algorithmic governmentality, sensory power, post-liberal government, neuroliberalism — either presuppose an idealised subjective autonomy that digital systems threaten, or dissolve the subject through anti-subjectivist genealogy, or frame the posthuman as technological enhancement/subjugation. None adequately accounts for the embodied, negotiated, phenomenologically complex experience of freedom that people actually have when using digital technologies.

Research question: how do people actually experience freedom — its possibilities and constraints — in relation to smart/intelligent technologies, and what conceptual resources can account for this experience without falling back on either idealist autonomy or anti-subjectivist dissolution?

---

## 4. Core argument

Ricoeur's anti-dualist phenomenology of freedom provides resources that existing frameworks lack. His key move: freedom is located in the irreducibly complex relationship between the *voluntary* (what subjects initiate, consent to, decide) and the *involuntary* (what subjects receive — bodily conditions, habits, character, unconscious drives, situational constraints). Freedom is not the absence of constraint but a negotiated practice within and through the involuntary. Applied to digital/smart technology, the "involuntary" now includes both *biological* constraints (embodiment, affect, habit) and *digital* constraints (algorithmic recommendations, interface design, data structures, notification rhythms). Freedom in the digital age is the negotiated practice of the voluntary within this double involuntary.

The SenseMaker® empirical findings confirm and specify this: people do not experience their use of intelligent technology as either digital liberation or digital oppression, but as *complex negotiations between the biological and digital involuntary and voluntary acts of negotiated consent*. "Negotiated consent" is the paper's key proposed concept.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Two-stage: (1) *theoretical* — systematic reconstruction of Ricoeur's phenomenology against the landscape of existing frameworks (Zuboff, Foucauldian governmentality, neuroliberalism, posthumanism), showing what each framework misses; (2) *empirical* — SenseMaker® analysis: a participatory narrative inquiry tool in which participants situate their own narratives within a triangular "signification framework," producing both qualitative narrative data and quantitative pattern visualisations. The SenseMaker® data generate a picture of how people's experiences of digital technology cluster — where they locate themselves between liberation and constraint, between agency and determination. Patterns are interpreted through the Ricoeurian framework. The move from data to claim is careful and qualified: findings are *consistent with* rather than *proving* the framework.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Voluntary / involuntary** (Ricoeur): the foundational distinction — freedom is the capacity to act voluntarily, but the voluntary is always conditioned by the involuntary; the digital adds a new layer of involuntary alongside the biological
- **Negotiated consent** (proposed): the practice of freedom in digital contexts — not a binary yes/no to technology but a continuous, embodied, socially situated negotiation with its affordances and constraints
- **Anti-dualist phenomenology** (Ricoeur): a phenomenology that refuses both mind/body dualism and eliminative materialism; the self is embodied capability — capable of acting, narrating, promising, taking responsibility within conditions not of its choosing
- **Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff): referenced as a framework Ricoeur can supplement — it diagnoses the extraction of behavioural surplus but cannot account for the embodied experience of navigating it
- **Algorithmic governmentality**: Foucauldian extension to algorithmic systems — referenced as a framework that dissolves the subject rather than accounting for its capacity
- **SenseMaker®**: a participatory narrative inquiry tool that captures micro-narratives and uses self-signification frameworks to produce pattern-rich qualitative-quantitative data; the methodological vehicle of the empirical study

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately cautious. The theoretical argument — Ricoeur as more adequate than existing frameworks — is advanced with confidence and the critique of rivals is incisive. The empirical findings are presented as supporting but not definitively establishing the Ricoeurian framework. The conclusion calls for further research on specific configurations of voluntary/involuntary acts across different technologies, populations, and cultural contexts — open and research-agenda in character.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- SenseMaker® data capture perceptions and narratives, not behaviour or decision-making in real time — the method has a reflective, retrospective quality that may not capture the full complexity of in-the-moment digital negotiation
- The sample and site of the SenseMaker® study are not fully described, making external validity difficult to assess
- Ricoeur's framework was developed without reference to digital technologies; the degree of conceptual modification required is not fully specified
- The paper does not engage with the substantial HCI and technology acceptance literature that has generated extensive empirical data on how people negotiate digital affordances

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- The primary phenomenological counterpoint to the dominant Foucauldian frameworks in the corpus. Where Foucauldian approaches emphasise power *over* subjects, Ricoeur emphasises the subject's *capacity* to act within power — complementary rather than contradictory, but the difference in emphasis is significant.
- The SenseMaker® findings directly support and specify **Danter et al. (2016)**'s literary argument: Danter et al. claim fiction foregrounds qualitative experience against database logic; Whitehead & Hannah's participants enact exactly this in practice.
- Connects to **Young et al. (2025)** on consent as a key concept in digital governance — "negotiated consent" gives empirical content to what Young et al.'s "procedural ethics of transparency" calls for in design practice.
- The only paper in the corpus with primary empirical data (SenseMaker®), making it a critical grounding point for the theoretical claims made elsewhere.
