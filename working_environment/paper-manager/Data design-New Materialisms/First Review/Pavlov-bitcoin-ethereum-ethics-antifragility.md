# Pavlov et al. (n.d.) — From Bitcoin to Ethereum: Ethics and Antifragility of Decentralization

**Zotero key:** HJLRA2L5 | **Collection:** First Review

## 1. Paper type: Mixed — Type 2b (canonical thinker reconstruction for normative application) with Type 4 elements

**Move sequence:** `puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → genealogical_reconstruction [Foucault + Taleb] → framework_proposal → case_introduction [×3] → normative_derivation → conclusion_open/research_agenda`

---

## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens by diagnosing a gap: existing frameworks for blockchain governance fail to account for the novel power configurations Ethereum introduces. Two canonical thinkers — Foucault (biopower, panopticism) and Taleb (antifragility, Skin in the Game) — are reconstructed and adapted to the blockchain context. The framework ("adaptive decentralised power") emerges from this reconstruction. Three historical incidents (DAO hack, Parity Wallet, PoS transition) are then introduced as illustrative-constitutive cases demonstrating the framework's explanatory value. This is Type 2b because the canonical thinker reconstruction is the primary argumentative engine, and Type 4 because the cases do real work in demonstrating the framework against contingent events. The conclusion is open and research-agenda.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

How does the Bitcoin-to-Ethereum transition transform concepts of power, resilience, and ethics in decentralised digital systems? Bitcoin established the paradigm of decentralised trustlessness; Ethereum extends it with programmability (smart contracts, DAOs). What new power configurations does this produce, and can Foucault and Taleb — thinkers developed entirely outside blockchain — illuminate them?

---

## 4. Core argument

The Bitcoin-to-Ethereum transition instantiates *adaptive decentralised power*, a new governance paradigm with three interlocking properties:

1. **Codified biopower**: Algorithmic consensus replaces institutional authority as the site of governance. Foucault's biopower — management of populations through normalising knowledge — is enacted not by institutions but by protocol code. The network disciplines participants through transparent, automatic enforcement of smart contract rules.

2. **Economically incentivised antifragility**: Ethereum's Proof of Stake operationalises Taleb's "Skin in the Game" as a protocol-level design principle. Validators stake ETH as collateral; malicious or negligent behaviour triggers "slashing" (loss of stake). This structurally aligns individual incentives with systemic resilience: crises (the DAO hack) are absorbed and the system emerges stronger — the definition of antifragility.

3. **Panoptic consensus**: Blockchain's immutable public ledger functions as distributed Foucauldian surveillance. Unlike the Panopticon, however, surveillance is horizontal (peer-to-peer) rather than vertical (authority/subject), and disciplines through transparency rather than through asymmetric visibility. This constitutes a novel form of surveillance that simultaneously enables and constrains autonomy.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Three-stage conceptual methodology: (1) theoretical synthesis — adapting Foucault and Taleb to the blockchain context; (2) qualitative case selection — three incidents chosen for their significance in demonstrating the framework (DAO hack 2016, Parity Wallet 2017, The Merge 2022); (3) interpretive synthesis across the cases. No original data collection; evidence is secondary (documentation, reports, existing analyses). The move is genuinely reconstructive: neither Foucault nor Taleb were designed for this context, and the paper's intellectual contribution lies in the adaptation itself.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Biopower** (Foucault, adapted): management of populations through normalising knowledge — here recast as algorithmic consensus
- **Panopticism** (Foucault, adapted): discipline through the possibility of being watched — here recast as distributed blockchain transparency
- **Antifragility** (Taleb): systems that gain from disorder and stress, as distinct from merely robust (resists stress) or fragile (damaged by stress)
- **Skin in the Game** (Taleb): the principle that decision-makers must bear the consequences of their choices — operationalised in PoS staking as protocol design
- **Adaptive decentralised power** (proposed): the synthesis concept — the novel governance configuration produced by combining codified biopower, antifragility, and panoptic consensus
- **Lex cryptographia**: the idea that cryptographic code functions as self-executing, borderless law
- **DAOs**: blockchain-native governance structures whose rules are encoded in smart contracts

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately strong as a framework proposal. The synthesis of Foucault and Taleb is novel and the three-part concept of "adaptive decentralised power" is internally coherent. The conclusion is explicitly research-agenda: the framework is proposed as generalisable beyond blockchain (distributed science communities, sharing economy platforms), with calls for future quantitative validation and cross-platform comparison. The paper does not claim Ethereum has solved the problems of power and ethics — it argues it instantiates a new configuration that requires these concepts to understand.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- Exclusive focus on Ethereum limits generalisability; the framework may not apply to Bitcoin, Solana, or other ecosystems
- Qualitative-conceptual approach cannot substitute quantitative validation
- Real-world plutocratic tendencies undercut the "Skin in the Game" ideal: the top 5 ETH staking providers control ~60% of staked ETH
- The 32 ETH staking barrier (significant capital threshold) creates fairness problems the paper flags but does not resolve
- The paper does not engage with the environmental critique of PoW (pre-Merge) or the political economy of Ethereum's developer community

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Shares the Foucauldian power framework with **Young et al. (2025)**, **Hu (n.d.)**, **Jaros (2019)**, and **Amaral et al. (2025)**, but applies it to a technical-protocol context none of the others enter.
- Taleb's antifragility is unique in the corpus — a useful complement to the predominantly Foucauldian toolkit.
- Connects to **Rieder & Simon (2015)** on algorithmic accountability: Ethereum's transparency partially addresses the opacity problem Rieder & Simon diagnose, though plutocratic concentration complicates the parallel.
- The DAO hack case resonates with **Hu (n.d.)**'s analysis of how blockchain crises are absorbed and redeployed within governance regimes.
