# Young, Husca & Pandit (2025) — Are You Being Played? Video Games as a Lens for Artificial Intelligence Ethics and Data Politics

**Zotero key:** ALAPAAPV | **Date:** 2025-12-26 | **Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type: Mixed — Type 3a (cultural object as critical parable) with Type 4 framework-application elements

**Move sequence:** `puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → framework_proposal → case_introduction → case_development [×3] → normative_derivation → conclusion_open/research_agenda`

---

## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens with the puzzle (games incorporate AI-driven surveillance *through their mechanics*, not just their narratives) and maps rival theoretical resources (Foucault, Deleuze, Zuboff, Bogost, Galloway). The positive framework — the "allegorithm" — is assembled before the cases are introduced. Three cases then do *double work*: each instantiates a distinct register of surveillance (atmospheric discipline / everyday normalisation / networked consent) and collectively generate the paper's positive proposal, the *procedural ethics of transparency*. This sits between Type 3a (cases are constitutive, not merely illustrative) and Type 4 (the theoretical framework pre-exists and the cases test/extend it). The conclusion is open and research-agenda in character.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

If games incorporate surveillance not only through narrative and aesthetics but through AI-driven systems that track, model, and adapt to player behaviour *as part of the act of play itself*, what can this dual status (game-as-text + game-as-system) reveal about AI ethics more broadly? The underlying stakes: games are already laboratories where algorithmic governance is rehearsed and players are habituated to surveillance logics — studying them is not peripheral to AI ethics but central to it.

---

## 4. Core argument

Games stage surveillance at three registers: (a) *atmospheric discipline* — Little Nightmares enacts Foucauldian surveillance as environmental texture, normalising bodily constraint through aesthetic ambience rather than explicit rule enforcement; (b) *everyday normalisation* — The Sims embeds surveillance capitalism in domestic life simulation, where the player simultaneously watches and is watched, habituating the logic of monitored selfhood; (c) *networked consent* — Cobra Club stages explicit but parodic data exposure and voluntary sharing in sexual contexts, showing how consent is both procedurally required and structurally undermined.

Across all three, games make ethical arguments through code as well as story — this is the **allegorithm** (Galloway): the rule-based procedural system enacts ideology regardless of narrative framing. A game can *normalise* algorithmic tracking through its mechanics even while its narrative *critiques* it. From this the paper derives a *procedural ethics of transparency*: AI systems, like games, should be evaluated not only by their stated purposes but by what their procedural logics actually enact and normalise.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Theoretical synthesis of four frameworks (Foucault → disciplinary power; Deleuze → control societies; Zuboff → surveillance capitalism; Bogost → procedural rhetoric) extended by Galloway's allegorithm. The key move is combining Bogost and Galloway: Bogost shows games make arguments procedurally; Galloway shows the code layer carries ideology independently of narrative. Applied to surveillance, this means the ethics of a system cannot be read off its stated purposes — it requires reading the procedural layer. Three game analyses are then conducted as constitutive close readings that generate the positive framework. No empirical player research; the method is textual-systemic.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Procedural rhetoric** (Bogost): games make arguments through rule-based systems, not just stories
- **Allegorithm** (Galloway): the code/procedural layer of a game operates ideologically, independent of narrative
- **Procedural ethics of transparency** (proposed): a framework for evaluating how rule systems enact ethical stances regardless of stated intent — the paper's main contribution
- **Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff): behavioural data extraction as capital accumulation
- **Control societies** (Deleuze): governance through continuous modulation rather than disciplinary enclosure
- **Atmospheric discipline** (authors' term): Foucauldian discipline diffused into aesthetic/environmental texture

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately assertive on the framework; explicitly research-agenda on its application. The claim that games function as "laboratories for AI ethics" is well-supported. The "procedural ethics of transparency" is offered as generalisable to AI systems, recommendation algorithms, and data governance more broadly — but this extension is gestured at rather than demonstrated. The paper ends by calling for the framework to be adopted in AI design practice and regulatory discourse.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

No empirical engagement with actual player experience or reception — the analysis remains at the level of designed systems and their theoretical reading. Case selection is illustrative, not systematic. The claim that games "rehearse resistance" to algorithmic governance attributes critical dispositions to players without evidence that players develop them. The paper does not engage with the games industry's own data practices (telemetry, engagement optimisation), which would be the obvious empirical extension.

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- **Rieder & Simon (2015)** — both address algorithmic opacity; Young et al. approach it from within the system (games as enacted AI ethics) rather than from outside (accountability mechanisms).
- **Whitehead & Hannah (2025)** — the Cobra Club analysis stages explicit consent as procedural mechanic, paralleling Whitehead & Hannah's "negotiated consent" concept empirically.
- **Danter et al. (2016)** — shares the Foucauldian "technologies of the self" framework; where Danter et al. use literary fiction as counter-model, Young et al. use game procedurality as diagnostic lens.
- Extends the Foucauldian framework dominant in the corpus into cultural-aesthetic territory the other papers do not enter.
