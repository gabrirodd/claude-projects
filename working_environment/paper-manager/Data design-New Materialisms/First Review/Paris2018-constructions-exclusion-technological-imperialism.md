## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens with a sharp contextualising moment: April 2018 — Zuckerberg's Senate testimony, Cambridge Analytica, Facebook's complicity in violence in Burma, predictive policing in New Orleans. These are framed not as exceptional failures but as symptomatic expressions of a structural pattern. The framework of "technological imperialism" and Star & Ruhleder's infrastructure-as-political-process is established before the book summaries. Paris then develops both books as constitutive cases — Noble addressing the contemporary US platform context, Hicks the historical British computing context — and synthesises them as complementary genealogies of the same underlying logic. The review format allows Paris to develop an independent normative argument through the books rather than simply summarising them. Conclusion is prescriptive: calls for regulatory action, public knowledge infrastructure, and systemic intervention before the tech industry "forecloses every last sector of the social contract."

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

How do technology and computing infrastructure encode and reproduce structural inequalities of race, gender, and class — both historically (British state computing, 1940s–1980s) and in the contemporary networked world (US platform capitalism) — and what forms of accountability and redress remain possible?

---

## 4. Core argument

**Noble's argument** (*Algorithms of Oppression*): Google's search engine is not a neutral retrieval system but a political-economic apparatus that promotes structural racism and sexism through its design, market logic, and the knowledge organisation traditions (library science, citation analysis) on which it draws. Near-decade-long documentation: searches for "black girls" returning pornographic results. Mechanism: Google's relevance algorithm, derived from citation analysis, treats "popularity" and "importance" as proxies — and popularity is a function of economic power (SEO, advertising spend). Consequences range from individual radicalisation (Dylann Roof's search history leading to white supremacist content) to systemic FCC failure to regulate. Neoliberalism has gutted the public institutions that could have held platforms accountable while Google has achieved effective monopoly status.

**Hicks's argument** (*Programmed Inequality*): The exclusion of women from computing in Britain was not a natural drift but a *manufactured outcome* of state policy driven by heteronormative gender assumptions built into job classification systems. Trajectory: (1) interwar — women predominantly operate card-punching machines; (2) post-WWII — computing remains "women's work," categorised as low-skill despite increasing technical complexity; (3) 1950s–60s — labour shortage and "White Heat" technocratic project create pressure to upgrade computing; rather than reclassifying women's existing work upward, the state imports college-educated men at higher grades; (4) women are required to train their male replacements at lower pay; (5) by the 1980s the discourse has naturalised the conclusion that "men are just better at computers." This process directly undermined British computing competitiveness — the exclusion of experienced women was economically self-defeating, demonstrating that structural sexism is not just unjust but functionally irrational within the system's own terms.

**Paris's synthetic claim**: *Technological imperialism* — the use of technocratic rhetoric and infrastructure to extend the power of dominant groups — is a historical pattern, not a contemporary accident. Both books establish this; neither book alone is sufficient. Together they constitute complementary genealogies — one contemporary, one historical — of the same underlying logic: Silicon Valley's ideology of neutrality and meritocracy is precisely the ideology that obscures and enables this pattern.

## 5. Argumentative move

Paris reads both books through a critical-political framework — neoliberalism, technocratic imperialism, structural inequality — and synthesises their accounts to draw broader implications about regulation, civil society, and the public sphere. The review uses Star & Ruhleder's concept of infrastructure as negotiated social process (not a "thing" but an ongoing negotiation between social structures and technical tools) to frame both books. It also draws on the "Californian Ideology" (Barbrook & Cameron 1996) to contextualise Silicon Valley's neutrality claims. The review format enables Paris to develop an independent argument through the books, not merely report them.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Technological imperialism**: the use of technocratic rhetoric, infrastructure design, and platform monopolisation to extend and naturalise the power of dominant social groups — the organising concept linking both books
- **Algorithms of oppression** (Noble): the argument that search algorithms structurally reproduce racism and sexism, not as bugs but as outcomes of design choices embedded in political-economic structures
- **Programmed inequality** (Hicks): gender exclusion in computing as manufactured through deliberate policy choices embedded in job classification, pay structures, and the gendering of skill categories
- **Infrastructure as political process** (Star & Ruhleder): technological infrastructure is not a neutral carrier but a negotiation between social structures that define social institutions and individuals who work with technical tools — what it enables and forecloses is always political
- **Californian Ideology** (Barbrook & Cameron): the ideological formation of Silicon Valley fusing libertarian anti-statism with technocratic market faith, erasing the political-economic conditions that produce technological outcomes
- **Knowledge organisation and bias**: Noble's argument that library science's history of racialised subject headings (Library of Congress Subject Headings) is genealogically connected to Google's search algorithm — both organise knowledge according to dominant norms
- **Search engine optimisation (SEO) as economic power**: the mechanism by which economic power converts into search relevance, making "important" and "wealthy" functionally synonymous in the algorithm's operation

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Assertive in diagnosis; cautiously hopeful in prescription. Paris endorses both authors' calls — Noble's push for new regulatory landscapes, public knowledge projects, civil society advocacy; Hicks's call to expose invisible injustices while redress is still possible. The warning is sharp: without systemic intervention the tech industry will "foreclose every last sector of the social contract." The conclusion is prescriptive and rallying rather than analytically open — Paris is synthesising toward a political call to action, not a research agenda.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

Paris largely endorses both books without significant critique. She notes, following Noble, that diversifying Silicon Valley's leadership is insufficient — the systemic problem requires systemic solutions. She does not engage with critics of Noble or Hicks, or with the technical literature on algorithmic fairness interventions active in 2018. Hicks's British focus limits transatlantic comparison; Paris does not develop this limit.

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Extends and contextualises **Rieder & Simon (2015)**: where Rieder & Simon diagnose structural obstacles to accountability abstractly, Noble and Hicks (as read by Paris) show the concrete consequences for specific communities and in specific historical moments — race, gender, and labour add the dimensions Rieder & Simon's analysis lacks.
- The Californian Ideology framework (Barbrook & Cameron) aligns with **Amaral et al. (2025)**'s genealogy of Silicon Valley discourse and its political-economic consequences globally.
- Hicks's historical dimension — the manufactured exclusion of women from British computing — is unique in the corpus and offers a counterpoint to the presentism of most other papers; it shows that the structural patterns the corpus diagnoses have deep roots that predate digital capitalism.
- The knowledge organisation and library science genealogy (Noble via Paris) connects to **Jaros (2019)**'s concern with how knowledge production is colonised by price-signal logic — both identify an epistemic dimension to algorithmic power beyond "biased data."
