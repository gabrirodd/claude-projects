# Danter, Reichardt & Schober (2016) — Theorising the Quantified Self and Posthumanist Agency: Self-Knowledge and Posthumanist Agency in Contemporary US-American Literature

**Zotero key:** LWNCZAV7  
**Date:** 2016-3-1  
**Collection:** First Review

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (theory-driven interpretive / literary analysis)

The primary contribution is conceptual — mapping the quantified self onto frameworks of posthumanism, economy-driven subjectivity, and information-age knowledge cultures, and arguing for the specific historical-cultural genealogy of QS within American tradition. The literary case (Shteyngart's *Super Sad True Love Story*) is constitutive: it functions as a critical counter-model, not mere illustration. The claim is that fiction constitutes a genuine epistemological alternative to QS logic, not just a commentary on it.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

What are the cultural implications of quantified self technology? How do contemporary US-American novels reflect on, and critically contest, the qualitative changes to the human condition produced by numerical self-description? And is the quantified self a genuinely global phenomenon, or does it emerge from a specifically American model of subjectivity — and if the latter, what are the historical roots of that model?

## 3. Core argument

The paper makes two interlocking arguments:

**The genealogical claim**: The quantified self is not a globally neutral technological phenomenon but emerges from an historically specific US-American model of subjectivity rooted at least since Franklin's *Autobiography* (1791). That model emphasises individualism, economic self-optimisation, and a techno-euphoric belief in self-control and self-possession. The paper traces this through three theoretical discourses: (1) economy-driven subjectivity (neoliberal self as entrepreneur of the self); (2) posthumanism (the QS as a form of Hayles's informational posthuman — pattern over presence, data over embodiment); (3) knowledge cultures of the information age (Manovich's database logic — relational, modular, combinatory — as the dominant episteme).

**The literary counter-model claim**: Fictional texts function as "critical systems of second-order observation" (Luhmann's term adapted here): they observe and comment on first-order social practices like QS. Shteyngart's novel constitutes an epistemological counter-model by foregrounding the *qualitative* dimension of human experience — what is lost, distorted, or rendered invisible by the database logic of self-quantification. In doing so, fiction (re-)inscribes human agency into technologies that would otherwise eliminate it. The "technologies of the self" (Foucault 1984) are here recuperated as sites of resistance rather than merely domination.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Two-stage: (1) theoretical mapping — constructing the conceptual scaffold (neoliberal subjectivity, posthumanism, knowledge cultures) through engagement with Franklin, Hayles, Manovich, Foucault, and others; (2) close reading of *Super Sad True Love Story* as a case that enacts, challenges, and reimagines the QS subject. The argumentative move is to show that the novel's formal choices — its use of both app-like data streams and intimate diary entries — reproduce the tension between database logic and qualitative experience at the level of narrative form, not only content. No empirical data on QS users or practices; the method is entirely interpretive.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Quantified self (QS)**: the practice of tracking bodily and behavioural data (steps, calories, mood, sleep) for purposes of self-knowledge and optimisation
- **Technologies of the self** (Foucault 1984): practices by which subjects act on themselves to transform or understand themselves — here applied to QS as both discipline and potential resistance
- **Posthuman** (Hayles): the figure of the human as informational pattern, in which embodied presence is subordinated to data-structure; QS as one instantiation
- **Database logic** (Manovich): the relational, modular, combinatory organisation of information characteristic of digital culture, privileging paradigmatic over syntagmatic structure (lists over narratives)
- **Second-order observation** (Luhmann): observation of observations — the capacity of literary systems to observe and comment on first-order social practices
- **Economy-driven subjectivity**: the neoliberal construction of the self as human capital, perpetually to be invested in and optimised
- **Distributed agency**: agency understood as spread across human and non-human actors rather than located in individual intention

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately assertive. The genealogical claim — that QS is distinctively American — is argued with historical specificity (Franklin through to Silicon Valley) and is the paper's most original contribution. The claim that literary fiction constitutes a genuine epistemological alternative (not just critique) is advanced with confidence but relies on the strength of the Shteyngart reading, which is not itself contested. The conclusion is open to comparative extension: the paper calls for cross-national comparison of QS literatures and for further examination of how fictional forms negotiate the database/narrative tension in different cultural contexts.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

Primarily implicit rather than explicitly stated. The analysis is confined to one novel and one national tradition — the claim that QS is "distinctively American" is supported by historical argument but not tested against non-American QS movements (e.g., the European Quantified Self community, which has different genealogies). The genealogical line from Franklin to contemporary QS involves significant historical compression. The paper does not engage with empirical QS user research, which would add a layer of evidence for (or against) the cultural specificity claim.

## 8. Relation to corpus

- The "technologies of the self" framework (Foucault) directly connects to **Young et al. (2025)**, which uses the same Foucauldian apparatus to analyse games as sites of self-discipline and normalisation.
- Connects to **Whitehead & Hannah (2025)** on embodied negotiation with digital technologies — Danter et al. argue for qualitative experience as a counter to database logic; Whitehead & Hannah's SenseMaker® findings show that people in fact experience digital freedom as embodied negotiation, supporting the literary argument empirically.
- The posthumanism debate links to **Niu (2008)**, which proposes the "post-posthuman" as a counter to Hayles's disembodied posthuman — a figure that maps onto the literary resistance Danter et al. identify in Shteyngart.
- The American cultural specificity argument implicitly complements **Paris (2018)**'s account of Silicon Valley as the source of technocratic imperialism, and **Amaral et al. (2025)**'s genealogy of how Silicon Valley's discourse travels globally.
