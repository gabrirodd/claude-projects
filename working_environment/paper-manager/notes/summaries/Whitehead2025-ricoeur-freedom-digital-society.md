# Whitehead & Hannah (2025) — New Figurations of Freedom: Ricoeurian Perspectives on the Digital Society

**Zotero key:** 9LVII4BH  
**Date:** 2025-09-30  
**Collection:** First Review

---

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (theory-driven empirical)

Combines substantial conceptual work — developing Ricoeur's phenomenology of freedom as a framework for the digital age — with an empirical investigation using SenseMaker® (a participatory narrative inquiry method). Both layers are load-bearing: the theoretical framework guides what the empirical work looks for, and the empirical findings test and refine the theoretical claims.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

Existing critical frameworks for analysing digital power — surveillance capitalism (Zuboff), algorithmic governmentality (Foucault-influenced), sensory power, post-liberal government, neuroliberalism — have in common that they either presuppose an idealised *subjective autonomy* that digital systems threaten, or they dissolve the subject altogether through anti-subjectivist genealogy, or they frame the posthuman as technological enhancement/subjugation. None of them adequately accounts for the embodied, negotiated, phenomenologically complex experience of freedom that people actually have when using digital technologies.

The research question: how do people actually experience freedom — its possibilities and constraints — in relation to smart/intelligent technologies, and what conceptual resources can account for this experience without falling back on either idealist autonomy or anti-subjectivist dissolution?

## 3. Core argument

Ricoeur's *anti-dualist phenomenology of freedom* — developed primarily in *Freedom and Nature* (1950) and *Oneself as Another* (1990) — provides resources that existing critical frameworks lack. Ricoeur's key move is to locate freedom in the irreducibly complex relationship between the *voluntary* (what subjects initiate, consent to, decide) and the *involuntary* (what subjects receive — bodily conditions, habits, character, unconscious drives, situational constraints). Freedom is not the absence of constraint but a negotiated practice within and through the involuntary. The self is not autonomous (uncaused) or dissolved (wholly determined) but *capable*: capable of acting, narrating, promising, and taking responsibility within conditions not of its choosing.

Applied to digital/smart technology, this means that the "involuntary" now includes both *biological* constraints (embodiment, affect, habit) and *digital* constraints (algorithmic recommendations, interface design, data structures, notification rhythms). Freedom in the digital age is the negotiated practice of the voluntary within this double involuntary. The empirical findings from the SenseMaker® study support this framework: people do not experience their use of intelligent technology as either digital liberation or digital oppression, but as *complex negotiations between the biological and digital involuntary and voluntary acts of negotiated consent*. The paper proposes "negotiated consent" as the key concept for understanding digital freedom.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Two-stage: (1) *theoretical* — systematic reconstruction of Ricoeur's phenomenology against the landscape of existing critical frameworks (Zuboff, Foucauldian governmentality, neuroliberalism, posthumanism), showing what each framework misses and how Ricoeur addresses those gaps; (2) *empirical* — SenseMaker® analysis: a participatory narrative inquiry tool in which participants situate their own narratives within a "signification framework" (a triangular space of interpretive options), producing both qualitative narrative data and quantitative pattern visualisations. The SenseMaker® data generate a picture of how people's experiences of digital technology cluster — where they locate themselves between liberation and constraint, between agency and determination. These patterns are then interpreted through the Ricoeurian framework.

The argumentative move from data to claim is careful and qualified: the empirical findings are described as *consistent with* the Ricoeurian framework rather than as *proving* it.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Voluntary / involuntary** (Ricoeur): the foundational distinction — freedom is the capacity to act voluntarily, but the voluntary is always conditioned by the involuntary (body, habit, situation); the digital adds a new layer of involuntary
- **Negotiated consent** (proposed by authors): the practice of freedom in digital contexts — not a binary yes/no to technology but a continuous, embodied, socially situated negotiation with its affordances and constraints
- **Anti-dualist phenomenology** (Ricoeur): a phenomenology that refuses both the mind/body dualism (Descartes) and the reduction of mind to matter (eliminative materialism); the self is embodied capability
- **Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff): the extraction of behavioural surplus as raw material for prediction products — referenced as a framework Ricoeur can supplement
- **Algorithmic governmentality**: the extension of Foucauldian governmentality to algorithmic systems — referenced as a framework Ricoeur can supplement
- **Sensory power / post-liberal government / neuroliberalism**: three competing frameworks for mapping digital power, each emphasising a different dimension — referenced as the landscape Ricoeur can navigate more adequately
- **SenseMaker®**: a participatory narrative inquiry tool (developed by Cognitive Edge / Cynefin Co.) that captures micro-narratives and uses self-signification frameworks to produce complex, pattern-rich qualitative-quantitative data

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately cautious. The theoretical argument — Ricoeur as a more adequate framework for digital freedom — is advanced with confidence and the critique of existing frameworks is incisive. The empirical findings are presented as supporting but not definitively establishing the Ricoeurian framework: they show that people experience negotiated complexity rather than binary liberation/oppression, which is consistent with the theory. The conclusion calls for further research on specific configurations of voluntary/involuntary acts across different technologies, populations, and cultural contexts — making it openly research-agenda in character.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

- SenseMaker® data capture *perceptions and narratives* of experience, not behaviour or decision-making in real time — the method has a reflective, retrospective quality that may not capture the full complexity of in-the-moment digital negotiation
- The sample and site of the SenseMaker® study are not fully described in the available abstract, making external validity difficult to assess
- Ricoeur's framework was developed without reference to digital technologies; the paper acknowledges it requires careful adaptation, but the degree of conceptual modification needed is not fully specified
- The paper does not engage with the substantial literature on human-computer interaction (HCI) and technology acceptance, which has generated extensive empirical data on how people negotiate digital affordances

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Provides the corpus's primary phenomenological counterpoint to the dominant Foucauldian frameworks (Young, Hu, Pavlov, Amaral, Jaros). Where Foucauldian approaches emphasise power *over* subjects, Ricoeur's approach emphasises the subject's *capacity* to act within power — the two are complementary rather than contradictory, but the difference in emphasis is significant.
- The SenseMaker® empirical findings directly support and specify **Danter et al. (2016)**'s literary argument: Danter et al. claim fiction forecloses qualitative experience against database logic; Whitehead & Hannah's participants enact exactly this qualitative, negotiated experience in practice.
- Connects to **Young et al. (2025)** on consent as a key concept in digital/algorithmic governance — Whitehead & Hannah's "negotiated consent" gives empirical content to what Young et al.'s "procedural ethics of transparency" calls for in design practice.
- The corpus's only paper with primary empirical data (SenseMaker®), making it a critical grounding point for the theoretical claims made elsewhere.
