# Young, Husca & Pandit (2025) — Are You Being Played? Video Games as a Lens for Artificial Intelligence Ethics and Data Politics

**Collection:** First Review

## 1. Paper type detected: Mixed (theoretical framework with constitutive interpretive cases)

The paper's primary contribution is conceptual — a new framework called "procedural ethics of transparency" — but the game analyses are constitutive of the argument rather than illustrative add-ons. The paper sits in science fiction/game studies and AI ethics, and operates by reading games as both cultural texts and technical systems.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

How do video games incorporate surveillance — not only through narrative and aesthetics but through AI-driven systems that actively track, model, and adapt to player behaviour as part of the act of play — and what can this dual status (game-as-text and game-as-system) reveal about AI ethics more broadly?

The underlying stakes: if games are already laboratories where algorithmic governance is rehearsed and players are habituated to surveillance logics, then studying them is not peripheral to AI ethics but central to it.

## 3. Core argument

Games stage surveillance at three registers: (a) *atmospheric discipline* (Little Nightmares — Foucauldian space that imposes bodily constraint and fear as normalised ambience); (b) *everyday normalisation* (The Sims — surveillance capitalism embedded in domestic life simulation, where the player is both the watcher and the watched); (c) *networked consent* (Cobra Club — explicit but parodic staging of data exposure and consensual sharing in sexual contexts). Across all three, games make ethical arguments through code as well as story — this is the "allegorithm" (Galloway): the rule-based procedural system enacts ideology regardless of narrative framing. The paper proposes a *procedural ethics of transparency* as a framework: games, and by extension AI systems, should be evaluated not only by their stated purposes but by what their procedural logics enact and normalise.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

The paper synthesises four theoretical frameworks — Foucault's disciplinary power, Deleuze's control societies, Zuboff's surveillance capitalism, and Bogost's procedural rhetoric — and extends them via Galloway's "allegorithm." The key move is the combination of Bogost and Galloway: Bogost shows games make arguments procedurally; Galloway shows the code itself carries ideology beyond the story. Applied to surveillance, this means a game can *normalise* algorithmic tracking through its mechanics even while its narrative *critiques* it. The three game analyses instantiate this tension in different registers. No player research or empirical data collection; the method is textual-systemic close reading.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Procedural rhetoric** (Bogost): games make arguments through rule-based systems, not just stories
- **Allegorithm** (Galloway): the procedural/code layer of a game operates ideologically, independently of narrative
- **Procedural ethics of transparency** (proposed by authors): a framework for evaluating how rule systems enact ethical stances, regardless of stated intent
- **Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff): the extraction and commodification of behavioural data as capital
- **Control societies** (Deleuze): governance through modulation and continuous monitoring rather than disciplinary enclosure
- **Atmospheric discipline** (authors' term): Foucauldian discipline diffused into environmental/aesthetic texture rather than explicit rule enforcement

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

The conclusion is moderately assertive on the framework and explicitly generative as a research agenda. The claim that games function as "laboratories for AI ethics" is advanced with confidence and is well-supported by the analyses. The "procedural ethics of transparency" is proposed as generalisable beyond games — applicable to AI systems, recommendation algorithms, and data governance more broadly — but this extension is gestured toward rather than demonstrated. The paper ends by calling for this framework to be applied to AI design practice and regulatory discourse.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

The paper does not engage empirically with actual player experience or reception — it remains at the level of the designed system and its theoretical reading. The three cases were selected to illustrate the framework rather than through systematic sampling. The claim that games "rehearse" resistance to algorithmic governance is theoretically interesting but not evidenced: it is unclear whether players actually develop the critical dispositions the framework attributes to them. The paper also does not engage with the game industry's own data practices (telemetry, loot boxes, engagement optimisation), which would be an obvious empirical extension.

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Shares procedural/ethical critique of algorithmic systems with **Rieder & Simon (2015)**, which analyses the structural opacity of corporate algorithms; Young et al. approach the same problem from within the system (games as enacted AI ethics) rather than from outside (accountability mechanisms).
- Connects to **Whitehead & Hannah (2025)** on negotiated consent with digital systems — the Cobra Club analysis in particular stages explicit consent as procedural mechanic.
- The concept of "technologies of the self" (Foucault) links to **Danter et al. (2016)** on the quantified self as a Foucauldian practice.
- Extends the Foucauldian framework shared across the corpus (also Hu, Pavlov, Jaros, Amaral) into cultural-aesthetic territory most others do not enter.
