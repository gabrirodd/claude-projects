## 1. Paper type detected: Theoretical / normative (dual book review developing an independent argument)

This is a survey review of two books — Marie Hicks, *Programmed Inequality: How Britain Discarded Women Technologists and Lost Its Edge in Computing* (MIT Press, 2018) and Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism* (NYU Press, 2018) — but Paris uses the review format to develop a coherent independent argument about technological imperialism, going well beyond description of either book.

## 2. Puzzle or research question

How do technology and computing infrastructure encode and reproduce structural inequalities of race, gender, and class — both historically (British state computing, 1940s–1980s) and in the contemporary networked world (US platform capitalism) — and what forms of accountability and redress remain possible? The immediate provocation is the cultural moment of 2018: Zuckerberg's Senate testimony, the Cambridge Analytica scandal, predictive policing in New Orleans, Facebook's complicity in inciting violence in Burma. These are not random failures but symptomatic of a structural pattern both books illuminate.

## 3. Core argument

**Noble's argument** (*Algorithms of Oppression*): Google's search engine is not a neutral retrieval system but a political-economic apparatus that promotes structural racism and sexism through its design, market logic, and the knowledge organisation traditions (library science, citation analysis) on which it draws. Noble's primary evidence: near-decade-long documentation of Google searches for "black girls" returning pornographic results. The mechanism: Google's relevance algorithm, derived from citation analysis, treats "popularity" and "importance" as proxies for one another, and popularity is itself a function of economic power (search engine optimisation, advertising spend). The result: marginalised communities are systematically misrepresented or erased from search results in ways that have real consequences — from the individual (Dylann Roof's radicalisation via Google-directed white supremacist content) to the systemic (the FCC's pattern of putting the onus on individuals to prove harm rather than regulating at scale). Noble also argues that neoliberalism has gutted the public institutions — the FCC, public libraries, regulatory agencies — that could have held platforms accountable, while Google has achieved effective monopoly status.

**Hicks's argument** (*Programmed Inequality*): Moving historically, Hicks shows that the exclusion of women from computing in Britain was not a natural drift but a *manufactured outcome* of state computing policy driven by heteronormative gender assumptions built into job classification systems. The trajectory: (1) interwar period — women predominantly operate card-punching machines for social services; (2) post-WWII — computing remains "women's work," categorised as low-skill despite increasing technical complexity; (3) 1950s–60s — labour shortage and the "White Heat" technocratic project create pressure to upgrade computing, but rather than reclassifying women's existing work upward, the state imports college-educated men at higher grades; (4) women are required to train their male replacements at lower pay; (5) by the 1980s, the discourse has naturalised the conclusion that "men are just better at computers." This process directly undermined British computing competitiveness — the exclusion of experienced women was economically self-defeating — demonstrating that structural sexism is not just unjust but functionally irrational within the system's own terms.

**Paris's synthetic claim**: Both books establish that *technological imperialism* — the use of technocratic rhetoric and infrastructure to extend the power of dominant groups — is a historical pattern, not a contemporary accident. The Silicon Valley ideology of technological neutrality and meritocracy is precisely the ideology that obscures and enables this pattern. The difference between them: Noble focuses on contemporary US platform politics and its consequences for marginalised communities; Hicks provides a historical case in which the same structural dynamic operated and can be traced in detail. Together, they constitute complementary genealogies — one contemporary, one historical — of the same underlying logic.

## 4. Method or argumentative move

Paris reads both books together through a critical-political framework — neoliberalism, technocratic imperialism, structural inequality — and synthesises their accounts to draw broader implications about regulation, civil society, and the public sphere. The review uses the Star & Ruhleder concept of infrastructure as negotiated social process (not a "thing" but an ongoing negotiation between social structures and technical tools) to frame both books: what they share is the insight that infrastructure is political through and through. Paris also draws on the "Californian Ideology" (Barbrook & Cameron 1996) to contextualise the ideological function of Silicon Valley's neutrality claims.

## 5. Key concepts

- **Technological imperialism**: the use of technocratic rhetoric, infrastructure design, and platform monopolisation to extend and naturalise the power of dominant social groups — here applied as the organising concept linking both books
- **Algorithms of oppression** (Noble): the argument that search algorithms structurally reproduce racism and sexism, not as bugs but as outcomes of design choices embedded in political-economic structures
- **Programmed inequality** (Hicks): the argument that gender exclusion in computing was manufactured through deliberate policy choices embedded in job classification, pay structures, and the gendering of skill categories
- **Infrastructure as political process** (Star & Ruhleder): technological infrastructure is not a neutral carrier but "a negotiation between social structures that define social institutions and individuals who work with technical tools" — what it enables and what it forecloses is always a political question
- **Californian Ideology** (Barbrook & Cameron): the ideological formation of Silicon Valley that fuses libertarian anti-statism with technocratic market faith, erasing the political-economic conditions that produce technological outcomes
- **Knowledge organisation and bias**: Noble's argument that library science's history of racialised subject headings (Library of Congress Subject Headings like "Yellow Peril," "Race Question") is directly genealogically connected to Google's search algorithm — both organise knowledge according to dominant norms that marginalise Othered identities
- **Search engine optimisation (SEO) and economic relevance**: the mechanism by which economic power converts into search relevance, making "important" and "rich" functionally synonymous in the algorithm's operation
- **Obligatory passage point** (implicit, Callon): platforms like Google that have achieved monopoly status become obligatory passage points — all information access must flow through them, giving them structural power over what is visible and what is erased

## 6. Conclusion: strength and openness

Assertive in diagnosis; cautiously hopeful in prescription. Paris endorses both authors' calls for action — Noble's push for new regulatory landscapes (FCC reform, public knowledge projects, civil society advocacy), public information infrastructure, and the development of non-profit alternatives to commercial search; Hicks's call to expose invisible injustices to wider publics and policymakers while redress is still possible. Paris warns that without systemic intervention the tech industry will "foreclose every last sector of the social contract" — a strong claim that frames the stakes as civilisational rather than merely technical. The review closes politically rather than theoretically; it is a rallying synthesis, not a research agenda.

## 7. Limitations acknowledged

Paris largely endorses both books' arguments without significant critique. She notes, following Noble, that diversifying Silicon Valley's leadership ("more women and people of colour in powerful positions") would help but is insufficient — the systemic problem requires systemic solutions. She does not engage with critics of Noble or Hicks, or with the substantial counterargument literature in platform studies and algorithmic fairness. Hicks's chronological British focus limits transatlantic comparison; Paris does not develop this limitation. The review does not engage with the technical literature on algorithmic fairness interventions (debiasing, fairness metrics) that was active in 2018.

## 8. Relation to corpus

- Extends and contextualises **Rieder & Simon (2015)** on algorithmic accountability by adding race, gender, and labour as primary analytical dimensions. Where Rieder & Simon diagnose the structural obstacles to accountability abstractly, Noble and Hicks (as read by Paris) show the concrete consequences of accountability failure for specific communities and in specific historical moments.
- Connects to **Amaral et al. (2025)** on platform-enabled political reactionism — Paris's account of how neoliberalism has gutted regulatory institutions while enabling platform monopoly maps directly onto Amaral et al.'s analysis of how those same platforms amplify far-right content.
- The Californian Ideology framework (Barbrook & Cameron) cited by Paris aligns with **Amaral et al.**'s genealogy of Silicon Valley discourse and its political-economic consequences globally.
- Hicks's historical dimension — the manufactured exclusion of women from British computing — is unique in the corpus and offers a counterpoint to the presentism of most other papers; it shows that the structural patterns the corpus diagnoses have deep roots that predate digital capitalism.
- The knowledge organisation and library science genealogy (Noble via Paris) connects to **Jaros (2019)**'s concern with how knowledge production is colonised by price-signal logic — both identify an epistemic dimension to algorithmic power that goes beyond simply "biased data."
