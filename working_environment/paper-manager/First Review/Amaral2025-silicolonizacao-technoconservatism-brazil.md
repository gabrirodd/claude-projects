## 2. Move sequence unpacked

The paper opens with the puzzle (why did neoliberal rationality, amplified through Silicon Valley, enable the Brazilian far right to return to power?). It establishes Foucauldian genealogy as the method, and Sadin's "silicolonisation" + Fraser's "neoliberal reactionism" as the theoretical anchors. The analysis then unfolds in two stages: (1) genealogy of Silicon Valley discourse — tracing the transformation of 1970s countercultural libertarianism into neoliberal reactionism; (2) genealogy of Brazilian technoconservatism — mapping Olavo de Carvalho, his publishing ecosystem, digital platforms, and connections to the international far right. The case material is constitutive: the specific historical actors, groups, and networks are the argument, not merely its illustration. The conclusion is diagnostic rather than prescriptive. Type 4 because the contemporary case (Brazilian far-right ecosystem) is the diagnostic occasion; Type 2b because the canonical thinker reconstruction (genealogy via Foucault, Sadin, Fraser) is genuinely the argumentative engine.

---

## 3. Puzzle or research question

How has US neoliberal rationality — amplified through Silicon Valley's entrepreneurial discourse — enabled the Brazilian far right to return to power in the 21st century? What is the specific genealogy of "technoconservatism" in Brazil, and how is it structurally articulated with the international radical right and with digital platforms as both infrastructure and ideological actors?

The underlying puzzle: Brazil did not simply receive Silicon Valley's discourse as cultural import — it produced its own technoconservative tradition (centred on Olavo de Carvalho) that was coherently connected to but not reducible to the international far-right network. Why?

---

## 4. Core argument

Two-stage genealogical argument:

**Stage 1 — Genealogy of Silicon Valley discourse**: The countercultural libertarianism of the 1970s (Stewart Brand, Homebrew Computer Club, early hacker culture) was gradually absorbed into what Fraser calls "neoliberal reactionism" — the incorporation of emancipatory cultural movements (anti-institutionalism, freedom from authority, individual sovereignty) into market deregulation and corporate accumulation. By the 2000s–2010s, the same rhetorical toolkit (freedom, disruption, anti-elitism) was available for far-right appropriation: libertarian anti-statism becomes anti-democratic authoritarianism once state institutions are identified with a corrupt "establishment."

**Stage 2 — Brazilian technoconservatism**: Olavo de Carvalho, beginning in the late 1990s, mobilised diffuse segments of the Brazilian far right through a publishing market dedicated to translating and disseminating books used as weapons in the *guerra cultural*. His influence expanded structurally through digital platforms — YouTube, WhatsApp, Telegram — whose algorithmic amplification of outrage and engagement rewarded confrontational, conspiratorial style. The paper maps the specific individuals, groups, and companies constituting this ecosystem and their connections to the international far right (Bannon, Claremont Institute, European identitarian networks).

The genealogical claim: *silicolonisation* — Silicon Valley's colonisation of imagination, subjectivity, and politics through its technological and ideological exports — is the *enabling condition* of Brazilian technoconservatism. The digital platform business model did not merely carry Olavo's content; it structurally shaped which content would flourish.

---

## 5. Argumentative move

Foucauldian genealogy in two stages: (1) excavating the discursive formation of Silicon Valley's political discourse — tracing the forces, actors, and turning points that transformed countercultural libertarianism into neoliberal reactionism; (2) mapping the Brazilian technoconservative field — actors, networks, publishing infrastructure, and platform dynamics. Evidence drawn from political history, media analysis, secondary literature on Silicon Valley ideology, and analysis of Brazilian far-right publications. The genealogy deliberately avoids linear causal narrative, instead tracing "forces at play" at each historical moment.

---

## 6. Key concepts

- **Silicolonização / silicolonisation** (Éric Sadin): the process by which Silicon Valley's technological exports also export epistemological, political, and subjective frameworks — colonising imagination and governance globally
- **Neoliberal reactionism** (Nancy Fraser): the alignment of progressive cultural movements with neoliberal economic structures, simultaneously producing a reactionary backlash that recodes the same anti-establishment rhetoric for the right
- **Technoconservatism**: the hybridisation of technolibertarian Silicon Valley ideology with traditional conservative and far-right politics — anti-statist, anti-establishment, culturally reactive, digitally organised
- **Guerra cultural** (cultural war): the strategic deployment of culture — publishing, media, social platforms — as a site of political struggle
- **Foucauldian genealogy**: not a history of origins but a tracing of "forces at play" — the discontinuous, conflict-laden emergence of discursive formations through historical struggle
- **Digital platforms as governance infrastructure**: platforms not as neutral carriers but as active shapers of political possibility through algorithmic design, content moderation, and business model incentives

---

## 7. Conclusion: strength and openness

Moderately assertive and diagnostically complete. The connection between Silicon Valley discourse and Brazilian technoconservatism is argued with historical specificity. The paper does not overclaim: silicolonisation is the *enabling condition* of Brazilian technoconservatism, not its cause. The conclusion is diagnostic rather than prescriptive — it charts structural conditions for further analytical and political work, but does not specify remedies or counter-strategies.

---

## 8. Limitations acknowledged

- Does not address counter-movements or forms of digital resistance within Brazil — the frame remains structural/genealogical rather than agential
- Genealogical method resists precise causal attribution — the paper cannot specify how much platform amplification explains Bolsonarism vs. other factors (economic crisis, evangelical growth, security discourse)
- Implies broader applicability (to other Latin American far-right movements, to European identitarianism) without fully demonstrating the comparison
- Mapping of the Brazilian technoconservative ecosystem relies on publicly available information — covert funding flows and organisational structures are not fully visible

---

## 9. Relation to corpus

- Methodologically closest to **Hu (n.d.)**: both use Foucauldian genealogy to analyse how digital infrastructure is co-produced with political governance, and both refuse the simple repression/liberation binary. Contrast: Hu examines state-directed technoconservatism in China; Amaral et al. examine market-platform-enabled technoconservatism in Brazil.
- Directly complements **Paris (2018)** and **Rieder & Simon (2015)** on platform power and algorithmic accountability: Amaral et al. show the political consequences when accountability mechanisms fail and platforms amplify far-right content.
- Extends **Danter et al. (2016)**'s argument about American cultural specificity: not just that QS is American, but that Silicon Valley's ideology is an active export reshaping political culture elsewhere.
- Connects to **Jaros (2019)**'s "surveillance commoditism" — both diagnose a hegemonic digital-economic formation that colonises subjectivity, though Jaros focuses on epistemics and Amaral et al. on explicit political mobilisation.
