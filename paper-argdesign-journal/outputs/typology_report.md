# Argument Typology — Theory, Culture & Society / Philosophy and Social Criticism Corpus

## Corpus overview

- **50 papers** analysed
- **Year range**: 2006–2025
- **Journals**: *Theory, Culture & Society* (primary); *Philosophy and Social Criticism* (secondary)
- **Intellectual tradition**: Critical social theory in the broad sense — papers that combine normative ambition (a claim about what is wrong, what is pathological, what ought to be different) with theoretical and sometimes empirical work. The tradition shares a commitment to reflexivity about method and a suspicion of both positivism and pure deconstruction. Authors tend to move between Continental philosophy, political economy, cultural analysis, and social critique without treating these as incompatible domains.

---

## Epistemic landscape

The corpus is overwhelmingly normative. Only 4 of 50 papers operate primarily in an empirical or hermeneutic register without a normative conclusion; the other 46 make explicit claims about what is pathological, what democratic life requires, or what conceptual frameworks the social sciences should adopt. Yet normativity is not monolithic: papers distribute across four distinct epistemic postures depending on *how* they ground their normative claims.

The largest single group — 18 papers — uses a cultural object (film, novel, political movement, artwork, historical process) as an interpretive lens through which to argue a normative point. This is the corpus's signature mode, and it distinguishes the tradition from both sociology journals (which would demand constitutive empirical evidence) and philosophy journals (which would not need cultural cases at all). The second-largest group — 15 papers — operates entirely within conceptual space, making normative arguments by mapping, critiquing, and reconstructing theoretical positions. Ten papers use qualitative empirical evidence (ethnography, discourse analysis, institutional analysis) in service of normative argument, ranging from constitutive fieldwork to illustrative archival traces. Three papers deploy quantitative data. Two papers are purely interpretive (no normative conclusion), and two are primarily empirical (normative element residual).

---------------------------------------------

normative_with_interpretive_case      18    36%
normative_with_qualitative_empirical  10    20%
normative_with_quantitative_empirical  3     6%
purely_interpretive_hermeneutic        2     4%
primarily_empirical_qualitative        2     4%
---------------------------------------------
Total                                 50   100%

## Common epistemological ground

Regardless of typology, all papers in this corpus share a set of background commitments that function as methodological common ground. First, *pathology is the entry point*: papers open by diagnosing something that has gone wrong — a conceptual confusion, a political failure, an ideological mystification, a missed opportunity. The puzzle or crisis is the standard argumentative trigger; purely celebratory or descriptive openings are absent. Second, *theoretical positioning is obligatory*: every paper situates itself relative to existing intellectual positions, even if only to distinguish itself from one rival. No paper presents itself as working in isolation from a tradition. Third, *the normative and the analytical are treated as continuous*: papers move from describing what is the case to claiming what is problematic about it and (usually) what would be better, without registering this transition as a logical problem requiring justification. The is-ought gap is not thematized — it is traversed. Fourth, *reflexive qualification is aspirational but optional*: some papers acknowledge the limits of their own frameworks (typically in the conclusion), but many do not. When it appears, reflexivity is a marker of argumentative sophistication, not a logical requirement. Fifth, *disciplinary boundaries are treated as arbitrary*: authors routinely move between sociology, political philosophy, cultural theory, psychoanalysis, and STS without methodological apology. This is not sloppiness but a constitutive feature of the tradition.

## Type 1 — Special-issue introduction and genre framework

### Core logic (L1)
This type serves a framing and orientating function rather than advancing a thesis. The paper opens a research space, maps its conceptual terrain, and hands the problem to contributors or future researchers.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`genealogical_reconstruction → conceptual_distinction → framework_proposal → (puzzle_diagnosis) → conclusion_open/research_agenda`

The genealogical move establishes the intellectual heritage; conceptual distinction carves out a vocabulary; framework_proposal outlines the diagnostic grid the special issue will apply; a compressed puzzle_diagnosis may follow; the conclusion hands off to the research community rather than closing an argument.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Type 1 papers are defined by their *institutional* rather than purely argumentative function. They do not stake a thesis and defend it against rivals — they perform orientation. The genealogical reconstruction functions as credential-display and horizon-setting: showing which intellectual tradition the special issue inhabits (Habermas, Foucault) and where it departs from it. Conceptual distinctions are programmatic rather than concluded: they define the territory of inquiry, not the answer to a question. The research_agenda closure is diagnostic — the paper has named what must be studied without claiming to have studied it. This is the one paper type where *incompleteness is constitutive*: a Type 1 paper that fully resolved its central question would be anomalous.

Four papers in the corpus fit this type: the two public-sphere introductions (Seeliger-Sevignani 2022; Staab-Thiel's companion piece is Type 2), the pastoral power introduction (Stypinska-Rossi 2025), the neoliberalism review (Davies 2014), and the post-neoliberalism introduction (Davies-Gane 2021). The two interview-commentaries (Featherstone-Turner 2022; Loizidou 2024) are a variant that lacks even the framework_proposal move — they are pure documentary-hermeneutic and are the only corpus papers with `purely_interpretive_hermeneutic` epistemic mode.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `fully_normative` (or `purely_interpretive_hermeneutic` in interview variant)
- Evidence role: `absent`
- Empirical methods: none
- Case type: none (or `philosophical_text` in interview variant)

### Canonical examples
**A New Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere? An Introduction — Seeliger and Sevignani 2022**
Why canonical: Opens with genealogical reconstruction of Habermas, carves key conceptual distinctions (structural/functional transformation), frames the research programme without resolving it, closes with a research agenda addressed to contributors.

**Pastoral Power: Perspectives on the Present — Stypinska and Rossi 2025** (variant)
How it deviates: Includes an immanent_critique move (diagnosing that existing governmentality studies have lost theological specificity) that gives it more argumentative teeth than a pure introduction — it is a Type 1 with a Type 2 kernel.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 1 borders Type 2 (polemical reconstruction) when the introduction includes a substantive immanent_critique rather than merely mapping positions. The diagnostic marker is closure type: Type 1 always ends with `research_agenda` or `conclusion_open`; Type 2 always ends with a `strong_normative_conclusion` or, if open, an `open_ended_with_burden_on_reader` that implies a position has been staked. Stypinska-Rossi 2025 sits precisely on this boundary.

## Type 2 — Polemical reconstruction (fully normative)

### Core logic (L1)
The paper identifies a conceptual error or gap in existing theory, performs a sustained critique, and proposes a reconstructed position — all within purely conceptual space, with no cultural case or empirical evidence constitutively deployed.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → immanent_critique → (counter_objection_response) → framework_proposal → (conceptual_distinction) → (normative_derivation) → conclusion_strong`

The puzzle announces what is at stake; rival mapping identifies the existing positions that fail to resolve it; immanent critique shows *how* they fail on their own terms; a reconstructed framework is proposed; the conclusion often closes strongly with a prescriptive normative claim.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Type 2 is the dominant mode for purely conceptual argumentation in this corpus. The key distinguishing feature is the *immanent critique* as the engine of argument: the paper does not merely disagree with existing positions but shows that they fail by their own lights (internal inconsistency, unacknowledged presuppositions, self-undermining consequences). This is what distinguishes Type 2 from mere review or synthesis. The framework_proposal emerges as the *positive answer* to the immanent critique — it is what survives when the rivals' errors have been removed. The conclusion is typically strong: Type 2 papers commit to a position. When the conclusion is open (Kapoor 2024; Osborne-Rose 2023; Sevignani 2022), it is because the immanent critique is so extensive that the constructive alternative can only be gestured at. Two sub-variants are visible:

- **2a — Dialectical synthesis**: rivals are mapped as generating an antinomy; the framework resolves it. *Atkinson 2023* (field theory vs assemblage theory), *D'Cruz 2025* (biopolitics vs necropolitics), *Hoppe 2025* (vitalism vs extinction discourse).
- **2b — Genealogical deepening**: genealogical reconstruction supplements or replaces rival mapping, providing historical depth for the immanent critique. *Staab-Thiel 2022*, *Gane 2021*, *Lechte 2025*, *Sevignani 2022*.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `fully_normative`
- Evidence role: `absent`
- Empirical methods: none
- Case type: none (or occasional philosophical text as conceptual resource, not interpretive case)

### Canonical examples
**Field Theory and Assemblage Theory: Toward a Constructive Dialogue — Atkinson 2023**
Why canonical: Textbook dialectical synthesis. Puzzle diagnosed as a false antinomy between two sociological frameworks; each subjected to immanent critique for internal inconsistencies; a synthesized reconstruction proposed; counter-objection addressed; strong conclusion.

**Ecodependence: The Intrinsic Connection between Living and Killing — Hoppe 2025** (variant)
How it deviates: The framework_proposal introduces a *new* concept (ecodependence) rather than synthesizing existing rivals, making the constructive move more ambitious and the rival mapping more clearly an adversarial foil.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 2 borders Type 3 (immanent critique via cultural parable) when a conceptual argument deploys a philosophical text or cultural object as a case. The diagnostic question: is the case *constitutive of the framework* (Type 3), or merely *illustrative of a conceptually derived position* (Type 2)? Braidotti 2006 uses a philosophical text (posthumanism) as a lens but her framework is derived conceptually first; the case is subordinate. Ungureanu 2023 builds his counter-reading *from within* the film analysis — Ford's specific choices are what generate the "politics of truth" argument. That is Type 3.

## Type 3 — Immanent critique via cultural parable

### Core logic (L1)
The paper uses close reading of a cultural object (film, literary text, philosophical corpus, body of art) as both the site of immanent critique of rival theoretical positions and the generative source of the paper's own positive argument. The case is constitutive, not illustrative.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → rival_mapping → immanent_critique → case_introduction → case_development → case_interpretation → (counter_objection_response) → (meta_reflexive_qualification) → conclusion_strong/conclusion_open`

The rival mapping and immanent critique often precede the case but are then carried *into* it: the case development simultaneously reads the cultural object and prosecutes the critique of rivals. The conclusion extracts the normative lesson.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Type 3 is the corpus's most distinctive form and the most intellectually demanding. Its defining feature is that the cultural case does *double work*: it is the site where rivals are shown to be inadequate (immanent critique via the case) AND the source from which the positive argument is derived (framework_construction). The case is not an illustration of a conceptually settled position — it is the *ground* on which the position is constructed. This is why `case_function: generative` appears consistently in Type 3 papers. A reader could not reconstruct the paper's argument without engaging with the specific cultural material.

Two sub-variants:
- **3a — Single canonical text as philosophical mirror**: a single film or novel is read as adjudicating between theoretical rivals or as expressing an alternative ontology. *Ungureanu 2023* (Liberty Valance adjudicates Habermas/Derrida), *Ungureanu 2013* (earlier variant), *Yoneyama 2021* (Miyazaki's films generate "critical animism"), *Davison-Vecchione and Seeger 2021* (Le Guin's speculative anthropology as methodological model).
- **3b — Philosophical or historical text as genealogical parable**: a canonical intellectual text or tradition is read to extract an alternative conceptual grammar. *Bhatt 2020* (white supremacist metaphysics read to expose its internal logic), *Mignolo 2010* (decolonial thought read as epistemological counter-programme).

The presence of a `reflexive_move` distinguishes the most complete Type 3 papers. Ungureanu 2023 acknowledges Ford's own blind spots (gendered heroism, mythologized individualism). Yoneyama's absence of reflexive move is the one gap in an otherwise canonical execution.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `normative_with_interpretive_case`
- Evidence role: `absent` (the case is interpretive, not evidentiary)
- Empirical methods: close reading, philosophical hermeneutics
- Case type: `film`, `literary_text`, or `philosophical_text`; function: `generative` or `constitutive_of_framework`

### Canonical examples
**The Mystical Foundation of Democratic Society, Mythmaking and Truth in The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance — Ungureanu 2023**
Why canonical: Single film deployed to adjudicate between Habermas and Derrida (rival mapping), to counter Pippin's existing film reading (immanent critique), and to generate the "politics of truth" argument (framework construction). Counter-objection addressed and reflexive qualification included. The fullest execution of Type 3 logic in the corpus.

**Miyazaki Hayao's Animism and the Anthropocene — Yoneyama 2021** (variant)
How it deviates: Uses a body of films (not a single text), the rival mapping targets Tylor and Bird-David (new animism) rather than political philosophers, and the conclusion is research_agenda rather than strong — making it more programmatic and less conclusive than the canonical form. Reflexive move absent.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 3 borders Type 4 (diagnostic framework via contemporary case) at the question of *what kind of case* and *what the case does*. Type 3 uses canonical cultural texts (films, novels, philosophical corpora) where the interpretive labour is intrinsic to the argument. Type 4 uses contemporary political events, movements, or media objects where the case is primarily an occasion to demonstrate that a pre-existing theoretical framework applies and must be adapted. The boundary test: could the normative argument be reconstructed without close reading of the case? In Type 3, no. In Type 4, usually yes.

## Type 4 — Diagnostic framework via contemporary case

### Core logic (L1)
The paper deploys an existing theoretical framework against a contemporary political, cultural, or institutional phenomenon to diagnose what is wrong with it. The case generates refinements or corrections to the framework, but the core argument is framework-application rather than framework-construction.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → (rival_mapping) → framework_proposal → case_introduction → case_development → case_interpretation → (normative_derivation) → conclusion_open`

The framework is typically imported from a recognized theoretical tradition (Foucault, Laclau, Rancière, psychoanalysis, media theory) and the case tests, extends, or complicates it. The conclusion is almost always open-ended — the case reveals complexity rather than resolving it.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Type 4 is distinguished from Type 3 by the *direction of dependency*: in Type 4, the theoretical framework precedes the case and gives it shape. The case may generate modifications (making it more than pure application), but the reader could recover the theoretical position without the case. This is why Type 4 papers tend toward `conclusion_open` or `open_ended_with_burden_on_reader` — they resist strong conclusions because the case reveals genuine complexity and the framework's explanatory limits.

The case medium in Type 4 is almost always contemporary or recent: political movements (QAnon, the alt-right, Black Lives Matter), digital platforms (social media, algorithmic personalization), institutional processes (drone warfare, climate policy, fossil fuel law), or media objects (zines, campaign art). This distinguishes it from Type 3 (canonical historical or aesthetic texts) and Type 5 (empirical social processes requiring evidence rather than close reading).

Sub-variants by case medium:
- **4a — Political movement or event as diagnostic occasion**: *Baća 2024* (QAnon), *Finlayson 2021* (alt-right), *Baier 2023* (post-truth), *Black and Johanssen 2025* (AI/psychoanalysis), *Cheney-Lippold 2011* (algorithmic identity), *Busbridge 2017* (settler colonialism).
- **4b — Media object or cultural product as diagnostic occasion**: *Haworth 2023* (zines), *Lee 2023* (No DAPL artwork), *Lury and Day 2019* (algorithmic personalization), *MacKenzie and Munster 2019* (platform images).
- **4c — Institutional process or legal regime as diagnostic occasion**: *Folkers 2025* (fossil capitalization law), *MacKenzie and Munster 2019* (dual case).

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `normative_with_interpretive_case`
- Evidence role: `absent` (the case is treated interpretively, not evidentially)
- Empirical methods: close reading of public materials, political discourse analysis (informal)
- Case type: `political_event`, `media_object`, `artwork`, `institutional_process`, `historical_event`; function typically `test_case_for_framework`

### Canonical examples
**Narratives of Post-Truth: Lyotard and the Epistemic Fragmentation of Society — Baier 2023**
Why canonical: Lyotardian framework of metanarrative collapse deployed against contemporary post-truth politics; case generates a key refinement (the collapse is asymmetric, not universal); conclusion remains open and programmatic.

**QAnon and the Epistemic Communities of the Unreal — Baća 2024** (variant)
How it deviates: More toolkit-building in character — the paper builds a conceptual vocabulary for diagnosing QAnon rather than applying a single framework. This gives it more of a Type 2 kernel embedded within Type 4 structure.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 4 borders Type 5 (normative argument with empirical diagnosis) at the question of evidence. Type 4 papers read their contemporary cases interpretively — they cite news reports, policy statements, or platform features as textual materials for close reading, not as data to be coded, counted, or systematically analysed. Type 5 papers conduct original research (interviews, fieldwork, discourse analysis with a method section, archival investigation). The boundary is the presence of a `method_justification` move and the commitment to intersubjective replicability.

## Type 5 — Normative argument with empirical diagnosis

### Core logic (L1)
The paper grounds its normative argument in qualitative empirical research — ethnography, discourse analysis, institutional analysis, genealogical-archival investigation — that does real evidential work even though a normative claim is the argumentative destination.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`puzzle_diagnosis → (rival_mapping) → framework_proposal → (method_justification) → case_introduction → case_development → case_interpretation → normative_derivation → conclusion_open`

The empirical material is central rather than decorative. In fully constitutive cases, the normative claim cannot be made without the evidence (Amin 2014, Serafini 2024, Smith and Burrows 2021). In illustrative cases, the empirical material demonstrates the phenomenon that the theoretical framework already accounts for (Swyngedouw 2010, Geels 2014).

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Type 5 spans a sub-range by the evidence-role dimension:

- **5a — Constitutive ethnographic**: the fieldwork generates the normative argument. Without the Belo Horizonte occupations, Amin's concept of "infra-commoning" cannot be stated; without the Argentine art collectives, Serafini's "prefigurative ontological design" cannot be grounded. Papers in this sub-type typically include a `method_justification` move or at minimum a description of fieldwork conditions (dates, sites, collaborators). They also tend to include `meta_reflexive_qualification` because the field situatedness of the evidence is acknowledged as a limit.

- **5b — Constitutive critical analysis**: the evidential material is not original fieldwork but systematic critical reading of existing texts, platforms, or institutions that makes a claim that could not be made without it. *Smith and Burrows 2021* analyse the actual technical architecture of Urbit (galaxy/star/planet hierarchy, address distribution data) to demonstrate that "redecentralization" rhetoric conceals concentrated property. *Gregory 2012* deploys military doctrine documents, drone operation manuals, and legal precedent to argue that drone warfare inaugurates a new thanatopolitics.

- **5c — Illustrative diagnosis**: the empirical material demonstrates rather than constitutes. *Swyngedouw 2010* analyses media headlines, policy documents, and carbon market data, but the post-political theoretical argument could stand without them — they give it texture. *Geels 2014* and *Shove-Walker 2014* use institutional examples to make practice-theory arguments that are conceptually complete prior to the examples.

The transition from empirical to normative is the most philosophically exposed moment in Type 5. Some papers (Serafini 2024, Amin 2014) manage it explicitly via `normative_derivation` — a section that draws the normative lesson from the evidence. Others (Swyngedouw 2010) embed normative claims throughout and close with an appeal rather than a derivation. This difference in how the is-ought transition is handled is itself a typological marker.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `normative_with_qualitative_empirical`
- Evidence role: `constitutive` (5a, 5b) or `illustrative` (5c)
- Empirical methods: ethnographic fieldwork, discourse analysis, institutional analysis, archival investigation
- Case type: `ethnographic_field`, `historical_event`, `institutional_process`, `digital_platform`, `social_practice`

### Canonical examples
**Lively Infrastructure — Amin 2014**
Why canonical: Ethnographic fieldwork (Belo Horizonte, September 2013) is genuinely constitutive; the concept of "infra-commoning" emerges from the cases; the normative derivation is explicit; the reflexive qualification acknowledges fieldwork limits. Full Type 5 grammar.

**Apocalypse Forever? Post-political Populism and the Spectre of Climate Change — Swyngedouw 2010** (Type 5c variant)
How it deviates: Empirical material (media discourse, carbon market data) is illustrative rather than constitutive — the post-political framework drives the argument, and the discourse analysis demonstrates rather than generates the claim. No method_justification. This is Type 5 at its closest to Type 4.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 5 borders Type 4 (at the upper end — Swyngedouw) and Type 6 (at the lower end). The Type 5/Type 4 boundary is marked by the presence of systematic research: does the paper conduct original analytical work on empirical materials (coding, fieldwork, archival investigation), or does it read publicly available materials interpretively? The Type 5/Type 6 boundary is marked by the quantitative dimension: Type 6 uses statistical data or survey results, Type 5 does not.

## Type 6 — Empirically anchored framework

### Core logic (L1)
The paper combines a major theoretical framework with quantitative empirical evidence. The evidence either grounds, tests, or illustrates the normative theoretical argument; the framework gives the evidence its critical significance.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`(empirical_presentation) → puzzle_diagnosis → framework_proposal → (conceptual_distinction) → (method_justification) → empirical_presentation → normative_derivation → (meta_reflexive_qualification) → conclusion_open`

The sequence varies by whether empirical data opens the paper (as diagnostic trigger) or appears mid-paper (as framework test). The conclusion is consistently open — quantitative evidence generates diagnostic claims, not normative resolutions.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Only three papers occupy this type, making it the thinnest category. They share a critical feature: the quantitative evidence does not resolve the normative question but *sharpens* it. Habermas 2022 uses data on digital platform usage, media ownership concentration, and public sphere fragmentation as a diagnostic trigger for theoretical hypotheses about structural transformation — the data establishes that something has changed, but the normative argument about what it means is fully theoretical. Davies 2025 uses health statistics (ONS psychological distress data, housing asset data) constitutively — the empirical correlation between asset ownership structures and mental health outcomes is the argumentative foundation, and the normative claim about asset economy pathology cannot be made without it. Nachtwey-Seidl 2023 is the most methodologically self-conscious: a Weber-inspired qualitative survey (250+ interviews with digital workers) with an explicit method_justification that distinguishes their approach from standard Weberian sociology.

The pattern reveals a tension: quantitative evidence creates an obligation toward methodological transparency (method_justification, reflexive qualification) that purely normative papers avoid. Type 6 papers bear the weight of both normative commitment and empirical accountability.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `normative_with_quantitative_empirical`
- Evidence role: `illustrative` (Habermas), `constitutive` (Davies 2025), `supportive` (Nachtwey-Seidl)
- Empirical methods: survey, statistical analysis, secondary data analysis
- Case type: none (framework-level argument with data)

### Canonical examples
**The Solutionist Ethic and the Spirit of Digital Capitalism — Nachtwey and Seidl 2023**
Why canonical: Most methodologically complete — Weber analogy explicit, survey method justified, data analysed, reflexive qualification about limits of analogy included. The Weberian "spirit of capitalism" framework gives the quantitative material its critical edge.

**Another Age of Anxiety: Psychological Distress and the Asset Economy — Davies 2025** (variant)
How it deviates: Health statistics are constitutive (the paper's central claim rests on the empirical correlation) rather than supportive, making the empirical work more load-bearing. But the genealogical framing (Keynesian welfare state → asset economy) is so dominant that the quantitative evidence is mediated by a historical narrative rather than presented directly.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 6 borders Type 2 (fully normative) when the quantitative evidence is so marginal as to be effectively decorative. The test: would removing the empirical data change the argument's conclusions, not merely its rhetorical force? If yes, Type 6. If no, Type 2 with quantitative decoration.

## Type 7 — Primarily empirical (normative residual)

### Core logic (L1)
The paper conducts original empirical research as its primary contribution; any normative claim is subordinate to, or emerges residually from, the empirical findings.

### Standard move sequence (L2)
`(empirical_presentation) → puzzle_diagnosis → (rival_mapping) → framework_proposal → case_introduction → case_development → case_interpretation → (meta_reflexive_qualification) → conclusion_open`

The theoretical framework is used to structure the empirical analysis rather than to advance a normative thesis; the conclusion opens onto further empirical questions rather than closing a normative argument.

### Detailed grammar (L3)
Only two papers in the corpus occupy this type — Kinsella 2025 (ethnographic study of male subcultures in Japan) and Mackay 2015 (interview-based study of radical feminist organizing). Both involve sustained original fieldwork; both have theoretical frameworks (sociological, feminist political theory) that inform rather than determine the analysis; both conclude by describing what they found rather than by prescribing what should be done. The normative element is present but residual: Mackay's study implicitly endorses radical feminist organizing; Kinsella's study implicitly diagnoses patriarchal anxiety. But neither paper is structured to deliver a normative argument — the finding is the point. These papers sit at the edge of the corpus's intellectual tradition: they could appear in sociology or gender studies journals without modification, while the other 48 papers are recognizably products of critical social theory as a normative enterprise.

### Epistemic profile
- Primary mode: `primarily_empirical_qualitative`
- Evidence role: `constitutive` (empirical material is the paper's primary contribution)
- Empirical methods: ethnographic fieldwork, semi-structured interviews
- Case type: `social_practice`

### Canonical examples
**Male Subcultures in Japan — Kinsella 2025**
Why canonical: Ethnographic evidence is the primary contribution; theoretical framework (sexual capital, gender mobility) is analytic scaffolding rather than normative thesis; conclusion describes findings with interpretive gloss rather than prescribing alternatives.

### Boundary with adjacent types
Type 7 borders Type 5 at the question of normative intention. Type 5 papers conduct empirical research *in order to* make a normative argument — the evidence grounds a claim about pathology, injustice, or what ought to be different. Type 7 papers conduct empirical research *in order to* describe and explain a social phenomenon — the normative implications are downstream and unforced. The boundary test: is the normative conclusion the argumentative destination, or is it a by-product of the empirical finding?

## Deviation map

### Papers that partially fit multiple types

**Braidotti 2006 (Posthuman, All Too Human)**
Bridges Type 2 and Type 3. It uses a philosophical text (posthumanism, Haraway) as a conceptual lens in a way that approaches Type 3's constitutive case logic, but the framework is clearly derived from Spinoza-Deleuze conceptual resources prior to the case engagement. Classified Type 2 (variant) because the philosophical text is used as illustrative resource rather than as the generative ground of the argument.

**Swyngedouw 2010 (Apocalypse Forever)**
Bridges Type 4 and Type 5. The climate change discourse analysis (Type 5 — qualitative empirical) is conducted but at such a level of interpretive distance from the data that it reads as Type 4 (close reading of public texts). The paper includes real empirical enumeration (carbon derivatives market data: zero to 463 million tons, 2005–2009) that no pure Type 4 paper would offer, but this data is illustrative, not constitutive. Classified Type 5c (illustrative) because it conducts systematic (if brief) empirical discourse analysis and deploys market data.

**Gill and Pratt 2008 (In the Social Factory)**
Bridges Type 4 and Type 5. The "social factory" phenomenon is documented via qualitative analysis of working conditions (interviews, secondary statistics on precarity), but the Negri-Hardt-Federici theoretical framework so dominates the paper that the empirical material sometimes feels like Type 4 (illustrative contemporary case). Classified Type 5 because the fieldwork is original and the evidence is specifically about the labour conditions described (not borrowed from public discourse).

**Busbridge 2017 (Israel-Palestine and the Settler Colonial 'Turn')**
Bridges Type 3 and Type 4. The settler colonial framework is applied to Israel-Palestine (Type 4 diagnostic), but the paper performs a sustained immanent critique of settler colonial theory from within the case analysis (Type 3 logic). Classified Type 4 because the theoretical framework precedes the case and the case does not generate a new framework — it generates a critique of the framework's application.

**Moran 2025 (Love Makes Robots Human)**
Bridges Type 3 and Type 4. Uses a series of historical events (robot loveability instances) as cases — more like Type 4's contemporary/recent cases — but subjects each to close interpretive reading that is generative of the normative argument rather than merely illustrative. The multi-case structure (eleven case sequences) is unusual for Type 3 and unusual for Type 4. Classified Type 3 because the cases are treated as philosophical material that generates the "loveability as ideology" argument.

### Papers that resist typology

**Staab and Thiel 2022 (Social Media and the Digital Structural Transformation)**
This paper is classified Type 2 (fully normative, polemical reconstruction) but has a genealogical opening that could qualify as Type 1 (introduction/framing). It is not a special-issue introduction — it advances a thesis (fourth stage of public sphere development via privatisation-without-privatism) — but the genealogical reconstruction of Habermas's three historical moments functions similarly to the introductory genealogy of Seeliger-Sevignani. The paper resists clean classification because it performs both orientating and arguing functions simultaneously.

**Finlayson 2021 (Neoliberalism, the Alt-right and the Intellectual Dark Web)**
Blends genealogical reconstruction (tracing neoliberal intellectual lineages) with Type 4 diagnostic application (contemporary alt-right) in ways that make the genealogical move load-bearing — it is not mere background. The paper is a case analysis (Type 4), but the genealogical moves are so central that a reader primarily trained in intellectual history would read it as intellectual genealogy (closer to Type 2 or Type 3). Classified Type 4 because the alt-right movement is the case that matters, but the genealogical reconstruction moves deserve acknowledgment as a distinctive feature.

**Braidotti 2006 (Posthuman, All Too Human)**
In addition to the Type 2/3 ambiguity noted above: this paper was published in 2006, before the posthumanism programme was fully articulated. It is a *prospective* framework paper — it builds the programme — rather than the *consolidating* framework paper that Braidotti 2018 becomes. Its openness of conclusion (unlike 2018's strong conclusion) reflects its early-stage status. These two papers together illustrate how a single theoretical programme can shift from Type 3-adjacent (2006: case-based, open) to Type 2 canonical (2018: fully polemical-reconstructive).

### What the deviations reveal

The deviations cluster around three live tensions in the corpus's intellectual tradition.

**The genealogy question**: Several papers use genealogical reconstruction in a way that sits uncomfortably between background framing and argumentative move. The tradition is deeply historicist — it treats genealogy as a form of critique (showing that what appears natural has a contingent origin). But this means genealogical moves carry different weight in different papers: sometimes they establish intellectual heritage (Type 1), sometimes they constitute the immanent critique (Type 2), sometimes they provide historical depth for a case analysis (Type 4 or 5). The ambiguity is not a classification failure but a reflection of the tradition's genuine uncertainty about what genealogy *does* argumentatively.

**The theory-evidence transition**: Papers at the Type 4/5 border (Swyngedouw, Gill-Pratt, Gregory) reveal a field-level tension about when qualitative material counts as evidence versus when it counts as interpretive case. The tradition resists strong empiricist norms but cannot fully escape the claim that some arguments need empirical grounding. The papers that sit at this boundary are often among the most cited and influential — they satisfy both interpretive and empirical audiences simultaneously by holding the distinction unstable.

**The normative residue problem**: Type 7 papers (Kinsella, Mackay) sit at the corpus's edge. They appear in TCS or PSC journals that are normatively oriented, but they do not perform the normative closure the genre expects. Whether this represents an emerging methodological pluralism in the field or simple disciplinary boundary-crossing is unclear. If the corpus were expanded, the proportion of Type 7 papers might determine whether the tradition is genuinely pluralist or whether normative closure is a tacit requirement for publication.

## Methodological notes

**Confidence by type**: Type 2 and Type 3 assignments carry the highest confidence — the distinction between fully_normative and normative_with_interpretive_case is usually unambiguous, and the case_function field (generative vs. illustrative) is the reliable boundary marker. Type 4 and Type 5 assignments carry moderate confidence — the theory-evidence distinction is genuine but papers at the boundary require judgment calls. Type 1 assignments carry high confidence when closure_type is `research_agenda`; the two interview-commentaries required special classification logic (purely_interpretive_hermeneutic rather than fully_normative) because they lack any normative claim.

**Most uncertain profiles**: 
- *Braidotti 2006*: case of a philosophy text used as resource vs. lens — classified Type 2 variant but has Type 3 characteristics.
- *Swyngedouw 2010*: the strongest Type 4/5 boundary case — discourse analysis is real but thin.
- *Moran 2025*: multi-case structure unusual for Type 3; the "generative" function judgment is the pivot.
- *Gill and Pratt 2008*: the precarity fieldwork is present but the Negri-Hardt framework is so dominant that the evidential weight of the fieldwork is genuinely unclear.

**What would strengthen or challenge this typology**:
- Adding papers from journals with stronger empirical norms (British Journal of Sociology, Sociological Theory) would test whether Type 5 papers are distributed differently or whether the normative-with-empirical form is specific to TCS/PSC.
- Adding non-Anglophone papers would test whether the cultural-parable mode (Types 3–4) is an Anglophone rhetorical preference or generic to critical theory.
- The typology is currently synchronic — it treats 2006 papers and 2025 papers equivalently. Diachronic analysis might reveal a temporal shift toward Types 4 and 5 (more contemporary cases, more empirical engagement) and away from Type 2 (pure conceptual polemics), which would be consistent with the field's increasing engagement with digital culture, ecology, and postcolonial challenges.
