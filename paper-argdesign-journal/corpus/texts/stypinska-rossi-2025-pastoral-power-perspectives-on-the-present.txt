       Abstract                                              
       This introduction to this special section of Theory, Culture & Society focuses on the
       formation of power that Michel Foucault – in a number of texts and lectures from
       the late 1970s and early 1980s – analyzed under the label of ‘the pastorate’. Stressing
       the ongoing socio-political relevance of pastoral power, the article outlines some of
       the ways in which it continues to directly influence and animate an array of secular
       governmental techniques in manners that other critical paradigms can only partially
       account for. In this, it takes as its focal point two interrelated problematiques: (1) the
       relation between truth and subjectivity as a constitutive element of modern power
       relations and (2) the theological subtexts of modern governmentality – demonstrating
       how they are elaborated upon by the articles comprising the special section.
                                                             
       Keywords                                              
       governmentality, Michel Foucault, pastoral power, subjectivity, truth,
                                                             
                                                             
       Introduction                                          
       The central focus of this article, and the special section that it is introducing, is the con-
       tinuing theoretical, political and social relevance of the formation of power that Michel
       Foucault – in a number of texts and lectures from the late 1970s and early 1980s (see
       Foucault, 2005, 2009, 2011a, 2011b, 2014a, 2014b) – analyzed under the label of ‘the
       pastorate’. More specifically, our intention here is to evaluate afresh Foucault’s claim

      that the political rationality first inaugurated by the Christian pastorate continues to
      inform Western governance, ethics and subjectivity (see, for instance, Foucault, 2009:
      185) – as well as, as we shall argue, their present dissemination on a global scale.
        These questions, we contend, are particularly relevant to studying the current socio-
      political and economic phase, insofar as – both in its neo-liberal and neo-authoritarian
      expressions – it remains reliant on the ethical production of subjectivity and the analyti-
      cal management of pleasures, desires, cares and interests. Whilst the persistence of the
      former is most notably evinced by the panoply of economic, managerial and psychologi-
      cal techniques dedicated to the cultivation of flexibility, adaptiveness, resilience and
      inventiveness of individuals (see, for example, the bestsellers: John and Paisner, 2018;
      Reivich and Shatte, 2003), the latter is especially manifest in the proliferation of algo-
      rithms and new technologies of self-enhancement across virtually all domains of new
      media (for critical analyses see, for instance, Cheney-Lippold, 2011, 2017; Cooper,
      2020; Rouvroy and Berns, 2013; Stiegler, 2016; Striphas, 2015). Thus, although the the-
      ological and moral underpinnings of their original formulation have largely faded away,
      pastoral rationalities incontrovertibly continue to directly influence and animate an array
      of governmental techniques in ways that other critical paradigms can only partially
      account for.                                           
        One does not need to look far to find overwhelming evidence of this, with Foucault’s
      work, in particular, continuing to inspire and inform the ongoing academic research on
      the legacies of the Christian pastorate. To date, his conception of ‘pastoral power’ has
      been employed in a variety of disciplinary contexts, comprising, amongst others,
      Anthropology (Pandian, 2008; Rice et al., 2021), Business and Management (Downes
      et al., 2024; Ferry and Guthey, 2021; Leclercq-Vandelannoitte, 2021; Nikidehaghani
      et al., 2021), Education (Harjula and Kalalahti, 2024; Howley and Hartnett, 1992;
      McCuaig, 2012; McCuaig et al., 2020; Pelayo, 2014), Geography (Blake, 1999; Savić,
      2023), International Law (Quiroga-Villamarín, 2021), Moral and Political Economy
      (Curtis, 2017), Planning (Kamete, 2011), Psychology (Shin, 2021), Sociology of Health
      and Illness (Cook and Brunton, 2015; Jones, 2018; Martin and Waring, 2018; Rust, 2021;
      Tierney, 2004; Waring and Latif, 2017; Waring et al., 2016) and Work and Employment
      (Boland, 2021; Boland and Griffin, 2021; Richard, 2024). Its studies cover a broad range
      of topics – from analyses of confessional culture (Boland, 2021; Boland and Moore-
      Ponce, 2023; Ferry and Guthey, 2021; Mayes, 2009) all the way to the examinations of
      colonial power and violence (Kolia, 2022; Mayes, 2020; Petterson, 2012; Zavala-Pelayo,
      2016, 2018) – extending beyond Foucault’s original analytical setting and spanning a
      vast array of geographical regions, including Central America (Pelayo, 2014; Zavala-
      Pelayo, 2016, 2018), Southern Africa (Kamete, 2011; Katsaura, 2017; McGivern et al.,
      2020; Nauta and Lee, 2017), the Middle East (Johnson, 2015; Karataş, 2024), South Asia
      (Chatterjee, 2018; Pandian, 2008), East Asia (Shin, 2021; Zhang et al., 2018) and Oceania
      (Mayes, 2020; McCuaig, 2012; Schäfer, 2007; Shih et al 2017).
        Whilst, however, the questions surrounding the pastoral co-articulation of the pro-
      cesses of subjection and subjectification (see Foucault, 2005, 2009, 2011b) continue to
      be addressed by a very significant and wide-ranging Foucault-inspired literature, works
      taking the specificity of pastoral power into consideration are still few and far between.
      With a few notable exceptions (see Agamben, 2011; Leshem, 2017; Stimilli, 2018),

       broadly speaking, the literature on the pastorate has so far either focused on an exegesis
       of Foucault’s work on the subject (see, for instance, Chevallier, 2011; Lorenzini, 2012,
       2018; Senellart, 2014) or has tended to subsume pastoral power under the study of gov-
       ernmentality (see, for instance, Burchell, 1991; Dean, 2009; Martin and Waring, 2018;
       Petterson, 2012). As a result, it has either disregarded the uniqueness of its contemporary
       manifestations or somewhat diluted its specificity. In particular, the way that governmen-
       tality studies have discussed the pastorate seems to us to rely excessively on a paradigm
       of secular administration and rule, which tends to conceal some of the most vital insights
       brought about by Foucault’s work. As Confessions of the Flesh (Foucault, 2021) has
       further confirmed, the pastorate is a regime that is not only political-administrative but
       also, at the same time, ethical, ascetic, theological and veridictional (see also Lorenzini,
       2012; Senellart, 2014). Taking all these aspects into account, we contend, opens up a
       series of insights into the contemporary politics of truth and subjectivity that might oth-
       erwise be overlooked. As we will argue, these issues – and, in particular, the question of
       how the ‘manifestation of truth in the form of subjectivity’ (Foucault, 2014a, 2014b: 80)
       is still an object of multiple political investments – seem ever more pertinent and press-
       ing, especially as we come to inhabit societies where the notion of Truth not only appears
       more and more opaque and fluid, but is also increasingly dismissed amidst the ‘post-
       truth’ discourse (cf: Stypinska, 2022).               
        To demonstrate the relevance of pastoral power today, this special section elaborates
       upon two interrelated problematiques, which, we believe, have so far been either side-
       lined or only partially developed by Foucauldian studies: the relation between truth and
       subjectivity as a constitutive element of modern power relations and the theological sub-
       texts of modern governmentality.                      
       Truth, Subjectivity and ‘Truth’                       
       According to Foucault, the birth of the pastorate marked a turning point within the his-
       tory of Western politics, as it complicated the classical relation between power and Truth,
       metaphysically understood as representation of being qua universal logos or divine law
       (see, for instance, Foucault, 2009, 2014a). Pastoral direction requires permanent control
       not only of Truth, but also of the finite and contingent truth of the subjects of power, as
       ‘freely’ recognized by themselves. This observation, we contend, points to a theoretical
       question irreducible to, what Foucauldian literature often refers to as, ‘the production of
       subjectivity’ or ‘effects of subjectivation’. Whereas these and similar expressions are
       generally meant to indicate the way in which power constitutes the subject (see, for
       instance, Butler, 1990), our concern is with the way power relations form in the triangu-
       lation between Truth, subjectivity and truth. The difference, as we see it, is between a
       type of analysis that tends to reify power as the cause or ‘prime mover’ of subjectivity
       and one that concentrates instead on the active role that the ‘labour of the self on itself’
       plays in the transmission and circulation of power (cf: Lorenzini, 2016; Rossi, 2015).
        In line with this, this special section aims to lay bare a set of problems crucial to the
       relationship between politics and (post-)truth in the present. In addition to the classical
       means of manipulation of factual truths – as already analyzed, most notably, by Arendt
       (2006) – contemporary politics of truth operate through an array of technologies (digital

      and algorithmic ones especially) that mediate, extract and model themselves upon the
      truths autonomously produced by their subjects. Today’s government of behaviours,
      opinions, cares and interests largely rests on the individuals’ will to know, reveal and
      redefine the truth of their desires and, more generally, themselves (cf: Stypinska, 2022).
      Economically, this is the correlative of what has long been known as ‘investments in the
      self’, ‘human capital’, ‘self-entrepreneurship’, ‘life-long learning’, ‘play@work’, etc.
      Pastoral power, we will argue, resurfaces in every instance in which individuals are
      expected to problematize their truth in order to refashion themselves as subjects of
      power. When seen from this perspective, the instability of Truth in contemporary socie-
      ties would appear, first and foremost, as the correlative of power games that tend to
      analyze, dissect and multiply the truth of the ‘I’ in a seemingly endless manner.
        The articles included in this special section all seek, in different ways, to unravel the
      philosophical presuppositions and the empirical implications of this set of questions con-
      cerning today’s politics of truth. Arthur Bradley, Elettra Stimilli and Andrea Rossi ana-
      lyze the pastorate as a political technology geared to mediating the subject’s relation to,
      respectively, ‘interests’, the ‘end’, and the ‘world’. Bradley illustrates how, since its
      inception, pastoral direction has produced the ‘ontological usury’ of its subjects, and how
      this, in turn, has set the stage for the modern constitution of the self as interested, self-
      interested and interested in the self as a form of interest. Outlining the parameters of this
      subjectivation of human beings as ‘human interest’, he points to its coercively normative
      character, demonstrating how the pastoral imperative to borrow or lend money at interest
      is consistently presented ‘as the natural and rational expression of human “freedom”’,
      rather than a mechanism of subjection. In this, Bradley stresses the ultimate, and often
      overlooked, paradox of this set-up: as the demands of self-interest and financial interest
      do not always align, when faced with a contradiction between them, the subjects, he
      argues, are normatively obliged to indebt themselves, thus engaging in practices that
      pose nothing short of an ‘existential risk to their interests’.
        Bringing our attention to the temporal dimension, Stimilli demonstrates how the gov-
      ernment of the Christian subject substantially hinged on the subjects’ problematization of
      the ‘end of times’, in a way that still affects different forms of economic and ecological
      apocalyptics today. Expounding Foucault’s unique eschatological-apocalyptic perspec-
      tive, she focuses, in particular, on his understanding of ‘eschatological faith’ (see
      Foucault, 2009), demonstrating how, by addressing its question exclusively within the
      framework of anti-pastoral struggles, he effectively breaks away from the outlook stipu-
      lated by the history of secularized salvation. This, Stimilli contends, allows Foucault to
      conceive the revolutionary element of eschatology as ‘the potential implicit in any rela-
      tion of power’ and thus, simultaneously, as ultimately coinciding with the ontology of the
      subject. Such an approach, in turn, opens up a space for a completely different under-
      standing of one’s relation to both time and history – one that, she argues, entails autono-
      mous self-conduct by means of a radical intervention into the present, here understood
      ‘as an essential difference within history’.           
        Touching on a related theme, Rossi traces how, by erasing the question of the subject’s
      ekstatic relation to the world, coenobitic confession gave rise to an ethical model that, in
      spite of its manifold transformations, still appears hegemonic in advanced technological
      societies. Demonstrating the ways in which the Christian pastorate appropriated and

       refashioned ancient ‘techniques of finitude’, he elaborates on the consequences of the
       monastic submission of one’s ‘relation to the limits’ to permanent institutional media-
       tion. Contemporary ‘in-finite’ dependence on, and subjection to, governmental powers,
       Rossi posits, ought to be read as one of the offshoots of the pastoral model of conduct,
       which, he argues, furnished the basis for a wide range of practices of self-optimization
       and self-enhancement at once overdetermining one’s relation to the limits and overpow-
       ering the self.                                       
        Examining the digitalization of the secularized religious techniques of individualiza-
       tion, Stypinska, on the other hand, focuses on the ways in which the intensification of the
       pastoral directive results in the subjects themselves becoming the mediators of the domi-
       nant power. Taking Foucault’s claim that ‘the pastorate is a nexus for the formation and
       transmission of truth’ (2021: 314) as her point of departure, she analyzes social media
       practices through the lens of the sacramental paradigm of confession, reading digital
       performances – such as ‘influencing’ – as technologies of the subject. In this, Stypinska
       considers social media as a specific dispositif, arguing that it facilitates the amalgamation
       of the pastoral complex of activities of self-conduction, being conducted, and conducting
       others, thus engendering the constitution of subjects who simultaneously self-conduct,
       are conducted, and, as the end result, become conduits of power. Situating digital effi-
       cacy as a condition of possibility of relating to oneself, others and the world, she con-
       tends that social media, in effect, induces de-subjectification, instigating the production
       of ‘spectral subjects’ characterized by ethical indeterminacy.
       The Theological Subtexts of Modern Governmentality    
       Even though a number of recent works have examined the relation between Foucault and
       religion (see, for instance, Carrette, 2000; Jordan, 2014) and Foucault and theology (see,
       for instance, Bernauer, 2017; Heron, 2018; Tran, 2011) – thus also often touching on
       pastoral power – they have rarely, if ever, utilized their analyses to investigate contem-
       porary power formations. This is rather surprising given the growing interest – spurred
       by the work of prominent authors such as Agamben (2011, 2013), Badiou (2003),
       Cacciari (2018, 2021), Derrida (1994, 2002), Esposito (2011, 2015) and Žižek (2000,
       2003) – in the ways in which theology continues to inform the domains of law, finance,
       technology, art, etc. As already hinted at, governmentality studies, on the other hand,
       seem to interpret modern political rationalities predominantly in terms of secular rule
       and immanent management of men and things.            
        Starting from the assumption that secularization is not a process that breaks with reli-
       gious structures but, rather, a means of transposing theological constructs into the con-
       ceptual system of modernity (cf: Agamben, 2011), this special section examines how
       different dimensions of the Christian government of souls continue to shape the socio-
       political realm to the present day. Our aim, therefore, is to bring Foucauldian studies into
       a productive dialogue with political and economic theology, and, in particular, to supple-
       ment the latter’s focus on the persisting influences of Christian metaphysics (understood
       as universal discourse on God and Truth) with an approach that emphasizes instead the
       ethical and epistemic techniques that political modernity has inherited from the Christian
       conduct of souls. Among other things, moving beyond metaphysics will allow searching

      for the traces of Christianity outside of the domain of sovereign and juridical power and
      their ontological foundations (cf: Schmitt, 1985). As several studies have shown, ele-
      ments of Christian ethics and politics continue to shape spheres – such as the economy
      and the digital – whose functioning presupposes no dogma, theology, metaphysics,
      Theory or Truth (see Anderson, 2008; Benjamin, 1996; Stiegler, 2019; Stimilli, 2018).
      Concentrating on the pastorate’s techniques of veridiction will thus allow the retrieval of
      the lines of continuity between political Christianity and modernity, while at the same
      time acknowledging their metaphysical incompatibilities.
        The contribution of Foucault’s exposition of pastoral power vis-à-vis political theol-
      ogy is the focus of Stimilli’s article, which demonstrates the ways in which his scholar-
      ship offers us a means of radically breaking with, and moving beyond, the secular
      conceptual system of modernity. Starting from the premise that Foucault’s examination
      of the Christian pastorate as a genealogy of modern governmentality constitutes, in fact,
      a radical deconstruction – rather than an archaeology – of political theology (cf. Büttgen,
      2007; Senellart, 2014), Stimilli draws our attention to the importance of Foucault’s rejec-
      tion of the linear analysis in favour of the one centring on repetitions and reversals. This
      move, she argues, not only explains Foucault’s apparent omissions of the questions of
      apocalyptics and eschatology in his investigation of the pastoral power, but also, more
      pertinently, allows him to effectively challenge two problems at the centre of the 20th
      century debate on modernity, namely, the interpretation of modern revolutions according
      to the progressivist secular vision of history and the privileged status of ‘sovereignty’ in
      the conceptions of Western power. Accordingly, Stimilli demonstrates how the perspec-
      tive opened up – yet not fully developed – by Foucault’s pastoral could be utilized to
      decipher contemporary forms of apocalyptics in ways that 20th-century debates on polit-
      ical eschatology (most notably, Schmitt, Taubes, Löwith and Kojève) could not. Crucially,
      her contribution underscores the ways in which Foucault’s unique approach towards
      theology allows for the consideration of pastoral power from the perspective of counter-
      conducts, thus placing the emphasis on the ‘revolutionary points of resistance’, autono-
      mous practices of self-constitution, and an inherent ‘potentiality which may deflect what
      seems to have always had but only one direction’, and as such challenging the operation
      of dominant power.                                     
        Complicating prevailing understandings of governmentality, other articles address
      more specifically the ways in which different aspects of the Christian pastoral – beyond
      those initially examined by Foucault – continue to inform domains that might appear, at
      first, largely de-sacralized. Departing from both Foucault’s and Agamben’s economic
      theologies, Bradley recasts the genealogy of the early modern debate on usury from
      Calvin to Bentham, reading it as a theory of pastoral power. He argues that what is, in
      fact, at stake in the classic political, theological and economic scenes under his investiga-
      tion is the pastoral production of a subject whose allegedly inherent self-interest mani-
      fests itself paradigmatically as financial interest. This, Bradley expounds, lends itself to
      retrospective naturalization of the cause of political economy whereat chrematistic pro-
      ductivity is rendered anthropological and the notion of the private, self-interested indi-
      vidual is positioned as the foundation of financial interest. Situating the theory of subject
      as ‘human interest’ within the broader genealogy of the self-interested, entrepreneurial
      and indebted subject from Hirschman, through Foucault, to Lazzarato, he demonstrates
      the importance of addressing the chrematistic gap that persists in the dominant accounts.

       Significantly, Bradley’s discussion, in revealing the allegedly ‘salvific’ economy of
       financial interest to be but ‘a purgatory of infinite and unredeemable debt’, regards the
       production of individuals, businesses and sovereign states qua pastoral subjects of
       ‘human interest’ as one of the crucial aims of the contemporary economy and, in particu-
       lar, of the latest sovereign debt crisis in the Eurozone.
        Rossi illustrates how – in spite of their antithetical objectives and their diverse onto-
       logical and moral premises – contemporary technologies of performative self-enhance-
       ment paradoxically still bear the marks of pastoral practices of self- and world-denial.
       Following Foucault’s analysis of confession, he interprets the pastorate as the moment in
       which, within the history of Western philosophy as a transformative practice (cf. Hadot,
       2002), the care of the self ceased to be practiced as a way to relate to the world and the
       limits that it invariably imposes on the subject. Here, the problem became one of govern-
       ing a subject who was deemed structurally uncapable of facing up to its finitude and
       weaknesses, as codified, in particular, via the themes of demonic influences and tempta-
       tions. Rossi posits that the contemporary legacy of the pastorate, accordingly, does not
       lay primarily in its theological premises, but in a certain technique of government,
       whereby in order to master itself – and what lies beyond itself, i.e. the world and all that
       ‘it makes impossible’ – the subject is required to submit to a system of permanent and
       detailed conduction. Today, and since the advent of capitalist economies, this formation
       of power, he shows, appears to have resurfaced in an array of technologies and tech-
       niques (managerial, financial, actuarial, digital, etc.) devised in particular to guide the
       subject to navigate material and informational scarcity – the primary rendition of ancient
       understandings of finitude and the pivot of virtually all modern economics.
        Finally, Stypinska shows how, behind the alleged neutrality and anonymity of digital
       infrastructures, there still lurks a secularized sacerdotal element that engenders the very
       ‘(a)sociality’ of social media. Examining today’s preoccupation with digital perfor-
       mances, she focuses on their constitutive imperative to ‘conversional exposure’, demon-
       strating it to be but an intensification of the pastoral directive. Specifically, Stypinska
       posits that, despite its fundamental discursive re-framing, digital subjectification rests
       upon the same relation of obedience that marked the pastoral structure of existence, pro-
       ducing ‘beings of command’ (cf. Stypinska, 2020). Extending Foucault’s discussion of
       the pastoral matrix through engagement with Heron’s exposition of liturgical power
       (Heron, 2018), she traces the ways in which the digital model brings the notions of gov-
       erning and being governed into a zone of indistinction, occasioning reciprocal indiffer-
       ence of subjectification and de-subjectification (see also Agamben, 2009). In this, she
       focuses, in particular, on the contemporary problematique of the digital substitution of
       the self with an efficient performance, arguing that it leads to the actualization of the
       paradigm of instrumental use in the form of a despotic relation (cf. Diken, 2021).
       Advocating for self-constitution that defies the digitalized utilitarian pastoral directive,
       Stypinska concludes by calling for the embrace of the imperatives to opacity and useless-
       ness as a foundation for a new technology of the self.
        Although modest in its scope, we hope that this special section will go some way
       towards enhancing the scholarship in the above-outlined areas, further demonstrating the
       importance of engaging with the specificities of the contemporary governmental matrix
       as well as inspiring similarly minded research into the continuing legacies of the Christian
       pastorate.
